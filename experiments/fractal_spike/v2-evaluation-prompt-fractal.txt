**Engine notes specific to fractal:**
- Action IDs: placement is `y * 9 + x`. Action IDs that map to hole cells WILL raise an error. Use `engine.get_legal_actions()` — it already filters holes.
- `engine.topo.active_cells` is a `list[int]` of legal placement targets.
- `engine.topo.distance(a, b)` is graph distance over active cells (precomputed). Manhattan distance would leak influence through holes; the engine uses BFS.
- The controls (`*_control.json`) use the SAME ruleset on torus 8×8 (Pairs A/B) or grid 8×8 (Pair C — torus+connection is broken so grid is the comparator).

Three roles, run sequentially:

=== PHASE 1 — RULE COMPREHENSION ===

Read both candidate JSON files (fractal and control). Confirm the rules are IDENTICAL apart from `topology_type` and `axis_size`. Produce a 3-sentence summary of the shared ruleset.

Flag any degeneracy that affects only one of the two substrates (e.g. "threshold is unreachable on fractal because cell-degree drops near holes").

=== PHASE 2 — STRATEGIC PLAY ===

Play **2 full games per pair**: one on the fractal, one on the control. Same role assignment for both. **Swap seats between the two games** so each player experiences both seats on both substrates.

For each move:
- Explain what you are considering, including any substrate-specific concerns (hole proximity, available active neighbors, route around central 3×3 block, etc.).
- State your chosen move and the reasoning (1–2 sentences).
- Predict the opponent's response.

After each game:
- Did the endgame reach the stated win condition or hit max_turns / double-pass?
- Did either player adopt a strategy that ONLY makes sense on the fractal substrate (e.g. "I deliberately built around the central hole as a wall to deny my opponent route options")?

=== PHASE 3 — STRATEGIC ANALYSIS (joint) ===

Joint discussion comparing fractal vs control:
- **Did the fractal play differently?** Specific moves where the hole pattern changed your decision.
- **Choke points / districts**: are some active cells strategically more valuable on the fractal because they're adjacent to multiple holes (forced corridors)?
- **Influence shadows**: did blocked propagation across holes create exploitable terrain?
- **Path routing** (especially Pair C): does forced detour around the central hole create meaningful tempo asymmetry?
- **Tempo / first-move advantage**: was P1 dominance more, less, or the same on the fractal?

Quantify with at least one comparable metric across both games (e.g. game length, decisive outcome, captured pieces).

=== PHASE 4 — SUBSTRATE CRITIC (MANDATORY) ===

The Substrate Critic argues the fractal adds NOTHING. It must:

(a) Argue the fractal is just "8×8 grid with extra dead cells" — no new strategic concept.
(b) Argue any apparent difference is artifact of the threshold scaling (Pairs A/B keep threshold constant despite changed cell degree, so the fractal is just "harder threshold game").
(c) Test "would an expert in the rectangular game transfer immediately?" — and if yes, the fractal adds nothing.

Player 1 and Player 2 rebut with specific Phase-2 moments where the substrate genuinely changed strategy.

Substrate-novelty score (1–10): "rules play out the same, fractal is decoration" → 2–3; "the substrate created fundamentally new strategic considerations I had to learn from scratch" → 7+.
