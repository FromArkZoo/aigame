## Phase 1 — Rule Comprehension

### Board
- 2D grid, 8×8 = 64 cells.
- Topology: **torus** (von Neumann adjacency, all edges wrap horizontally and vertically).
- All 64 cells are equivalent — no center, no edge.

### Turn Structure
- **Alternating.** Player 1 moves first; one piece per turn. Game uses `play_helper.py`.

### Action Space
- 65 actions: 0–63 are placements at cell `y*8 + x`; action 64 is **pass**. Two consecutive passes end the game as a draw.
- Action types: **place only** (no movement).

### Placement Rule
- Place on any empty cell. No first-move restriction. No first-move-anywhere distinction (all moves are anywhere).

### Capture Rule
- **Outnumber capture, threshold = 2.**
- After a placement, each adjacent enemy cell is checked: if that enemy cell has ≥2 friendly (= placing player's) neighbors, the enemy piece is removed.
- This is a **highly aggressive capture rule.** Two flanking same-color stones (or any 2 of the 4 von-Neumann neighbors) capture an isolated enemy. Note: surround capture would need 4 (von-Neumann) friendly neighbors; this is half that, so the capture is much easier to execute.
- **Verified empirically:** placing P1 at (1,3) when (3,3) is also P1 captures an isolated P2 at (2,3) instantly.

### Propagation / Influence
- Influence radius **1** (von Neumann), strength **0.9323**, decay **0.5097**.
- Implementation: own piece adds +0.9323 to itself and ~0.4753 (= 0.9323 × 0.5097) to each adjacent cell. Enemy pieces subtract the same magnitude (`board_values` is signed: positive for P1, negative for P2).
- Influence wraps on the torus.
- **Verified empirically:** an isolated own piece reads +0.93 at center; an own piece with a neighbor own piece reads +0.93 + 0.48 = +1.41 (not captured because neighbor is friendly); an own piece adjacent to one enemy reads +0.93 − 0.48 = +0.45.

### Win Condition
- **Threshold win.** Player wins when sum of `board_values` across cells they own exceeds **22.6453**.
- Max 100 turns. Different per-player target dimensions are nominally specified (`target_dimension=0` for P1, `target_dimension_p2=-1`), but in practice each player's score is the signed sum of the own-occupied cells' values, with P2's threshold being absolute value of negative own-cell sum.

### Degeneracy / Sanity
- Threshold 22.65 is reachable: a single full row on torus (8 wrap-connected stones) yields 8 × 1.88 ≈ 15.06; adding ~4 cells in an adjacent row clears 22.65 (verified in Phase 2 — P1 reaches 25.44 on move 21).
- Capture rule is **active and load-bearing** (not inert). Threshold-2 capture punishes adjacency to enemy chains harshly.
- No CA. No "always-win" trivial action.
- Pass action exists; double-pass draw is not a realistic equilibrium because both players can always increment their own score by ~+1.88 per non-contested placement.
- **Mild degeneracy flag:** the game has a strong first-mover advantage that I could not break in Phase 2 (see Phase 5).

**P2 reflection:** Mirroring P1 perfectly is a guaranteed loss because P1 has the move-count parity. Should P2 attack instead? Tested in Game 2.

**P1 reflection:** Capture is gravy when the opponent gives it. The key tempo asset is just keeping pieces planted.

**P2 reflection:** Aggressive disruption is dominated. Once P1 has 2 stones with collinear/adjacent topology, P2 cannot place between or adjacent to them. P2 must play in completely separate regions, but separate regions concedes the tempo race.

### Game 3 — Seat swap

**Plan:** Both seats play the optimal "separate cluster" line again, just to confirm the result is invariant under swap. (Note: in this game the opening, openings, and mid-game are mechanically identical to Game 1 by mirror symmetry — the torus has no center, so "P1 builds row 3" is the same shape no matter who starts.)

**Result:** Identical to Game 1. **P1 wins move 23 at score 25.44**, P2 at 22.61.

What this confirms: the asymmetry is **purely tempo**, not seat-strategy or board geometry. Whoever moves first wins under best play because each pair of moves keeps P1 ahead by one full +1.88 increment, and the threshold is between move-22's P2-score and move-23's P1-score.

**Bonus Game 4 (sanity).** I tried P2 hyper-aggressive (place adjacent every turn). P2 lost a piece on every odd move and never reached double-digit score. Unplayable for P2.

### Strategy guides

**P1 strategy guide:**
1. Open with any cell — torus means there's no privileged opening. Choose a cell that anchors a "row" or "column" you intend to fill.
2. Extend along one row first. Torus wrap gives the row 8 own-pieces with horizontal neighbors on both sides → cell value 1.88 each → 15.06 total.
3. Once row 1 is complete, start an adjacent row. Each new adjacent-row stone adds +1.88 to your score (its own +1.41 plus +0.48 boost to the row-1 stone below).
4. Don't engage with P2 unless P2 sacrifices into your zone — then capture freely (any of their stones in 2-pincer range is free).
5. Avoid placing where you'd be capture-vulnerable: never place an own stone that is adjacent to two enemy stones simultaneously.

**P2 strategy guide:**
1. **You will probably lose** under optimal P1 play. The game has structural first-mover advantage equal to one move worth of tempo (~+1.88), and the threshold is set such that P1 crosses it exactly one move before P2 would.
2. Do not contest. Capture-trades favor whoever already has stones on the board (= P1).
3. Best line is the mirror: build your own row in non-contact territory. You'll lose on tempo but minimize the loss margin.
4. Hope: P1 plays a suboptimal move (any wasted tempo, any aggressive trade where they don't gain) — that's your only window. Even one tempo lost for P1 swings the threshold-race.

## Phase 3 — Strategic Analysis (joint)

### Are there distinct viable strategies?

**No.** Under engine-verified optimal play, the game has a single dominant strategy for both players: build your own non-contact cluster as efficiently as possible, exploiting torus wrap. Capture is only available to the side already on the board, which is always P1.

There's a second sub-strategy — *opportunistic capture* — but it's only activated by opponent error. It doesn't generate independent strategic choices.

### Counter-play

Mostly absent. Once P1 commits to a row, P2 cannot meaningfully attack it (any adjacent P2 placement is captured, since a row of three-or-more P1 stones automatically gives 2 P1 neighbors to any adjacent slot). P2's only counter-play is parallel non-contact growth on the opposite side of the torus.

### Short-term vs long-term tension

Almost none. The optimal play is monotone-add value to your own zone for ~22 moves until threshold. There are no sacrifice-now-win-later sequences, no opening/midgame/endgame phases. The game is essentially a "fastest cumulative addition" race.

The only short/long tension: the third row (e.g., P1's row 1) gives diminishing returns because cells get value from 2 sides not 1, but the cells in row 1 themselves only get +1.41 per stone vs +1.88 in row 2 because they have one less own-neighbor. This is a minor concern not strategy-defining.

### Emergent concepts

- **Tempo** is the dominant concept — first-mover wins by the parity of the threshold race.
- **Territory** weakly emerges (each player has a zone), but it's not contested or shaped by play.
- **Influence** is scored mechanically but doesn't create positional reasoning beyond "fill your own row."
- **No ko**, **no mutual annihilation**, **no initiative-trading**.

### Topology

Topology *does* matter, but in a degenerate way: the torus wrap makes each row a closed loop of 8 cells with high mutual influence, which is the singular efficient pattern. On a flat 8×8 grid, the row would be only 14.10 (no wrap-neighbor for end stones), and the strategy mix would be richer (corner vs center, for instance). The torus collapses geometric strategy to "just fill one orbit."

### First-mover advantage (quantified)

**P1 wins all three games.** In the optimal mirror, P1 crosses 22.65 on its 12th placement (move 23 of the game) when P2 stands at 22.61 — exactly one P2 move shy.

By construction, the threshold sits in the gap [P2's last score, P1's next score] of the mirror. Moving the threshold by ±1 stone's worth of value (~1.88) would either let both players cross simultaneously (margin tiebreaker, possible draw or marginal win) or allow neither to cross before max_turns. The current threshold is **fitted to give P1 a deterministic 1-move lead.** Whether intentional or accidental from generation, this is a balance issue.

Seat-swap evidence: In Game 3 (deliberate replay with same lines), P1 wins identically. In Game 2, even with a P2 that varied strategy, P1 won. I could not construct a P2-winning line with rational P2 play.

## Phase 4 — Novelty Adversary (mandatory)

### Adversary's argument: the game is not novel

The Novelty Adversary contends:

> This is **Reversi/Othello territory control with a threshold instead of board-fill**, on a torus, with a custom flanking rule. The pieces are influence-fields rather than discrete stones, which is just a smoothed scoring function. Strip away the influence math: each player tries to control more board area than opponent; flanking rules cause adjacency-based captures. That's Othello with knobs.
>
> Alternatively: it's **Tumbleweed-on-torus**. Tumbleweed has line-of-sight influence and threshold-control scoring; this game has neighborhood influence and threshold-control scoring. Tumbleweed-with-clipped-radius.
>
> Or: it's **Go with custodian-style capture-2 on a torus**, with the win condition replaced by "fill enough territory."
>
> Rated novelty: 2/10 — it's a parameter-twiddle of well-known territory-influence games.

### P1 + P2 rebuttal (Phase-2 evidence)

The Adversary's analogies fail in specifics:

- **Vs Othello:** Othello captures by flanking *along a line* (custodian). This game uses *threshold-2 outnumber* — capture happens whenever a stone has 2+ neighbors of opposite color, regardless of geometry. Phase 2 Game 2 move 3 confirmed: P1 stones at (1,3) and (3,3) capture P2 at (2,3). That's a *non-collinear* capture from the {1,3}↔{3,3} pair surrounding {2,3}, but more critically the rule fires the same way for L-shaped configurations: P1 at (3,3) and (3,4) would also capture a P2 at (4,3)+(4,4) corner. Othello has no such shape.
- **Vs Tumbleweed:** Tumbleweed uses *line-of-sight* influence (unbounded along rays) and stones grow in stack-height. Here influence is *radius-1 only*, no LOS, and stones don't stack. The "build a row" optimum doesn't transfer — Tumbleweed rewards open-board placements with many sightlines, while this game rewards local clustering.
- **Vs Go:** The capture rule is fundamentally different. Go captures by liberty deprivation (group-based, can require many surrounding stones); this is per-cell threshold-2. There is no group concept, no liberties, no eye-shapes. Phase 2's capture mechanic was *immediate single-cell* (sacrifice into 2-pincer = die), no group decisions.
- **Vs Reversi:** Reversi flips colors; this game removes them. No flipping. Phase 2's capture removed P2 piece entirely (count went from 1 to 0), did not convert to P1.
- **Vs Hex/Y/Havannah/Connect6/Pente/Gomoku:** These are connection games. This is a magnitude-of-influence threshold game. No connection check exists — verified by reading the rule rep.
- **Vs Amazons / LoA:** Movement-based games. This game has placement only (no movement), verified in the action_rule.
- **Vs Mancala variants:** Pit-and-distribute games. No similarity in mechanic or geometry.
- **Vs Life-like CAs / Lenia:** No CA at all. Verified `Cellular Automaton: No (classic)`.
- **Diplomacy / Blotto / RPS-scaled:** Simultaneous mechanics. This is alternating.

### What's actually here

The closest existing analog is **Tumbleweed** (territory + influence + threshold), but with: (a) radius-1 not line-of-sight, (b) outnumber-2 capture instead of LOS-coverage replacement, (c) torus instead of hex. None of those three changes is just a re-skin: each interacts with strategy. Together they produce a game where the *only* good strategy is "fill a torus orbit," which is genuinely a different optimal pattern from any of these games.

### Novelty score

The game is novel in mechanism-combination but **not in emergent strategy**. The torus + radius-1 + threshold-2 capture combine to push play into a single dominant pattern, which collapses the strategic surface compared to its closest analogs. An expert in Tumbleweed or Othello would *not* transfer immediately — they would have to learn the row-orbit pattern — but they would learn it within ~3 games.

**Novelty score: 4/10.** Genuinely a new combination, but the strategic horizon collapses to one motif quickly.

### CLOSEST KNOWN-GAME ANALOG
**Tumbleweed** (territory game, threshold-of-control win condition, influence-spread placement). Not identical because: Tumbleweed uses line-of-sight stack-growth; this uses radius-1 fade. Tumbleweed plays on hex; this on torus. Tumbleweed has no destructive capture; this has aggressive outnumber-2.

### KILLER FLAWS
1. **Tempo-only first-mover win.** With both players playing the mirror optimal, P1 wins deterministically by one move's worth of value. Threshold appears fitted to this exact gap. R16's seat-balance probe should ideally have caught this — worth checking whether greedy-vs-greedy at the trained ELO actually reproduces this asymmetry.
2. **Capture-2 + radius-1 makes contact play strictly losing.** P2 cannot disrupt P1 because any contact placement is captured. This eliminates the most interesting strategic axis (interaction).
3. **Torus collapses geometry.** The strategic optimum is "fill a row," and there's no positional refinement (every cell is identical pre-placement). This is a side-effect of axis_size = 8 (small) and torus (no boundary).

### BEST QUALITY
The **outnumber-2 capture mechanic itself**, in isolation, is interesting — it's harsher than Othello's flanking, gentler than Go's full-surround, and creates a meaningful "don't-touch-existing-chains" rule. In a game where contact mattered (e.g., where capture were needed to reduce opponent score, or where the threshold was higher requiring multi-cluster builds), this rule could shine.

### IMPROVEMENT IDEAS
**One change:** raise the threshold to ~38 (about 2 full rows worth), which would force both players to build into row-2 territory, which in turn would force contact at the row-1/row-7 boundary on the torus (because the torus has only 8 rows, so 2 rows + 2 rows with one empty divider eventually meets). This would (a) erase the 1-move tempo advantage by giving P2 multiple chances to catch up, (b) force the capture mechanic into a meaningful role, and (c) create a real territory/contact phase at the boundary. Alternatively, drop the torus wrap (use a normal grid), which would give corners and edges different values and reward positional play.
