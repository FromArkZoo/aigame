# Team 13 Evaluation — Game c6bb58075520 (Run 16, GE rank 3)

## PHASE 1 — RULE COMPREHENSION

**Board:** 8x8 torus (64 cells, edges wrap). Adjacency is **von Neumann** (4-neighbor; no diagonals), confirmed by `topology.py`. Note: `play_helper.py rules` correctly labels this "von Neumann" (no mislabel).

**Turn structure:** **ALTERNATING** — P1, then P2, one place per turn. No simultaneous mechanics. Used `play_helper.py --action play` and direct `engine.step(action)` for verification.

**Action types:** `place` only. Action `id = y*8 + x`. Action 64 = pass. Two consecutive passes = double-pass draw.

**Placement constraints:** Place on any empty cell. First-move-anywhere = True (no centre/corner restrictions).

**Capture:** Outnumber, threshold 2. When a player places a stone, each adjacent enemy is removed iff it has ≥2 friendly (placer-color) von Neumann neighbours after the placement. Capture is checked only on enemies adjacent to the placed cell. Capture changes ownership but **does NOT reset board_values** — pre-capture influence residue persists indefinitely on the cell and on its neighbours.

**Propagation:** Influence, radius 1, strength 0.9322817703212022, decay 0.5097131432079061. Each placed stone deposits `+strength` at the cell itself (P1 sign positive, P2 sign negative) and `+strength * decay = +0.4752` at each radius-1 neighbour. Effects are additive (subsequent placements stack onto the same cell). Values clamped to ±100.

**Win condition:** Threshold. Effective score per player = sum of `board_values[c]` over cells they own (P2 effective uses the negated sum so larger is "more P2"). First player whose effective sum > 22.6453 wins. Same-tick crossing resolved by margin under R16's fix (was P1-bias under R15). Max turns 100; tie-break on max-turns is by piece count (and double-pass is a draw).

**Degenerate-rule check:**
- Capture rule **is alive**: triggered multiple times in our games (cf. Game 1 move 22, Game 2 move 8, Game 3 move 24).
- Threshold **reachable**: hit on turn 23-25 in all three games.
- No CA, so no "dead transition" concern.
- No double-pass draws encountered.
- Influence radius 1 + decay 0.51 keeps every individual placement's contribution local — no exploding sums.
- Marginal degeneracy concern: the game is essentially a **race to fill a 3x3 cluster**, with the threshold reachable in 12-13 stones.

**Empirical "atomic" swings:**
- Stand-alone stone (no friends): +0.93 swing (cell self).
- 1-friend extension on existing cluster: +1.88 swing (1.41 cell + 0.475 buddy boost).
- 2-friend extension (between two existing friends): +2.83 swing.
- Capture move (placement that triggers ≥1 capture): variable, often 1.88-2.83 net swing.

### Strategy guides

**P1 guide:**

2. Build a 3x3 in 9 moves — prefer 2-friend extensions (+2.83) over 1-friend (+1.88).

## PHASE 3 — STRATEGIC ANALYSIS (joint)

**Distinct viable strategies:** Yes, two layers — the **race build** (place tight 3x3) dominates the early game; **capture tactics** activate when extensions saturate. Neither dominates exclusively; the race is the default and tactical breaks are conditional. There's also a **swap-attack opening for P2** that's situational but real.

**Counter-play:** Yes. Capture threats spawn capture-and-recapture sequences (Game 1 moves 20-23 had four capture moves). Defensive walls (filling the 3x3) reduce capture vulnerability since interior stones are uncapturable.

**Short-term vs long-term tension:** Modest. Each move's contribution to the threshold is locally computable, but capture trades inject permanent residue that matters in later evaluations. The window of meaningful tactics is small (~5 moves at endgame).

**Emergent concepts:**
- *Influence territory:* Each cluster's owned-influence is a clear scoring quantity.
- *Tempo / parity:* P1's first-move advantage compounds.
- *Capture trades:* Sacrifice for influence damage.
- *Influence residue:* Captured cells retain prior influence — a unique mechanic absent from Reversi/Go.
- *Threshold race:* Both players visibly progress; no "long opening" obscurity.

**Topology matters?** Modestly. Torus wrap rarely activates because clusters fit comfortably in a 3x3 interior block. Did matter in Game 2 where the corner opening's wrap-neighbours enabled the swap-attack. Otherwise the torus is functionally equivalent to a grid for cluster-builders.

**First-mover advantage:** **Strong.** P1 won 3-0 across the three games. Even with seat swap (Game 3) and aggressive P2 play (Games 2, 3), P1's threshold-crossing parity advantage held. Margins: Game 1 P1+3.27, Game 2 P1+1.88, Game 3 P1+2.85. The 1.88 in Game 2 is the closest, suggesting that swap-attack is the strongest P2 line but still insufficient. Seat-swap was respected (Game 3 had P1 still winning despite our role-reversed mindset).

*Acknowledged seat-identity bias:* Single agent played both seats sequentially. We mitigated by writing each side's reflection before switching, and by genuine swap in Game 3 (different opening for P1). Bias likely understates novelty (we found the "right" lines fast) but accurately characterises the strategic depth.

## PHASE 4 — NOVELTY ADVERSARY (rebutted)

**Adversary's case:**
- (Reversi/Othello) Place-and-flip on a flat board with a numerical winner; the "outnumber capture" rule is a softened flip and "influence" is a smoothed evaluation function. An Othello expert recognises cluster-building and edge tactics immediately.
- (Go) Stones placed, captures by surrounding, threshold win is Go's territory + captures with a fixed cut-off. Influence radius 1 is "graded territory" — Go endgame ko fights map roughly to capture trades here.
- (Conway / Life CA games) Even though this game is `ca_active=False`, the influence propagation produces Life-like spatial gradients.
- (Lattice/influence boardgames) Many indie boardgames use additive influence on a grid (e.g. *Risk*-style pressure tracks, Splendor-adjacent territorial games).
- **Reskin claim:** This is "Influence-Reversi on a torus" — the topology is incidental, the capture rule is Reversi-soft, influence is a smoothed score. Expert transfer is high.

**Player rebuttals (with Phase 2 evidence):**

1. **Influence residue persistence after capture is unique.** In Game 1 (move 20→23) and Game 3 (moves 2→3), captures changed ownership but the previously-deposited influence stayed on the cells. This produces "permanent dents" — playing an attack that gets captured still injects board_values that can never be reversed. Reversi instantly flips and Go instantly clears stones; neither preserves influence. This makes attack-then-be-captured a real strategy, distinct from any referenced game.

2. **The "denial move" in Game 3 move 19 (P1 plays (2,2) for own value -0.02) doesn't transfer to any classical game.** P1 placed a strictly score-negative stone purely to deny P2 a +2.83 cell. In Reversi you only place where you can flip (always positive); in Go you don't place tactical "loss" stones unless for ko fights. The graded 2-friend mechanic (+2.83 vs +1.88 marginal value) creates a distinct optimization landscape.

3. **The 2-step "attack-then-capture" combo (Game 1 moves 20+22) leveraging the outnumber rule's threshold-2 is a specific tactic absent from Reversi/Go.** Reversi captures via flank, not friend-count. Go captures via liberty depletion, which requires complete surrounds. This game's "≥2 friend neighbours of enemy" rule allows partial-surround captures that work even at corners of clusters.

4. **The continuous-threshold win on summed influence is closer to Carcassonne's points race than Othello's stone count.** The threshold creates a clean visible progress bar (P1=22.61 → 24.51 etc.), changing how players evaluate move tempo vs. positional builds. Othello/Go don't have a "race to a number" mid-game.

**Adversary's residual point:** "Cluster-building strategy is identical to Othello's wall-building." Conceded — yes, the macro strategy *is* familiar (a Reversi/Othello player will instinctively know to build solid masses). The novelty is in the *micro-tactics* (residue, denial, 2-step capture), not the macro-strategy.

**Novelty verdict: 4-5/10.** Recognisable family (Reversi/Go-influence hybrid on torus) but with three genuinely novel micro-mechanics (residue, denial, threshold-2 friend-count capture). Not "X on a torus" pure (≥2-3) but well short of "no analog" (≥7).
