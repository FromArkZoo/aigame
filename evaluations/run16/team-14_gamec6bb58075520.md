## PHASE 1 — RULE COMPREHENSION

### Board structure
- 2D **torus**, axis size 8 → 64 cells.
- **Adjacency:** von Neumann (4 face-neighbors), wrapping at all four boundaries.
- Important caveat from the prompt: on torus, **influence propagation wraps**, but **custodian-capture walks do not** (irrelevant here — capture is `outnumber`, not `custodian`).

### Turn structure
- **ALTERNATING** (`turn_structure.turn_type = "alternating"`, `pieces_per_turn = 1`). Played via `play_helper.py --action play`.

### Action types and placement
- **Place only** (no move). Action IDs 0–63 are placements, action 64 is **pass**.
- Placement target: any **empty** cell, anywhere. First move anywhere = True.

### Capture
- Type: **outnumber**, threshold **2**.
- After a placement, each adjacent enemy stone is removed iff that enemy has ≥ 2 friendly (current-player) neighbors. The just-placed stone itself is not subject to self-capture.
- **Super-ko is enforced** (`needs_ko_rule=True` because capture_type ≠ none). Position-repetition moves are silently rolled back and treated as a pass — observed live in Game 2 (move 22 by P2 was rejected as ko).

### Propagation (influence)
- Radius 1, strength 0.9322817703212022, decay 0.5097131432079061.
- Each placement adds `±strength` to the placed cell and `±strength·decay ≈ ±0.475` to each of its 4 von Neumann neighbors (sign + for P1, − for P2). **Effects are persistent** — captures remove ownership but NOT the historical influence delta the captured stone made on its neighbors.
- Per-cell values are clipped to ±100.

### Win condition
- Type: **threshold**, **22.6453**.
- For each player, effective score = sum over all cells they own of `±board_values[c]` (P1 sums positive, P2 sums negation). If a player's effective > 22.65 they win. If both cross on the same tick, R16's margin-based tie-break decides (higher margin wins; equal margins draw).
- max_turns = 100, double-pass = draw (R15 fix).

### Degeneracy check
- **Reachable threshold?** Yes. Single isolated stone gives ≈ 0.93 own-cell value (≈ 24 stones to threshold without clustering). A 2×2 cluster gives ≈ 7.53 from 4 stones (≈ 1.88×/stone density). So a **3.5×3.5-ish** dense block wins. With 64 cells and two players racing, threshold is hit in ~22–28 plies.
- **No dominant first move.** Torus is fully translation-symmetric — every opening cell is identical by symmetry.
- Capture rule is **active and consequential** — both Game 1 and Game 2 saw multiple captures and ko rollbacks.
- No CA rules to worry about (classic mechanics).
- No degenerate forced-win sequence found.

### Strategy guides

**Player 1 strategy guide:**
1. Open in the densest possible build zone — any cell on torus is equivalent, so just pick (3,3).
2. Grow into a 2×2 → 2×3 → 3×3 block as fast as possible. Each "double-attach" placement (a cell with 2 existing friendlies as von Neumann neighbors) is worth ≈ +2.83 effective (vs +1.88 single-attach).
3. **Watch for outnumber-capture threats** at every move. Specifically: any of your stones with ≥ 1 enemy adjacent and ≥ 1 empty cell that the enemy could fill to make it 2 enemy neighbors is in capture danger.
4. If you must break tempo, prefer a defensive own-cell-fill over a poisoning move — the poisoning move trades roughly equally but exposes new pieces.
5. **Coordinate-arithmetic discipline**: action = y·8 + x, NOT x·8 + y. Easy to transpose; always verify against the displayed board.
6. The **ko rule is your friend if you captured most recently** — your opponent often cannot retake.

**Player 2 strategy guide:**
1. **Do not contest** the P1 central cluster directly. Direct adjacency cedes a free capture (P2's first stone touching P1's first stone has 1 P1 friendly already, and any second touching stone triggers capture).
2. Build your own dense block at maximum torus distance from P1.
3. You are exactly one tempo behind. The only tools to claw back: (a) capture threats, (b) double-attaches that P1 missed, (c) poisoning moves that subtract from P1's high-value cells.
4. If a capture-recapture cycle is in play, try to be the player who closes it into a ko-protected position — but recognise that, by parity, this usually goes to P1.
5. Late-game, look for P1 stones with exactly 1 P2 neighbor + 1 empty adjacent cell — those can be captured on the spot.

## PHASE 3 — STRATEGIC ANALYSIS (joint)

**Distinct viable strategies?** Two cleanly:
- "Race": build a dense 2×n/m×n block far from opponent, win by tempo.
- "Contest": capture-trade into the opponent's cluster. Live games and the cross-test show this is **strictly worse for P2** but mostly because P2 starts a tempo down; for P1 contesting is also rare because peaceful build is strictly +EV when P2 plays the corner.

**Counter-play?** Yes, in two flavours:
- **Capture defense:** see the capture pattern early and play to deny the second adjacent enemy. This is concrete and tactical.
- **Poisoning:** placing adjacent to a high-value enemy cluster cell drops their effective score by 0.475 per neighbor without any capture. Persistent because influence does not decay.

**Short vs long term tension?** Mild. Most moves are "+1.88 own + 0 enemy" or "+1.41 own + −0.475 enemy" or "+2.83 own (double-attach)". The +2.83 moves run out fast (only at concave corners of your cluster), so each side spends most moves in single-attach territory. **Capture moves swing 2-5 effective points**, dominating positional play whenever available. The strategic interest is mostly in recognising capture geometry early, not in long-range planning.

**Emergent concepts:**
- **Tempo / parity** is real and decisive in symmetric build races (P1 reaches threshold one ply before P2).
- **Influence permanence** ("poisoned cells") creates a Go-like territory feel — even after a capture, the captured stone's influence lingers, so capturing a stone that has been adjacent to many of yours doesn't fully restore your score.
- **Ko-protection through full enclosure**: a stone whose 4 neighbors are all filled is uncapturable forever. This produces the surprisingly Go-feel of "make eyes" but in an outnumber-capture context.
- **Concave corners of clusters** are the high-EV cells (2-attach), creating a value gradient that pulls play into compact shapes — a clear emergent territory dynamic.

**Topology matters?** **Torus is mildly relevant.** The wrap-around of influence (radius 1) means edge cells of your cluster pull on cells across the board, but at radius 1 with a 8×8 board it's cosmetic — practical play is identical to grid topology. The main quirk is "all openings equivalent by symmetry" which removes the corner-vs-center opening-theory dimension.

**First-mover advantage:** Under greedy-vs-greedy with symmetric far-apart openings, **P1 wins 4/6 of seat-cross variations**. The cases where P2 wins are exactly when P1's chosen opening leaves P2's opening in a strictly better cluster-growth zone (relative geometry). The seat-by-itself effect is +1 tempo (≈ 1 stone, ≈ 0.93 effective) — not overwhelming. This game looks **better-balanced than R15's CA-based attempts**, in the sense that the trained-vs-trained metrics in the database (final winrate 0.5, 1.0, 0.5, 1.0 across 4 seeds) are not all 1.0.

## PHASE 4 — NOVELTY ADVERSARY (mandatory)

**Adversary opens:** "This is just **Reversi (Othello)** with influence smoothing. Like Othello: place a stone, flip/capture adjacent enemies who are 'outflanked'. The radius-1 influence is a gimmick — strip it away and you have place-and-flip on a torus."

**Rebuttal (P1 + P2 jointly):**
- Othello captures by **bracketing in a line** (custodian-style); this game's `outnumber` rule is **counting friendly neighbors of an adjacent enemy stone**. These are very different geometries: in Othello, a single stone can capture a long line at once; here, capture requires a stone to have ≥ 2 friendly neighbors and only that one stone is removed. Game 1 M20 illustrates: a single P2 placement at (3,1) captured exactly P1's (3,2) and not (3,3) or (4,2). In Othello-on-torus the same placement would behave totally differently.
- The win condition is **influence-density** (sum of effective values on owned cells), NOT piece-count. This makes shape and clustering matter intrinsically — Othello's "place-and-flip-many" tactic has no real analog. Game 2 M22 (the ko rollback) is also impossible in Othello.

**Adversary, second attempt:** "Then it's **Go without ko-eyes** — place stones, capture-by-outnumber is just ataris with threshold 2 instead of 0 (i.e. last liberty)."

**Rebuttal:**
- Go's surround-capture removes a **group** when it has zero liberties; here outnumber removes one stone at a time and never propagates. Game 2 illustrates: even when P2's (4,3) was surrounded by 3 P1 stones, it was NOT captured (because outnumber-capture only triggers on placements adjacent to an existing enemy, and no empty cell adjacent to (4,3) remained).
- The threshold-via-influence-sum win condition has **no Go analog** — Go ends by territory + captures, never by a numerical "energy on own cells" total.
- Influence radius-1 propagation is a **continuous-valued field** that the player must manage; Go has no analog.

**Adversary, third attempt:** "Then it's a **Lebesgue-like territory game on a torus** — minor variations of Tumbleweed or some Reversi-Go hybrid."

**Rebuttal:**
- Tumbleweed has direction-of-sight stack-counting; nothing remotely similar here.
- The "outnumber-capture + influence-sum threshold + ko + persistent post-capture influence" combination is genuinely new. The closest known game in spirit is probably **Reversi with a Go-like ko + a continuous-valued territory threshold**, but no single existing game combines these three.
- The **persistent-influence-after-capture** detail is unusual and creates the "poisoned cells" dynamic, which is a clear emergent feature with no clean analog.

**Adversary's final bid:** "An **expert at Reversi or Go would still transfer most of their intuition**: connect, surround, race for territory."

**Rebuttal:** Yes — high-level pattern recognition transfers (cluster-density is good, capture is good, tempo is good). But the specific **tactical primitives** — outnumber capture (not bracketing, not surround), influence-sum threshold (not piece count, not territory), ko on the influence board, persistent post-capture influence — would all need to be relearned and would change concrete-line analysis materially. A Go/Othello expert would be roughly mid-strength for the first 5-10 games.

**Novelty score: 4/10.**
- Not a re-skin: 1 of the 3 novelty axes (capture rule × win condition × persistence) is borrowed cleanly from existing games.
- Not deeply novel: every component (place-only, outnumber, influence, threshold) exists in the GE catalog.
- The **specific combination** is new and produces emergent behavior (poisoned cells, ko-after-influence, full-enclosure ko-immunity) that doesn't transfer 1:1 from any single known game.

## PHASE 5 — VERDICT

**Team ID:** team-14
**Game ID:** c6bb58075520
**Rules Summary:** Alternating place-only on 8×8 torus. Outnumber capture (threshold 2) on adjacent enemies. Each placement adds ±0.93 to its cell and ±0.475 to von-Neumann neighbors (radius 1, decay 0.51). Win when sum-of-influence-on-own-cells > 22.65. Ko enforced.
**Topology:** 2D torus, axis_size 8, von Neumann adjacency, full board observation.
**Turn Structure:** alternating

### SCORES (1-10)

- **Strategic Depth: 6** — Capture-geometry has real tactical content (saw 5+ captures across Games 1+2, including a ko rollback). Influence persistence creates "poisoned cells" that punish tactical mistakes. But the strategic palette is narrow: ~95% of moves are picked from a small menu of {build double-attach, build single-attach, defensive-fill, capture}. Limited long-range planning beyond "race vs cluster shape."
- **Emergent Complexity: 6** — Three real emergent dynamics: (1) poisoned cells from persistent post-capture influence, (2) full-enclosure ko-immunity (a cell with 4 filled neighbors is uncapturable forever), (3) the clear "concave corner = double-attach" value gradient that pulls play into compact shapes. Less rich than Go's emergent concepts, more interesting than Reversi's.
- **Balance: 5** — P1 wins 4/6 greedy seat-cross openings; P1 wins both fully-played reasoning games (Game 1 + Game 2). Database training-runs final-winrates: [0.5, 1.0, 0.5, 1.0] suggest some seat-imbalance. **Tempo advantage is +1 stone ≈ +0.93 effective ≈ ~4% of threshold**, which is meaningful but not crushing. The R16 seat-balance probe (worst-of-three) was specifically introduced to catch this kind of bias and this game still ranked third — suggesting GE found it acceptable.
- **Novelty: 4** — see Phase 4. Combination of (outnumber + influence-threshold + ko + persistent-post-capture-influence) is new in combination, but every component is borrowed.
- **Replayability: 5** — 64 cells × 2 placements/move × max 100 moves gives a large move-tree, BUT torus translation symmetry collapses opening theory to "where do I open relative to opponent?" — only ~3 strategic opening families. Mid-game variety is moderate (race vs contest vs poison). I'd play 3-4 times and feel I've seen most of the game.
- **Overall "Would I play this again?": 5** — Pleasant but not gripping. The capture geometry is satisfying when you spot it; the threshold-race feel is faintly gomoku-like. After a few games the patterns become rote.

### CLOSEST KNOWN-GAME ANALOG
**Reversi (Othello) on a torus + Go-like ko + influence-density win.** Not identical because: capture is outnumber-of-adjacent-enemy (not bracket-flip), and the win is a continuous-valued threshold on a propagated influence field (not piece count).

### KILLER FLAWS
- **First-mover tempo bias** (P1 wins ~67% of greedy openings). Not crushing, but visible.
- **Coordinate-arithmetic foot-gun** (action = y·8+x, easy to transpose). Real-world this caused Game 1 M19 mistake; this is a UX issue but it surfaces because the strategic surface is otherwise fairly thin.
- **Torus + radius-1 propagation is wasted** — wraparound rarely matters on 8×8 with radius 1; the topology choice contributes very little to strategic flavor.

### BEST QUALITY
**Persistent influence after capture creates "poisoned cells".** This is the most genuinely interesting feature: capturing a stone removes the piece but not the historical effect on neighboring values, so a captured cell becomes a permanent dead-zone for its old neighbors. Game 2's M22 ko rollback was a beautiful illustration of how this combines with super-ko: a position seemingly perfect for recapture was forbidden because the influence-modulated board state literally repeated.

### IMPROVEMENT IDEAS
- **Make influence reversible on capture** — when a stone is captured, subtract its historical contributions to neighbors. This would weaken the "poisoned cells" feature but vastly increase the value of capture as a tactical tool, deepening the capture vs build trade-off.
- *Alternatively:* **Reduce the win threshold to ~15** to make captures and tempo more decisive. At 22.65 the game is mostly a build-race with occasional captures; lowering the threshold would force earlier engagement.
- *Alternatively:* **Add diagonal influence at half strength** to give the radius-1 propagation more directional structure on the torus, which would actually exercise the topology choice.
