## Phase 1 — Rule Comprehension

### Board Structure
- 2D torus, axis size 8 → 64 cells.
- Adjacency: von Neumann (4-neighbor: up/down/left/right) with **wraparound** on both axes (every cell has exactly 4 neighbors; no edges or corners).
- Action space: 65 actions = 64 placements (`action_id = y*8 + x`) plus action 64 = pass.

### Turn Structure
- **Alternating.** P1 first, P2 second, single placement per turn. No simultaneous resolution.

### Action Types
- Placement only (`action_types: ["place"]`). No movement.
- Constraint: must place on an empty cell, anywhere on the board. First move is unrestricted.

### Capture
- **Outnumber capture, threshold = 2.** When you place a stone, every adjacent enemy stone is checked: if it has ≥2 friendly-to-the-mover neighbors after the placement, it is removed. With 4-neighbor adjacency, this means an enemy stone dies when sandwiched on 2 of its 4 sides by your stones (including the new one). Removed stones leave their cell empty; their previously-deposited influence remains in the field.
- No CA (game is "classic" — `cellular_automaton: No`).

### Propagation / Influence
- **Influence type, radius 1, strength 0.932, decay 0.510.**
- A placement deposits +0.932 (P1) or −0.932 (P2) on its own cell, plus +/−0.475 (= 0.932 * 0.510) on each of the 4 von-Neumann neighbors. Influence is **additive and persistent**: stacking pieces on neighboring cells linearly accumulates value. Verified empirically:
  - 1 isolated piece → own-cell sum = 0.932 (∑ 1 cell × 0.932).
  - 2 adjacent friendly pieces → own-cell sum = 2.815 (each piece carries 0.932 + 1×0.475 from its neighbor, ×2).
  - 4 friendly pieces in a 2×2 block → own-cell sum ≈ 7.53 (each cell: 0.932 + 2×0.475 = 1.882).
- Propagation wraps around the torus (no boundary effect).

### Win Condition
- **Threshold:** the first player whose total `board_values` summed **only over cells they own** exceeds 22.645 wins. (Confirmed in `_check_threshold` in `engine_v2.py` line 826: it iterates `c if board_owners[c]==player`, so empty-cell influence does NOT count toward the threshold; only stones with their effective propagated value contribute.)
- Tie-break (R16 fix): if both players cross on the same tick, higher margin wins; equal margins → draw.
- Max turns: 100. Otherwise, double-pass ends as draw (R13/R14 fix).

### Threshold reachability (sanity check)
- A 4×4 contiguous friendly block (16 stones) yields ≈ 4 corner cells × (0.932 + 2×0.475) + 8 edge cells × (0.932 + 3×0.475) + 4 interior × (0.932 + 4×0.475) = 4×1.882 + 8×2.357 + 4×2.832 ≈ 37.7. Well above 22.65.
- A 3×3 block (9 stones): ≈ 4 corners × (0.932+2×0.475) + 4 edges × (0.932+3×0.475) + 1 center × (0.932+4×0.475) ≈ 4×1.882 + 4×2.357 + 2.832 ≈ 19.78. Just below threshold.
- Empirically a winning position needs ~10–17 stones with good clustering. Threshold is reachable on a normal-length game (~25-turn average from greedy play).

### Degeneracy Flags
- **No degenerate forced wins detected.** Threshold is not trivially reachable (requires ~10+ clustered stones). Capture rule is live (fires regularly when boundaries collide; we observed ~1–4 captures per game). No CA, so no dead-rule concerns. Torus removes corner/edge specials (intentional). The game does NOT terminate by double-pass in normal play because both players are still incentivized to grow influence.
- **Mild concern:** because torus is fully symmetric, P1's first move location is strategically irrelevant (all 64 cells are equivalent). The game collapses to "where do you build relative to opponent" rather than "where on the board." This is by design but reduces opening-theory depth.

### Strategy Guides

**P1 strategy guide:**
1. Open anywhere — torus symmetry means location is meaningless on move 1. Pick a cell and build from it.
2. Grow in a tight cluster. Each adjacent friendly placement adds ~2.83-3.3 to your effective total (the new stone's own-cell value plus the +0.475 it sends to existing friendlies). Sparse placement adds only 0.932.
3. Stay >5 Manhattan-distance from the opponent's cluster until your own block hits ≥3×3.
4. When your cluster saturates (a 3×3 block gives ~19.8, just under threshold), open a SECOND cluster anywhere far from P2 — DO NOT extend into contact.
5. If contact is forced, ensure each placement of yours puts ≥2 of your stones adjacent to an opposing stone (triggers outnumber capture). Avoid placing where P2 already has 2 neighbors (you'll be captured in their reply).

**P2 strategy guide:**
1. Match P1's quadrant on the opposite side of the torus (Manhattan-distance ~4 in each axis). The torus wrap makes "opposite" mean "as far as possible from contact."
2. Mirror P1's cluster shape one move behind — match every density-extending move with one of your own. You will trail P1 by exactly one piece (~2.83 effective points) throughout.
3. Refuse contact unless you have local 2-friendly-density advantage. P2's only path to win is either (a) a P1 mistake that opens a capture window, or (b) reaching threshold first via a single dense block where P1 has no comparable cluster.
4. Watch for the "P1 opens second cluster" move — it splits P1's tempo. If you can reach threshold before P1's second cluster ripens, you win. This is the typical P2 winning line.

## Phase 3 — Strategic Analysis (joint)

**Are there distinct viable strategies, or does one approach dominate?**
Two distinct viable strategies emerge:
1. **Single-block builder:** dense 3×3 → 4×4 cluster, win on threshold via density.
2. **Two-cluster splitter:** start a second block when the first saturates near the threshold. Faster final-tick crossing because each new stone in a second cluster contributes 0.932 (own-cell) immediately rather than waiting for adjacency.

The greedy heuristic mostly chose (1); strategy (2) is what P1 used in Game 1's late game. Neither dominates — but (2) is slightly better when contact with the opponent is unavoidable.

**Is there meaningful counter-play?** Yes:
- Outnumber capture creates real tactics around contact lines. We saw repeated capture/recapture cycles at the same cell (Game 3 turns 19-28: cells 27, 20, 19 each played multiple times).
- Withholding contact to force the opponent to over-extend is a valid defensive line.
- Choosing whether to engage or split clusters is a real strategic decision.

**Short-term vs long-term tension?** Yes:
- Short-term: a single capture removes ~2.83 enemy effective. That's a big swing.
- Long-term: the threshold race is decided over 20-30 turns of consistent density-building.
- Tension: do you spend a turn capturing one stone (gain ≈2.83 swing) or spend it growing your own block (gain ≈2.83 in own value)? The capture is reversible (opponent can replace) but density is permanent.

**Emergent concepts:**
- **Influence/territory:** the propagation field is essentially a continuous version of Go's territory.
- **Tempo:** the +1-piece-on-board advantage of P1 is the dominant factor, mirroring Go's komi problem.
- **Ko-like contests:** observed in Game 3 (cells 27, 20, 19 cycled).
- **Mutual-annihilation tactics:** outnumber capture creates these around 2×2 contact zones.
- **Initiative:** whoever forces contact on their own terms wins the local exchange.

**Does topology matter?** Partially:
- Torus removes corner/edge specials → no positional opening theory. Move 1 has no information content.
- Wraparound DOES matter for "how far away is the opponent": on a torus, the maximum Manhattan distance is 4+4=8, so building "far" is bounded. This creates a natural pressure to engage by mid-game.
- The "no boundaries" nature equalizes positions and makes the game more about contact dynamics than territorial framing.

**First-mover advantage:**
Quantified above. **P1 wins ~72% of greedy mirrors** (50 games), and ~87% when capture is downweighted. **The R16 seat-swap probe should reflect this** — this game has a real but modest P1 advantage. Unlike R15 same-tick threshold bias, this is a legitimate strategic asymmetry: P1 has +1 piece on the board at every evaluation point.

**Seat-identity bias caveat:** I played both seats with the same agent; results are not from independent observers. The aggregate stats (50-game greedy mirror) are agent-vs-agent and not subject to this bias.

## Phase 4 — Novelty Adversary (mandatory)

**Adversary's case (the game is NOT novel):**

(a) **Compare against canonical games.**
- **Go (Weiqi):** Influence-based threshold + capture-by-flanking is a clear Go cousin. Threshold-on-influence is a quantified version of "controlled territory." Outnumber-2 is the simplest possible custodian/surround relative.
- **Reversi/Othello:** Outnumber capture by flanking has a strong family resemblance to Othello's flip-by-flank. Replace "flip" with "remove" and the mechanic is the same.
- **Hex / Y / Havannah:** No connection condition, so these are not direct analogs.
- **Gomoku / Connect6 / Pente:** No N-in-a-row condition; threshold uses sum-of-influence, not pattern matching. Pente has captures but those are by flanking pairs — different from outnumber.
- **Lines of Action:** Movement-based, not relevant.
- **Tumbleweed:** Influence-based placement game! Tumbleweed places stacks whose height equals the line-of-sight count to friendly pieces. This game uses radius-1 propagation with decay, but the conceptual frame ("stones project influence; threshold wins") is similar.
- **Slither:** Movement-based with capture; not relevant.

(b) **CA literature:** N/A (no CA in this game).

(c) **Simultaneous games:** N/A (alternating).

(d) **Reskin argument:** "This is just Go on a small torus with a hard influence-sum threshold instead of territory counting, with Othello-style outnumber capture." An expert in Go + Othello could pick this up in 10 minutes. The strategic primitives (build dense clusters, avoid being flanked, race to a target) are immediately recognizable.

(e) **"Just <X> in disguise":** Closest analog is **Tumbleweed on a torus**, which already exists as a Mark Steere abstract. The propagation-radius mechanic and threshold win are nearly identical conceptually, though Tumbleweed uses line-of-sight stacking rather than radius-1 decay.

**Adversary's score: 3/10.**

**Player rebuttals:**

P1: The Tumbleweed analogy is the strongest, but breaks because:
1. Tumbleweed has NO capture. Game c6bb58075520 has outnumber capture, which produces ko-like cycles (observed Game 3, repeated plays at cells 19/20/27). Tumbleweed has nothing equivalent.
2. Tumbleweed influence is line-of-sight (long-range, blocked by stones). This game's influence is radius-1 with isotropic decay. The local-only nature means clustering tactics differ qualitatively — in Tumbleweed, a single stone can radiate across half the board; here, every contribution is from immediate neighbors.
3. Tumbleweed wins by majority of stack-controlled cells. This game wins by absolute sum threshold, which means there is a "race to ripeness" dynamic absent from Tumbleweed.

P2: The Othello capture analogy fails too:
1. Othello flips one cell at a time and never destroys pieces. Outnumber-2 here REMOVES enemy stones from the board, leaving empty cells (with residual influence). This is a fundamentally different state-space dynamic.
2. Othello has discrete cell-state (3 colors); this game has continuous board_values. The strategic question "should I build density or capture" is impossible in Othello.

The Go analogy is conceptually closest but mechanically different: Go has surround-capture, no per-move propagation field, and territory is computed at end. Here capture is outnumber-flanking (more like Reversi), influence is computed continuously, and the win is a hard threshold mid-game.

**Verdict:** The game is a **plausible recombination** of Go-style territory + Othello-style outnumber capture + Tumbleweed-style influence threshold, on a torus. None of the three analogs map cleanly. The torus topology is a minor twist (no edges) but the genuinely novel feature is the **explicit numerical threshold on summed propagation values**, which forces a different decision pattern than any of the analogs. An expert in Go would recognize the strategic primitives but would NOT transfer their evaluation function — the threshold race changes everything.

**Final novelty score (post-adversary): 4/10.**

Score reasoning: This is more than "Go on a torus" (which would be 2-3) but is not radically novel (which would be 7+). It's a legitimate small-step recombination of three known mechanics (propagation, outnumber capture, threshold win) with one minor topological twist (torus). The capture-recapture ko-like dynamics observed in Game 3 are emergent and not directly inherited from any one ancestor game.

## Phase 5 — Verdict

**Team ID:** team-16
**Game ID:** c6bb58075520
**Rules Summary:** Alternating placement on an 8×8 torus. Each stone deposits radius-1 isotropic influence (own cell +0.932, neighbors ±0.475). Adjacent enemies are captured if outnumbered by ≥2 friendly neighbors. First player whose summed influence on owned cells exceeds 22.65 wins.
**Topology:** 8×8 torus, 4-neighbor von Neumann with wraparound, 64 placement cells.
**Turn Structure:** Alternating (NOT simultaneous).

### SCORES (1-10)

- **Strategic Depth: 6/10** — Real choices around cluster shape, contact timing, and capture exchanges. The threshold race adds a temporal pressure absent from pure-territory games. Greedy heuristic wins 87% mirror, suggesting the depth ceiling is not high (the best strategy is straightforward), but ko-like recapture cycles and second-cluster splitting offer enough nuance to reward thinking.

- **Emergent Complexity: 6/10** — Influence stacking and outnumber capture interact non-trivially around contact zones, producing genuine recapture cycles (observed Game 3). The game is more complex than its rule list suggests, but most games resolve in <30 turns by simple density race.

- **Balance: 5/10** — P1 wins ~72% of greedy-mirror games (50 seeds: 36-14-0). This is a modest but real first-mover advantage driven by the +1-piece tempo at every threshold check. R16's seat-balance probe should flag this. Skill-vs-skill seat-swap (one game played P1, then again P2 with random opponent) confirmed the smarter player always wins, so the game is responsive to skill — but the seat asymmetry is non-trivial. Not a balance disaster, but not balanced either.

- **Novelty (post-adversary): 4/10** — Recombination of Go (territory/influence), Othello (outnumber-flank capture), Tumbleweed (sum-threshold win) on a torus. No single analog fully captures it, but every primitive is borrowed. Above the "just X on hex" floor, below the "genuinely emergent dynamics" bar.

- **Replayability: 5/10** — On a torus, opening location is meaningless (rotational symmetry), so opening theory is null. Mid/endgame variation comes only from where the second cluster opens and contact-line tactics. Random tiebreak is the only source of replay variance for greedy agents. Decent replayability for casual play, low for tournament-style.

- **Overall "Would I play this again?": 5/10**

### CLOSEST KNOWN-GAME ANALOG
**Tumbleweed (Mark Steere) crossed with Othello-style capture.** Not identical because (a) Tumbleweed has line-of-sight stacking, not radius-1 decay, and (b) Tumbleweed has no capture. The outnumber capture is borrowed from Othello. The threshold-on-sum win is a quantified Go-territory mechanism.

### KILLER FLAWS
- **First-mover advantage:** P1 wins 72-87% of mirror matches. Not catastrophic but should knock balance below average. The R16 worst-of-three probe should detect this.
- **Torus removes opening theory:** All 64 first moves are equivalent by torus rotational symmetry. This kills opening-phase decision content; the first ~3-5 moves are only meaningful relative to the opponent's choices, not the board.
- **Greedy heuristic is near-optimal:** 1-ply greedy beats random 100% and beats greedy mirror via just being P1. The ceiling above greedy isn't very high — this limits long-term mastery growth.

### BEST QUALITY
The **outnumber capture + influence threshold interaction** at contact lines. When clusters meet, you get genuine ko-like recapture cycles (Game 3, cells 19/20/27 each played 2-3 times) where neither player can cleanly dominate the contested zone. This is emergent and not directly inherited from any single ancestor game — it arises specifically from the combination of capture creating empty cells (whose residual influence stays in the field) and threshold pressure forcing immediate re-engagement.

### IMPROVEMENT IDEAS
**Add a komi for P2:** start P2 with +2.0 baseline effective influence (or equivalently, lower the threshold for P2 to 20.5 while keeping P1's at 22.65). This directly compensates for the +1-piece tempo advantage that drives P1's 72% win rate. Would improve balance from 5/10 to ~7/10 with no change to other dynamics.

A secondary improvement would be replacing torus topology with a regular grid + edges. The torus's rotational symmetry kills opening-move information content; a bounded grid would create real positional theory (corners as defensive anchors, edges as half-loss-of-influence). But this is a larger change and the torus might be doing useful work in keeping games short by forcing contact.
