# Genesis Run 16 Human Evaluation — team-15 / game c6bb58075520

## Phase 1 — Rule Comprehension

**Board structure:** 8×8 torus, 64 cells, 2 dimensions, axis_size=8. Each cell has exactly 4 neighbors (von-Neumann adjacency with wraparound on both axes). State dim = 131; num_actions = 65 (64 placements + pass).

**Turn structure:** **ALTERNATING**, 1 piece per turn. P1 moves first. (Verified via `turn_structure.turn_type = "alternating"` in rules and seat-by-seat play with `play_helper.py`.)

**Action types:** Place only (`action_rule.action_types = ["place"]`). No movement actions in legal_actions output. Action ID for placement is `y * 8 + x`. Action 64 = pass.

**Placement constraints:** Target = empty cells; constraint = anywhere; first_move_anywhere = True. So any empty cell is always legal.

**Capture rule (outnumber, threshold=2):** When a player places at cell C, every adjacent enemy stone E is removed if E has ≥2 same-color (placer) neighbors after the placement. With 4-neighbor torus topology, this means a stone adjacent to your placement that already had ≥1 of your neighbors gets captured. The placed stone itself is NOT checked for self-capture, so over-extension into enemy territory is legal but creates next-turn vulnerability. Capture walks DO wrap on torus (uses `topo.get_neighbors`, which is wrapped).

**Cellular automaton:** None (`uses_ca = False`, classic capture/propagation). N/A for non-trivial CA transition count.

**Propagation rule (influence, radius=1):** Each placement adds `strength * decay^distance` to `board_values` for cells within 1 step. Strength = 0.9322817703212022; decay = 0.5097131432079061. So:
- Placed cell itself: +0.932 (or −0.932 for P2; sign reflects placer).
- Each radius-1 neighbor (4 cells): +0.475 (or −0.475).
- Influence is **additive and persistent**: capturing a stone removes its ownership but NOT its accumulated influence on the board (residual influence stays). This is a subtle and load-bearing engine quirk.

**Win condition (threshold sum, 22.645):** A player wins when the sum of `board_values` over their owned cells exceeds 22.645289471714786 (effective). P1's effective is the raw sum (P1 stones get positive influence); P2's effective is the negation (P2 stones get negative influence which is summed and negated). Max_turns = 100; double-pass = draw (R15 fix); same-tick threshold cross resolves by margin (R16 fix).

**Reachability sanity check:**
- Isolated stones: 0.932 each → need ≈24 to reach threshold.
- 2×2 block (4 stones): 7.53 effective.
- 2×3 (6 stones): 12.25.
- 3×3 (9 stones): 19.79.
- 3×4 (12 stones): 27.33 → **wins**.
- A clean 3×3 + 3 more adjacent stones suffices. With ~50% board occupancy by P1 (32 stones) the threshold is easily achievable. Average game length in DB: 22-29 turns, consistent.

**Degenerate-rule flags:** None.
- Placement is well-defined (cannot self-capture; isolated stones legal).
- Outnumber capture activates regularly (saw 7 capture events across our 3 games).
- Threshold is reachable in ~12-15 cluster stones; not unreachable.
- Super-ko is enabled and fires non-trivially: capture-recapture cycles roll back to a pass, which structurally privileges whoever captures first in a contested zone. This is a major strategic feature (and a quirk worth flagging).

## Phase 2 — Strategic Play

### Game 1 (alternating, P1 first)

Both sides played by me sequentially with separated reasoning. P1 anchored at (3,3), P2 at (7,7). Both built mirrored 2×4 clusters (12 vs 12.25 effective at 8-stone parity). On move 19 P1 played (5,3) into capture range; P2 captured on M20 (P1's first tactical error). P1 attempted to recapture on M21 but the engine **rolled it back as a pass via super-ko** — recreating the position from before P2's capture matched a previous hash. Major strategic learning.

After ko-rollback, the contested zone produced several capture trades: P1 captured (5,5) on M25; P2 attempted recapture on M28 which was also super-ko-rolled-back. This let P1 retain the captured stone and build a winning cluster.

**Result:** P1 win at M29, effective 24.02 > 22.65. P1 cluster: 3×4 block at cols 3-5, rows 1-5 (12 stones).

### Game 2 (alternating, P1 first; pure race)

P1 anchored at (4,4); P2 anchored at (0,0). Both built clean 3×3 clusters with no border contact. By M18, both at 19.80 effective with 9 stones each. Then sequential extensions: P1 reached threshold first at M21 with effective 24.51. **No captures occurred; pure threshold race.**

This game cleanly demonstrates first-mover advantage on this game: when both sides race symmetrically, P1 wins by 1 turn (~1 stone of effective).

**Result:** P1 win at M21, P2 at 21.68 effective (one move from winning).

### Game 3 (seat-swap variant: aggressive P2)

I played P2 with an aggressive harassment-and-capture strategy to test whether the first-mover advantage observed in G2 can be eliminated. P1 anchored (3,3); P2 immediately attacked at (2,3), then captured (3,3) on M4, followed by capturing (3,5) on M6.

P1 was down 1 stone with 1.41 vs 0.90 effective by M6 — P2 had successfully exploited the lone-stone-vulnerability of P1's anchor.

P1 recovered via:
- (5,4) capture of (4,4) on M9 (counter-capture; +3.29 swing).
- Building the 2×3 block at cols 4-5, rows 2-4.
- (4,4) "fortress" placement on M17 — a cell with all-occupied neighbors, structurally invulnerable to outnumber capture.
- (5,3) on M31 capturing (6,3), exploiting cluster overlap to push effective from 19.32 → 23.09.

**Result:** P1 win at M31, effective 23.09. P2 at 18.37.

**Seat-swap tally:** P1 won 3/3 games. Even with aggressive P2 play targeting P1's first-stone vulnerability, P1 recovered and won.

### Reflections

**P1 strategy guide:**
1. Open in mid-board (3,3) or (4,4); avoid the lone-stone-attack trap by extending immediately on M3.
2. Build a contiguous 2×N or N×N block. Each placement should be adjacent to ≥1 existing P1 stone (gains +1.88 minimum).
3. Look for 2-friendly-neighbor moves (+2.83) — these are the highest-value building plays.
4. Use "fortress" placements: cells where all 4 neighbors are non-empty after placement are immune to outnumber capture.
5. When attacked, accept that a single-stone capture is sometimes unavoidable, but counter-capture exploits super-ko: the FIRST player to capture in a contested zone keeps the gain.
6. Race tempo: with first-move advantage, you'll cross threshold ~1 move ahead of P2 in symmetric racing.

**P2 strategy guide:**
1. If P1 anchors centrally, attack adjacent immediately to threaten capture of the lone stone.
2. Build mirror clusters at distance, but extend efficient shape (favor compact rectangles over L-shapes — saw ~0.5-1.0 effective penalty for 8-stone L vs 2×4).
3. To eliminate P1 first-mover advantage, you must convert tempo loss into a capture trade you win — which means initiating the FIRST capture in contested space (super-ko prevents P1 recapture).
4. Avoid placing isolated stones; mutual-subtraction harassment is only worth +0.93 swing per move vs +1.88 for clean building.

## Phase 3 — Strategic Analysis (joint)

**Distinct viable strategies:** Yes — three identifiable.
- (A) Race builder: pure +1.88/+2.83 cluster extension, no contact. Wins via tempo when uncontested.
- (B) Aggressive harasser/capturer: contact-builds adjacent to opponent, threatens captures. Punishes lone-stone openings.
- (C) Fortress + counter-capture: stabilizes after early loss via invulnerable cells and super-ko-favored counter-trades.

**Meaningful counter-play:** Yes. (B) beats naive (A) (Game 3 first half showed P2 leading 6.07 vs 4.70 by M12). (C) beats (B) (P1 recovered in Game 3 to win). (A) beats (C) when no captures available (Game 2's pure race).

**Short-term vs long-term tension:** Real. Captures are short-term swings (+1.4 to +3.7) but can leave structural vulnerabilities; building is long-term (+1.88/+2.83 per move) but punishable by capture if unprotected.

**Emergent concepts:**
- **Cluster density**: each adjacency between same-color stones adds +0.95 to your effective (0.475 each direction).
- **Mutual subtraction at borders**: enemy adjacency costs both sides 0.475 — symmetric, but tempo asymmetric.
- **Capture-tempo via super-ko**: the player who captures first in a contested zone wins the trade definitively because position-repetition rolls back the recapture.
- **Fortress cells**: positions with all 4 neighbors non-empty are permanently safe from outnumber capture.
- **Influence persistence after capture**: captured stones leave residual influence on the board (cell value isn't reset). Subtle but mostly irrelevant for top-level play.
- **Threshold race with first-mover lock-in**: in symmetric play, P1's tempo lead = ~1 stone = ~1 turn = decisive.

**Does topology matter:** Modestly. Torus wraparound for influence rarely activates in mid-board play (clusters of size <5 don't reach edges). All cells are degree-4 (no corner/edge variation), which makes the game more uniform than grid topology. The main consequence is that there are no "safe corners" — every cluster is equally vulnerable to attack from all sides.

**First-mover advantage:** **STRONG.** Across our 3 games (including aggressive P2 strategy designed to eliminate first-mover edge), **P1 won 3/3**. Quantitatively:
- Game 2 (pure race): P1 won by 1 turn = +2.83 in final move.
- Game 1 (mid-game capture trades): P1 won despite a tactical error.
- Game 3 (aggressive P2 with early captures): P1 won via fortress + counter-capture, despite being down by 2 stones at one point.

This game does NOT eliminate first-mover advantage. The capture mechanic is symmetric and super-ko favors whoever captures first — which P1 can usually do given their tempo lead. Self-played seat-swap (3 games, all P1 wins) suggests imbalance.

## Phase 4 — Novelty Adversary

The Novelty Adversary argues:

(a) **vs Go**: Same place-on-grid, same capture-by-numbers feel; just substitute liberty rule with "outnumber 2+" and territory with "influence sum." A Go expert transfers concepts immediately: thick walls = high effective influence; lone stones = capture-vulnerable.

(b) **vs Reversi/Othello**: Outnumber capture is a flank-and-remove (vs flip) mechanic. Same "stones change ownership based on neighbor counts" rhythm.

(c) **vs Tumbleweed**: Sight-line based stack-with-influence game with threshold capture. Family resemblance to influence-sum win.

(d) **vs Pente / Connect-N**: Density-of-stones win condition is morally equivalent to N-in-a-row; both reward concentrated placement.

(e) **vs Hex / Y / Havannah**: Connection-based wins. Not directly analogous.

(f) **vs Lines of Action / Amazons**: Movement-based; this is placement-only. Different.

(g) **CA literature**: No CA in this game. Skip.

(h) **Simultaneous catalogue (Diplomacy, Blotto, RPS-N, Gungo)**: Alternating game. Skip.

(i) **"Re-skin of Go"**: Direct claim. Outnumber-2 capture is fundamentally different from liberty capture (a stone with 0 friendly nbrs and 4 enemy nbrs is fine here; in Go it's captured). Threshold-effective-influence is different from territory + captures. Strategic dynamics differ accordingly.

(j) **"Re-skin of Reversi/Othello"**: Othello flips, doesn't remove; flip is line-based, not local-neighbor-count. Different scoring. Different strategic dynamics.

**Phase 2 rebuttal:**
- Game 1 M21: super-ko rolled back P1's recapture as a forced pass. **No mainstream board game has "violation = pass" semantics** — Go has positional super-ko but resolves it by illegality, not pass.
- Game 3 M17: P1's (4,4) "fortress" cell — placed into a position with 2 friendly + 2 enemy neighbors, all 4 occupied. **In Go, this is self-atari (often illegal)**; in Othello, this would flip pieces. Here it is permanently safe due to outnumber-only-checks-on-enemy-placement. This tactical primitive doesn't exist in any direct analog.
- Game 2: pure threshold-sum race with zero captures. No standard game has a numeric-threshold win condition over influence sums.

**Novelty score: 4/10.** Individual mechanics (placement + outnumber + radius-1 influence + threshold) are all individually known. The combination produces some novel tactical primitives (fortress cells, super-ko-locked capture trades, additive influence threshold) not present in any single named ancestor. But the overall feel is "Go with simplified capture and a numeric scoring proxy" — an experienced Go player would adapt within a session or two. Not a deep paradigm shift.

## Phase 5 — VERDICT

Team ID: **team-15**
Game ID: **c6bb58075520**
Rules Summary: Alternating placement on an 8×8 torus; outnumber-2 capture; radius-1 influence with strength 0.93 / decay 0.51; first to effective-influence-sum > 22.65 wins.
Topology: 8×8 torus, 4-neighbor von-Neumann with wraparound.
Turn Structure: alternating.

### SCORES (1-10)

- **Strategic Depth: 6/10** — Multiple viable strategies (race, harass, fortress + counter-capture). Cluster-density math (+1.88/+2.83 per move type) is non-trivial. Super-ko + outnumber capture creates tempo-locked capture trades that reward foresight. But endgame is largely formulaic (cluster extension to threshold).

- **Emergent Complexity: 6/10** — Fortress cells, super-ko-locked capture trades, mutual-subtraction borders, residual-influence-after-capture all emerge from simple rule combinations. Cluster-shape efficiency (2×4 vs 2-arm L) creates real geometric depth. But emergent dynamics are localized; no large-scale phenomena like Go's life-and-death or eyes.

- **Balance: 4/10** — Self-played seat-swap **3/3 P1 wins** including a game where I deliberately played aggressive P2 to break the tempo. The game has clear first-mover advantage. R16's worst-of-three seat-balance probe presumably caught some imbalance in training but the GE rank-3 placement suggests it scored P2-survivable; in human play, P1's tempo + super-ko's capture-first-wins property compound into a structural P1 edge.

- **Novelty (post-adversary): 4/10** — Closest to Go with substituted capture and scoring rules. Fortress cells and super-ko-as-pass are mildly novel tactical primitives. Not a paradigm shift.

- **Replayability: 5/10** — 3 distinct strategies + tactical-tree of capture trades give modest variety. But the threshold race endgame is mechanical, and P1 winning consistently (in our sample) reduces interest. Average game length 22-29 moves is reasonable for a casual game.

- **Overall "Would I play this again?": 5/10** — Pleasant tactical exercise once or twice. Modest strategic depth. Imbalance is the main detractor — beating P1 requires either P1 making a specific tactical error or aggressive P2 luck. Interesting enough to compare to top-10 R16 contemporaries; would not displace them.

### CLOSEST KNOWN-GAME ANALOG
**Go** (with outnumber-2 capture in place of liberty capture, and effective-influence-sum threshold in place of territory + captures). The cluster-density, the influence at distance, and the emphasis on density + adjacency all map directly to Go intuitions. Diverges at: outnumber rule (no self-capture, no eyes), super-ko rolls back to pass (vs Go's outright illegality), no concept of seki / life-and-death / komi.

### KILLER FLAWS
- **First-mover advantage**: 3/3 P1 wins in self-played seat-swap, including aggressive P2 strategy. The R16 worst-of-three seat-balance probe rated this game well enough to land at GE rank 3, but our human evaluation suggests P1 has a structural edge.
- **Capture-recapture super-ko-as-pass quirk**: not a flaw per se but creates non-intuitive engine behavior where attempted recaptures silently become passes. Players need to learn this; can lead to surprise consecutive-pass draws if both players blunder simultaneously.
- **Residual influence persists after capture**: capturing a stone removes its ownership but not its accumulated influence contributions to neighboring cells. This is unintuitive and rarely matters but creates analysis edge cases.

### BEST QUALITY
**Super-ko-favors-first-capturer**: this is the most strategically interesting feature. It transforms what would be a back-and-forth recapture race (boring/cyclic) into a single-shot decision: whoever attacks first in a contested zone keeps the captured stone(s). This drives a tempo-evaluation layer to mid-game that Go's situational super-ko doesn't quite reach. Combined with fortress cells, mid-game tactical play has genuine choice depth.

### IMPROVEMENT IDEAS
**Add a seat-balance compensator**: give P2 a starting "compensation" stone or a small effective-influence head-start (e.g., −1.5 to P1's required threshold, equivalent to ~1 stone of tempo). This would directly counter the first-mover lock-in observed in our 3 games. Alternatively, allow P2's first move to be a **double placement** (place 2 stones on M2), which matches Go's komi/handicap intuition without changing the rules deeply. This single change would likely shift the seat-balance from 3/3 P1 to roughly 50/50.
