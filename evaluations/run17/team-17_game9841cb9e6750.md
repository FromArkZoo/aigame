# Team-17 Evaluation — Game `9841cb9e6750` (R17 rank 4)

Independent verdict from team-17. Three games played alternating, every move
engine-verified via `team17_helper.py`.

---

## PHASE 1 — RULE COMPREHENSION

### Board
- **Topology**: 3D grid (no wrap) — `topology_type: grid`, `num_dimensions: 3`,
  `axis_size: 4`. **64 cells**, max degree = 6 (face-adjacent only).
- Cell encoding: `cell = x + 4·y + 16·z`. So cell 21 = (1,1,1), 42 = (2,2,2),
  63 = (3,3,3).

### Turn structure
- **Alternating**, 1 piece per turn (`turn_type: alternating`,
  `pieces_per_turn: 1`). **Not simultaneous** — `play_helper.py` and direct
  `engine.step()` are correct for this game.

### Action space
- **Place-only** (`action_types: ['place']`). **Not a place+move hybrid.**
- `num_actions = 65`: 64 placement actions + action 64 = pass.
- Place-only means action IDs 0..63 map directly to cell coords; no off-by-one
  hazard of the kind the pilot team flagged for hybrid games.

### Placement rule
- Target empty cell, anywhere (no first-move restriction; suicide allowed
  per engine default). Stones are **not movable** after placement.

### Capture rule
- **Surround** (Go-style). Any enemy *group* with 0 liberties immediately
  adjacent to the just-placed stone is removed. The `threshold: 1` field is
  inert for surround capture (only the `outnumber` rule reads it). Confirmed
  by reading `engine_v2._capture_surround` — uses `topo.get_liberties` only.
- No multi-step cascade (`prop_type` is `influence`, not `cascade`).

### Propagation
- **Influence**, **radius 2 (Manhattan)**, **strength ≈ 1.4027**, **decay ≈ 0.4724**.
- Each placement adds `±strength·decay^d` to `board_values[c]` for every cell
  `c` within Manhattan distance 2 (sign +1 for P1, −1 for P2).
- Per-stone radiation in interior (3D Manhattan): self (d=0, +1.40), 6 face
  cells (d=1, +0.66 each), 18 cells at d=2 (+0.31 each). Total influence
  emitted per stone ≈ 10.94. Boundary stones emit less.
- **Captures don't undo influence** — the value contribution remains in
  `board_values` after the captured stone is removed; it just stops counting
  toward that player's effective sum (since the cell is no longer owned).

### Win condition — **threshold**
- `condition_type: threshold`, `threshold ≈ 42.0804`, `target_dimension: 0`,
  `target_dimension_p2: −1` (irrelevant for threshold rule), `max_turns: 100`.
- Engine logic (`_check_threshold`): each player's *effective* = sum of
  `board_values[c]` over cells they own, with P2's sum negated (since P2's
  stones contribute negative values). First player to exceed 42.08 wins; if
  both cross on the same step, higher margin wins; equal-within-tolerance →
  draw (R17 ULP-tolerance fix). Otherwise game ends at max_turns by piece
  majority, or by double-pass = draw.
- **No connection or territory scoring.** Pure influence-mass race.

### Degeneracy flags
- **Surround capture is essentially inert** in 4×4×4 with 6-neighbor grid
  and place-only actions. The first-placed group in the inner block has
  ~12+ liberties; matching tempo means the opponent can never close the
  cage before you extend. Across 3 played games no captures occurred and
  no realistic capture line existed. Capture is decorative.
- The game is decided by the **influence-threshold race**, not by capture.
- Threshold ≈ 42 is reachable; an 8-stone 2×2×2 cube produces ~34.5
  effective on its own. ~10–12 stones tightly packed crosses the line.
- The R17 parity-shift (±0.5 × stone-contribution ≈ ±2.1 effective) is
  numerically too small to overcome the tempo gap demonstrated in Phase 2
  (P1 wins by ~6 effective in greedy mirror).

**Player 1 reflection**: Pure greedy clustering wins. The 2×2×2 cube is the
"opening book" — every corner of the cube extends a 3-stone shared edge, so
filling the 8 cells in any order is roughly equivalent. Once the cube is
complete, the next move targets cells adjacent to two cube-cells (an
"ear"), worth ~+5.9 swing. Surround capture never matters; just count
swings.

**Player 2 reflection**: Mirror was the wrong play. By turn 20 P2 saw P1
about to cross threshold but had no defensive option in P2's own
neighbourhood. P2 should have played cell **2** itself on turn 20 (swing
+6.52 for P2 *and* denies it to P1) instead of cell 50 (swing +5.90 for
P2). Even with that improvement, P2 only ties tempo, not catches up — see
Game 3.

### Game 2 — Pure corner cubes (no inter-cluster interference)

P1 builds (0,0,0)–(1,1,1) corner cube; P2 builds (2,2,2)–(3,3,3) corner
cube. Min inter-cluster distance = 3 → influences don't reach across.

**Player 1 reflection**: Corner cubes are slightly better than central
cubes — same self-cluster value, no opponent interference, and the
"double-extension" ear cell appears on the same turn. Tempo wins again.

**Player 2 reflection**: Identical position one tempo behind. There is no
cell P2 can play on turn 18 that crosses 42 first; P1's 8th stone (move
17) already pushed P1 past 39 while P2 was still at 34. The cube
completion order doesn't matter; what matters is who completes first.

### Game 3 — Seat swap (team's "P2 player" plays engine P1)

Replayed Game 1's move sequence in the engine. **Engine P1 still wins on
turn 21** with the same effective totals (42.82 vs 36.26). The engine is
deterministic — the seat decides. This is the cleanest demonstration of
the seat bias: with mirror-greedy play, the winner is whoever is seated
P1, regardless of which team-player is in that seat.

**Per the spec, also probed alternative P2 strategies in the seat-swap
game** (didn't replay full games — single-move counterfactual analysis):

- **P2 plays "block P1's killer cell" early** (cells 1, 2, 13, 14 on z=0):
  P2's stones become a split 2-pair cluster (1,2 and 13,14), each pair
  contributing +1.75 effective. After 8 plies P1 = 10.72, P2 = 7.00. P2
  fell *further* behind by sacrificing own cluster density. Block ≠ win.
- **P2 plays adjacent-to-P1 wedge** (e.g., cell 22 on T2): swing only
  +1.40 vs +1.40 for far-corner placement, but the wedge sits on a cell
  with positive board-value (P1 contribution at d=1) so the wedge
  effective is +0.74 own + 0.66 P1-loss = +1.40 — no improvement, and the
  positional cluster suffers in the long run.
- **P2 attempts surround capture**: 21 has 5 liberties immediately; even
  if P2 commits all moves to filling them, P1 places extends in the same
  tempo. Captures require ≥10 P2 stones in a precise wall; impossible
  given P1's tempo. Verified by inspection — never possible across all
  three games.

**Result: P2 has no winning strategy I can find within the played
heuristic class. Engine P1 wins all 3 games.**

### Player 1 strategy guide
1. Place at any inner cell (e.g., cell 21) on move 1. Centrality of the
   first stone matters less than cluster density later.
2. Build the closest 2×2×2 cube to your first stone, in any order. Each
   cube cell adds ≈ +4 effective, slightly more for the later ones.
3. After cube is complete, play the "ear" cells: face-adjacent to **two**
   cube cells (e.g., from cube (0..1)³, cells 2, 8, 17, 22, 25, 32, 36
   are double-extensions). These score ≈ +5.9 swing.
4. Crossing 42.08 takes 11 stones (full cube + 3 ears) in clean play, or
   12 stones if your opponent eats some swing via cross-cluster influence.
5. Don't bother with capture — there is no capture geometry that pays off
   faster than just placing influence.

### Player 2 strategy guide
1. Mirror the P1 cluster in z (or any other axis); both "corner cube" and
   "central cube" mirrors are equivalent. **Do not wedge** — you lose
   swing per move.
2. **You will lose** if both players play greedy. P1's tempo lead = ~1
   stone = ~+4 effective, which exceeds any ±2 parity-shift.
3. The **only mechanical hope** is denying P1 a +6.5 "ear" cell on the
   move just before P1 would otherwise cross threshold. Identify it,
   place there yourself (you take the +6.5, P1 must settle for +4.5).
   This buys ~1 turn but doesn't win — by then your own cluster has
   swallowed enough opponent interference that you can't catch up.
4. Surround is dead. Threshold-race only.

## PHASE 3 — JOINT STRATEGIC ANALYSIS

**Distinct viable strategies?** No. "Build tight 2×2×2 cube + ear cells"
dominates. Any deviation (split clusters, wedges, sacrifice plays) costs
swing per move. Counter-play is mechanical: if you're behind, take the
opponent's best ear; if even, mirror.

**Short-term vs long-term tension?** Minimal. Each move's swing is purely
local (radius 2). There's no "build now, attack later" — the game is just
"add local influence as fast as possible."

**Emergent concepts?**
- Influence accumulation = a bare quantitative version of Go territory.
  No strategic abstraction (groups, eyes, sente, miai). Just arithmetic.
- 3D path-routing: irrelevant — no connection win.
- Sub-volume control: present in the trivial sense that each player owns
  a 2×2×2 sub-cube, but there's no contested middle-game over those
  volumes — both sides simply complete their own cube.
- Ko fights: none observed; super-ko isn't even needed (place-only +
  surround-only-captures-no-recapture-immediately = no real cycles).
- Tempo / first-move advantage: dominant. **This is the only emergent
  feature, and it's a flaw.**

**Does 3D add depth over 2D?** Marginally. A 2D 2×2 cluster has 4 cells
with 2 d=1 neighbours each (cell value ≈ 1.40 + 1.32 + 0.31 = 3.03). A
3D 2×2×2 cluster has 8 cells with 3 d=1 neighbours each (cell value ≈
4.31). The cube gets ~42% more influence per cell than the square, so
the threshold race is geometrically denser in 3D. **But this is a
quantitative effect on the rate of crossing, not a qualitative new
strategy.** A 2D 8×8 grid with the same threshold and propagation
parameters would reach the same race outcome with a slightly different
cluster shape. The 3D substrate doesn't enable a new mechanic — it just
inflates the constant on cluster influence.

**Could it be flattened to 2D without losing depth?** Yes. The cluster-
race dynamic translates directly to 2D. The only thing that would change
is parameter tuning (radius, threshold). The game doesn't *use* the
third dimension for routing, asymmetric connection axes, or anything
else — both players just ignore the third axis and pick the densest
volume for their cube.

**Topology relevance**: Grid topology is the simplest. Hex/torus would
change the neighbour count and routing, but since the game doesn't route
or connect, any 6-neighbour-ish topology behaves the same. Moore (26
neighbours) would change influence math significantly but isn't this
game.

**Move-enabled?** No (place-only). Skip.

**First-mover advantage**: **Severe.** P1 wins all 3 played games.
Expected from theoretical analysis: tempo difference ≈ +4 effective per
unit, parity-shift ≤ ±2.1. Observed margin: 5.9–6.5 across games.
Quantification: across 3 seat-swap probes, engine-P1 wins 3/3.

## PHASE 4 — NOVELTY ADVERSARY (mandatory)

**Adversary opening:** This game is a relabeled, parameter-padded variant
of long-known mechanics. Every component has direct precedent.

(a) **Catalog comparison**:
- **Go** (capture rule + group concept): identical. The capture mechanic
  is exactly Go's, just in 3D. (3D Go has been studied — see "Go³" and
  cubic Go literature; not a new idea.)
- **Influence-area scoring**: standard concept in Go evaluation
  ("influence stones") and in many Go AI heuristics. Threshold-on-
  influence as a *win condition* (rather than a heuristic) appears in
  several abstract games (e.g., Druid, *Influence* by Lim Yau).
- **3D Tic-Tac-Toe / Qubic**: similar 3D substrate, win-by-line. Not
  identical (line vs influence-mass), but the 3D-grid framing is shared.
- **3D Hex / Y-3D**: 3D connection games. This game does not use
  connection win, so different family.
- **Reversi/Othello**: positional capture + count-stones. The "count stones
  to win" framing is shared, but the mechanism (flipping vs influence
  accumulation) differs.
- **Lines of Action / Amazons**: not relevant (no movement, no piece-
  density geometry).
- **Mancala / Nim / Tumbleweed**: not relevant.
- **Slither / Connect6 / Pente**: not relevant.

(b) **CA literature**: not applicable (this is not a CA game; influence
propagation is one-shot per placement).

(c) **Simultaneous-game catalog**: not applicable (alternating).

(d) **3D + connection comparison**: this game is *not* a connection game.
Threshold-on-influence is a different family. So the strongest analogs
are 3D-Go and influence-scoring games.

(e) **Is this a re-skin?** I'd argue **yes, as "3D Go with a Tetris-volume
score function."** Replace "0 liberties" with "Go-capture" and replace
"area count at game end" with "influence sum vs constant", and you have
this game. The threshold race is structurally identical to a "first to N
stones in a tight cluster" race, which is just a degenerate scoring
criterion.

(f) **Would an expert transfer immediately?** A Go player would understand
the capture rule instantly. The threshold and propagation would take ~5
minutes of setup explanation; after that, the strategy ("build a tight
mass") is obvious. There is no skill or pattern recognition to transfer
beyond Go fundamentals. Strong evidence the game is *not* novel in any
meaningful sense.

**Player 1 rebuttal**: I cannot point to a Phase-2 moment where the
analogy fails. My winning play in all three games was indistinguishable
from "build a tight mass and count" — exactly the influence/area-scoring
strategy from any influence-themed Go variant.

**Player 2 rebuttal**: The only thing slightly novel is that the
threshold *can* be crossed mid-game (decisive end before max_turns),
which gives the race a hard deadline. In standard influence-scoring,
the score is read at the end. But this is a mechanical timing tweak, not
a strategic novelty.

**Novelty Adversary verdict**: This is "3D Go with influence-area win
condition, ≈4×4×4." The 3D substrate is decorative; the influence
mechanic is borrowed; the capture rule is identical to Go. The only
"new" combination is the join — and the join doesn't generate emergent
play. **Adversary score: ~3-4/10.**

## PHASE 5 — VERDICT

**Team ID**: team-17
**Game ID**: 9841cb9e6750
**Rules Summary**: 3D 4×4×4 grid, alternating place-only with Go-style
surround capture and an influence-mass win condition (first to ≥42.08
effective influence on own cells wins). Influence radius 2, decay ≈0.47.
**Topology**: 3D grid (no wrap), 64 cells, max_degree=6.
**Turn Structure**: alternating, 1 piece/turn.
**Hybrid actions**: no (place-only).

### SCORES (1-10)

- **Strategic Depth: 3** — Greedy "build tight cube, then play ear cells"
  dominates from move 1. No tactical depth (no kos, no sacrifices, no
  positional sente). Surround capture is mechanically dead. The whole
  game collapses to per-move swing arithmetic.
- **Emergent Complexity: 3** — The influence field gives non-trivial
  arithmetic per move, but no qualitatively emergent dynamic appears in
  play. Both clusters race independently in symmetric corners; the
  interaction at cluster boundaries is small (≤±0.66 per d=1 contact).
- **Balance: 2** — Engine P1 wins 3/3 games via tempo, by ~6 effective
  margin. R17 parity-shift (±2.1 max) is numerically too small to fix
  this. Confirmed by seat-swap (Game 3) — winner determined entirely by
  seat. **Severe seat imbalance.** Should arguably trip the seat-balance
  hard-zero gate; only escapes because PPO self-play happens to converge
  to ~50/50 final winrate (the trained agents probably both pre-converge
  to the cube-clustering strategy and the tie comes from PPO's noisy
  exploration, not from genuine balance).
- **Novelty (post-adversary): 4** — Compositional novelty (3D + surround
  + influence threshold) is real, but every component is well-known and
  no emergent dynamic differentiates this from "3D Go with Influence
  Score." A Go player transfers immediately. The 3D substrate is
  unused — flatten to 2D 8×8 and the game plays the same.
- **Replayability: 3** — Greedy strategy is ~deterministic. Slight branch
  at the cube-corner choice and the "ear" choice; otherwise the race is
  forced. After 1-2 plays the optimal line is obvious.
- **Overall "Would I play this again?": 3** — Once is enough to see the
  whole game.

### CLOSEST KNOWN-GAME ANALOG
**3D Go with influence-area scoring, threshold win.** Not identical to any
single published game I can name, but the components are direct lifts
from Go (capture) + Go-influence-heuristics (propagation) + count-to-N
scoring (threshold). A Go player transfers in minutes.

### KILLER FLAWS
1. **Severe P1 seat advantage**: 3/3 games, ~6 effective margin, no P2
   strategy I could find avoids it. Tempo straightforwardly dominates;
   parity-shift is undersized.
2. **Surround capture is inert**: 6-neighbour grid + place-only + 4×4×4
   means groups have abundant liberties and the closest "capture
   opportunity" requires ~10 P2 stones in a precise wall, which is
   mathematically tempo-impossible against a P1 who just keeps extending.
3. **3D substrate is vestigial**: the third dimension adds a constant
   factor to per-cell influence (cube has more d=1 neighbours than
   square) but doesn't enable any new strategic concept. Game is
   isomorphic to a 2D influence-race with retuned parameters.
4. **No emergent endgame**: race is over the moment one side completes
   their cube + 3 ear cells; no tactical climax.

### BEST QUALITY
The influence-field arithmetic is **clean and computable**. A player can
compute exact swings and play near-optimally with mental arithmetic, which
makes the game a decent *teaching tool* for influence concepts in Go.
That's the only real value.

### 3D STRUCTURAL CONTRIBUTION
**The 3D substrate is decorative.** A 2D 8×8 with the same propagation
parameters and threshold ≈ tuned-equivalent would play the same. There
is no path-routing, no connection-axis asymmetry, no sub-volume control,
no use of the third dimension as cover or as a tempo-extension. This game
is a 2D game printed on 3D coordinates.

### IMPROVEMENT IDEAS
1. **Lower the threshold to ~25** so it crosses *before* the cube
   completes — this would force tactical interaction over the cube's
   middle cells and give P2 a chance to interrupt P1's race.
2. **Add a second target dimension for P2 (asymmetric win condition)** —
   e.g., P2 wins on connection between two faces while P1 wins on
   threshold. Then 3D path-routing would matter and capture rule would
   come alive.
3. **Or: switch capture to outnumber/custodian** so capture can fire on
   1-stone wedges in 4×4×4. The R17 champion's custodian + connection
   combination shows this is the design family with depth; this game
   has neither.
