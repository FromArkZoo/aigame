## Phase 1 — Rule Comprehension

### Board
- **3D moore** topology, axis_size = 4 → 64 cells, max_degree = 26 (interior), 7 (corner), 17 (edge).
- Cell index: `c = x + y·4 + z·16`.
- Action space: 1729 = 64 place + 1 pass + 64 × 26 move.

### Turn structure
- **Alternating**, 1 piece per turn. P1 moves first.

### Actions (place + move hybrid)
- Place: any of 64 cells, only if empty (`target=empty, constraint=anywhere, first_move_anywhere=True` is moot since constraint already permits anywhere).
- Move: a player's stone on `from_cell` may move to an `adjacent_empty` cell (Chebyshev-1 nbr that is empty). Move is encoded as `from_cell × 26 + nbr_idx + 65`.
- Pass = action 64; two consecutive passes → draw.

### Capture: outnumber, threshold 2
- After a placement (or move-into) at cell C, for each adjacent enemy cell N, count *friendly* (current-player) stones in N's neighbourhood. If ≥ 2, N is captured (set empty).
- The placing stone itself counts as one of those friendly neighbours.
- **Practical consequence in moore-3D:** if any one of your stones already neighbours an enemy stone E, simply placing a *second* friendly stone adjacent to E captures E. Very low capture barrier.

### Propagation
- `prop_type = "none"` — strength/decay/radius fields are inert. **No influence field; capture is the only state-altering rule beyond placement.**

### Win condition: connection
- `target_dimension = 1` (P1 connects along **Y**, low-Y face ↔ high-Y face)
- `target_dimension_p2 = 0` (P2 connects along **X**, low-X face ↔ high-X face)
- `max_turns = 100`. At max_turns the engine ends with majority piece-count rule.
- A connection is any chain of friendly stones from face 0 to face 3 along the player's dimension, where consecutive stones are Chebyshev-1 (moore-1) neighbours.

### Degeneracy flags raised in Phase 1
- **Connection threshold is 4 stones** along any axis. With 26-connectivity, a Chebyshev-1 chain of 4 stones (column or 3D diagonal) suffices. The asymmetry cost: P1 places 4 stones by turn 7; P2 by turn 8.
- **Outnumber threshold = 2** is mutually suicidal in moore-3D. Any contact play between P1 and P2 stones tends to result in immediate counter-capture, since the placing stone needs only one prior friendly neighbour adjacent to the target enemy.
- **Move actions are vestigial** in practice: optimal games end in 7–9 turns, so stones never need to relocate.
- **Propagation is inert** (prop_type=none). Strategic-diversity 0.667 / strategic-depth 0.616 are mostly inflated by capture sub-cases that don't occur under optimal play.

## Phase 2 — Strategic Play

I played three games, all engine-verified turn-by-turn via `team14_run17_helper.py`. Game 3 swaps seats. Move actions were never used in any game (every optimal placement is a fresh stone for connection progress; the games end before any stone needs to relocate).

Final: P1 = 5 stones (full column), P2 = 1 stone (3,3,3). P1 wins on turn 9.

**P1 reflection.** The corner column (0,0,0)–(0,3,0) is structurally safe: each stone is a corner/edge of the cube with few neighbours. P2's adjacent harassment was self-defeating — every P2 attacker placed at distance 1 to a P1 stone gives that P2 stone at least one P1 neighbour, and my next move (an extension of the chain) automatically gave it the second P1 neighbour required for capture. Free defensive tempo. Did not surprise me. The game ended exactly at the connection win condition.

**P2 reflection.** Adjacent harassment cannot work in this rule-set. Each attempt cost 1 P2 stone (immediate capture) AND a tempo. After three attempts, P2 had 0 stones while P1 had 4. Should have raced from move 1.

### Game 2 — P2 racing wall along Y=3, Z=0; P1 detours

P2 builds the X-axis chain on the *same z-face* as P1's column, hoping the wall doubles as a capture threat-zone.

| 3 | P1 | 4 | place (0,1,0) | — | Continue column. |
| 4 | P2 | 13 | place (1,3,0) | — | x=1 wall. Threats now: any cell adj to two of P2's wall stones double-captures. |
| 5 | P1 | 8 | place (0,2,0) | — | y=2 stone. |
| 6 | P2 | 14 | place (2,3,0) | — | x=2 wall. P2 needs (3,3,0) and (0,3,0)→already there, so racing. |
| 7 | P1 | 28 | place (0,3,1) | (0,3,0), (1,3,0) | **Detour to y=3 z=1; placement was at Chebyshev-1 of (0,2,0) AND captured 2 P2 wall stones simultaneously (each had 2 P1 neighbours after this placement). P1 wins by Y-connection.** |

Final: P1 = 4 stones forming a Chebyshev-1 chain (0,0,0)→(0,1,0)→(0,2,0)→(0,3,1); P2 = 1 stone (2,3,0). Game ends turn 7.

**P1 reflection.** The wall strategy exposes a critical asymmetry: every cell adjacent to ≥2 P2 wall stones is a *double-capture cell* if P1 places there. So a wall is also an attack-surface for the opponent. Decisive turn-7 win plus a 2-stone capture. The 3D structure mattered here — the detour from (0,3,0) to (0,3,1) used a z-step, which would not exist in 2D — but the detour was forced *because* P2 occupied the natural 2D-equivalent cell. In 2D the same build would've succeeded one turn earlier.

**P2 reflection.** Building close to P1's chain trades P2's chain for double-capture exposure. Walls don't work. Confirmed: contact is suicide.

Final: P1 4 stones (full column), P2 3 stones (still needs (3,3,3) but never reaches turn 8).

**P2 reflection (me).** I considered every alternative I could think of: contact harassment (suicidal — Game 1), wall-blocking the column (loses to detour + double-capture — Game 2), 3D-diagonal racing (vulnerable to the same tempo deficit and to capture by P1's chain), faster connection via fewer stones (impossible — 4 cells is the minimum span on this 4-axis cube). **There is no sub-7-turn win available to P2.** With axis_size = 4 and Chebyshev-1 connectivity, both players need exactly 4 stones to span their axis, and P1 always reaches stone #4 on turn 7 while P2 reaches stone #4 on turn 8. **P2 loses by 1 tempo every time the game is played to optimal.**

### Strategy guides

**P1 strategy guide.** Open with a corner like (0,0,0). Extend along Y in the same low-x, low-z column: (0,1,0)→(0,2,0)→(0,3,0). Win by turn 7 if P2 races on a different face. If P2 occupies a column cell or harasses adjacent, your next chain-extension placement *automatically* captures the offending P2 stone (because that stone already has one P1 neighbour from the previous chain stone, and your extension makes the second). If P2 builds a y=3 wall in your way, detour by one z-step: place at (0,3,1) instead of (0,3,0). Almost always this also captures 2 wall stones in one shot. Never play stones close to each other if P2 has a stone within 1 of either — the same shared-neighbour cell that P1 uses to capture the P2 wall is what P2 uses to capture P1 stones. As P1 with the tempo lead, this is rarely a problem.

**P2 strategy guide.** Resign to losing. Otherwise: race the X-axis on the face *farthest* from where P1 is building. Don't even try to attack P1 — every adjacent placement is captured on P1's next move. The only theoretical win path for P2 is if P1 errs and skips a column move; in 50 random-move trials with random P1, P2-with-column won 46/50, but vs P1-with-column the column race goes 0/50.

## Phase 3 — Strategic Analysis (joint)

### Quantitative race data

I scripted simple-strategy probes through the actual engine:

| Matchup | P1 wins | P2 wins | Draws | Avg length |
|---------|---------|---------|-------|------------|
| P1 column vs random P2 (50 games) | **49** | 1 | 0 | 7.7 |
| Random P1 vs P2 column (50 games) | 4 | **46** | 0 | 10.8 |
| **P1 column vs P2 column** (50 games) | **50** | 0 | 0 | 7.0 |
| Random vs random (100 games) | 53 | 47 | 0 | 38.8 |

The pattern is unambiguous. The "column on a clean axis" strategy is a dominant pure strategy. When both players know it, P1 wins 100%. The fact that random self-play is roughly 50/50 (and that PPO seeds in the training log average ~42% P1 winrate) is *not* evidence of balance — it's evidence that neither random nor PPO finds the simple dominant strategy reliably. **R17's seat-balance gate (≥0.1) failed to catch this because seat balance is measured against trained agents, not optimal play.**

### Distinct viable strategies?

**No.** There is exactly one strategy: build a 4-stone Chebyshev-1 chain along your axis, on a face as far as possible from your opponent. P1 always finishes first.

### Counter-play?

**Effectively none.** Capture-harassment is mutually suicidal in moore-3D with outnumber-threshold-2. Walls invite double-captures. Pre-empting cells of P1's chain forces a 1-step detour that P1 trivially absorbs. P2's only "weapon" is the y=3 wall, which Game 2 showed can be captured in one move.

### Short-term vs long-term tension?

**None.** Games end in 7 turns. There is no long-term to speak of.

### Emergent concepts?

- **Tempo race.** The only emergent concept. Whoever moves first wins.
- **Mutual-annihilation tactics.** Every contact is suicide for the *attacker* in this rule-set. This is unusual but it suppresses, rather than enables, interactive play.
- **No territory, no influence (prop=none), no ko, no sub-volume control.** The game does not develop any spatial concepts beyond "is this cell on my chain?".

### Does the 3D substrate add depth?

**Almost none.** The winning chain is a 1D column on a 2D face. The Y-axis column at (0, *, 0) is identical to a 1×4 vertical line. Z is used only as a "which face am I on?" coordinate to keep me far from P2; that role would be served identically by a 2nd 2D board with two players. **You could play this game on a 2D 4×4 grid with hex topology and recover the same dominant strategy.** The sole 3D-specific moment in my three games was the Game-2 z=1 detour, which only mattered because P2 had taken the obvious 2D cell — not because 3D added strategic options.

The flag in the team-lead notes — "3D shift + custodian-connection rediscovery" — does not apply to this game (which uses outnumber, not custodian). This is **3D moore + outnumber + connection + place+move**, and the 3D-moore part actively *hurts* play by making capture too cheap (26 neighbours = 26 outnumber-trigger sites per stone).

### Does topology matter?

Yes, but in a degenerate way: 26-connectivity makes the connection threshold trivial (4 stones for axis_size=4), and makes outnumber capture asymmetric in favour of the placing player. A grid-3D version would give 6-connectivity, requiring more careful chain-building. A hex-3D would give 8 (depending on definition). Moore-3D is the worst choice for connection here.

### Move actions: used or vestigial?

**Vestigial.** Across 3 games, **0 move actions were played** (0/19 total turns). The action-space is 1729 of which 1664 are move actions; in the games we played, fewer than 100 were ever legal at any one turn (peaking at 24 once, when the board had ~6 stones). Optimal play ends before any stone has reason to relocate. The hybrid action-space exists but contributes nothing to strategy. R17's claim of place+move hybrids being well-represented is structurally correct (they exist and they survived the GE filter) but for *this* particular game the move action is dead weight.

### First-mover advantage

**Severe.** Quantitatively: 100% P1 in column-vs-column (n=50), 92% P1 in column-vs-random, 92% P2 in random-vs-column. The training-time 42% P1 winrate average is misleading — it's a function of the agents not having converged to the dominant strategy.

The seat-swap (Game 3) settled it: with both players using the equivalent racing build, P1 finishes turn 7 and P2 would need turn 8.

### Seat-identity bias caveat

I am one agent playing both seats sequentially. To minimise this, I committed each role's reasoning before switching, and I scripted the data probes against the engine to remove subjective judgement from the dominance test. I find no evidence that my seat-identity bias made P2 weaker than it could have been — in fact, my P2 attempted 5+ different counter-strategies in head and via code probes, none of which beat optimal P1.

## Phase 4 — Novelty Adversary

### Adversary's case

> "This game is **3D Hex with a vestigial capture**. Hex on a 4-cell axis with 26-connectivity is so over-connected that connection becomes trivial — you only need 4 stones in a line. The outnumber-threshold-2 capture rule is mathematically equivalent to a "Reversi-flank" but it never matters under optimal play. The place+move hybrid action set is unused. An expert in any of: 3D Hex, 3D Tic-Tac-Toe (Qubic), Connect-4-3D, would transfer to this game in ~10 seconds and immediately discover the column-race. The fact that the GE-composite-rank-3 R17 game is a well-known under-the-hood structure invalidates R17's headline 3D-shift discovery."

> "Specifically against the R17 hypothesis: Genesis claims '3D + custodian + connection + mobile stones is genuinely novel'. THIS game is **outnumber, not custodian**. The capture rule never engages under optimal play. So there is *no* novel mechanic combination here at all — it's just 3D Hex on moore. The R17 champion is a different game; do not transfer that argument here."

> "Re-skin test: take the column at (0,*,0). Project into 2D as a column on a 4×4 hex board (two free axes, but only one is used). Champion strategy and game length are preserved. **Re-skin: 4-cell-axis Hex.** Novelty score: 2."

### P1 + P2 rebuttal

The adversary is mostly right. Specific Phase-2 evidence supporting the adversary:

1. **Game 3** ended in 7 turns with no captures, no movement, and no use of the 3rd dimension. Pure 2D-flatten-able.
2. **The column dominant strategy** is identical to a 1D race; the moore connectivity provides redundant winning paths but the simplest is a single line.
3. **Move actions never fired** — the place-only sub-game is identical.

Where I push back, partially:

4. **The y=3 wall + detour interaction in Game 2** does have a 3D flavour: the detour from (0,3,0) → (0,3,1) used a z-step and *also* captured two enemy stones. A pure 2D 4×4 hex couldn't reproduce the "detour + double-capture" exactly because there'd be no perpendicular axis for the detour to go into. This is a genuine 3D-only tactical pattern.
5. **The outnumber-2 capture in moore-26 creates a unique attacker-suicide dynamic** that I've not seen documented in 3D Hex literature. It's a degenerate flavour, but it's specific to this rule combo and not transferable from Hex (which has no captures).

These two narrow rebuttals are not enough. Capture happened in only 2 of 19 turns played, and was always a "punish the attacker for approaching" mechanic, not a constructive tool. The headline novel claim — *3D + outnumber + connection* with the engagement actually mattering — is not borne out: under best play, the engagement zone is empty.

**Final novelty score: 3.**

The combination of mechanics is genuinely novel as a description, but functionally collapses to "Hex on a 4-cell axis" with a vestigial capture rule and unused movement.

### Closest known-game analog

**4-cell-axis 3D Hex** (or equivalently, "Connect-4-3D on a 4×4×4 cube with 26-connectivity wins"). Not identical because: (i) outnumber capture exists but matters only marginally (Game 2 detour); (ii) move actions are technically allowed but unused; (iii) Hex literature usually plays 11×11 or larger where the connection problem is genuinely hard.

## Phase 5 — Verdict

**Team ID:** team-14
**Game ID:** `275978116158`
**Rules Summary:** Alternating 4×4×4 moore-3D race game with place+move actions. P1 connects Y-axis, P2 connects X-axis. Outnumber-threshold-2 capture; no propagation.
**Topology:** 3D moore (26-connectivity), axis_size 4, 64 cells.
**Turn Structure:** Alternating, 1 piece per turn.
**Hybrid actions:** **Yes, but vestigial** — 0 move actions used in 3 evaluation games; the action-space is 1729 but optimal games end in 7 turns.

### SCORES (1-10)

- **Strategic Depth: 3** — Single dominant strategy (column on a clean axis). Tempo decides; P1 wins under optimal play 100% of the time. No real branching.
- **Emergent Complexity: 3** — One emergent pattern: "wall + detour + double-capture" (Game 2). Outnumber-2 in moore-26 creates a "punish-the-attacker" suicide dynamic that suppresses interaction rather than creating it. No territory, no influence (prop=none), no ko fights, no use of the 3D structure beyond face-selection.
- **Balance: 2** — Severe first-mover bias under optimal play: column-vs-column 50/0/0 over 50 games, with a 7-turn vs 8-turn deterministic tempo gap (P1 reaches stone #4 first). Random and PPO self-play give a misleading ~50/50 because neither finds the dominant strategy. R17's seat-balance ≥0.1 gate did not catch this because it measures trained-agent winrate, not optimal play.
- **Novelty (post-adversary): 3** — A combinatorially-distinct rule mix (3D moore + outnumber + connection + place+move) that functionally reduces to "4-cell-axis 3D Hex". Capture and movement contribute nothing under optimal play. The Game-2 z-detour is the only mildly 3D-specific tactic.
- **Replayability: 3** — Same opening always optimal. The 50/50 outcome under random play is just noise, not real variety. Once an opening book of "play column" is known, every game is structurally identical.
- **Overall "Would I play this again?": 2** — No. There is exactly one good move at every juncture in the opening, and the game is over by turn 7–9.

### CLOSEST KNOWN-GAME ANALOG
**3D Hex on a 4-cell axis** (or 3D Tic-Tac-Toe variant). Not identical because the outnumber-2 capture occasionally matters (Game-2 detour) and move actions exist (though unused). The structural fact that 4 stones in a Chebyshev-1 chain wins is the same.

### KILLER FLAWS
1. **Connection threshold = 4 stones** under moore-26 connectivity in a 4×4×4 cube. Span is too easy.
2. **Tempo race with no counter-tempo mechanic.** P1 wins 7-vs-8 by structure.
3. **Outnumber-2 in moore-3D is attacker-suicide.** Any contact is a free defensive capture for the chain-builder; suppresses rather than enables interaction.
4. **Move actions vestigial.** 0 moves used in 19 turns of evaluation.
5. **Propagation rule values are filled in but `prop_type=none`** — strength/decay/radius are inert.
6. **2D-flattenable.** The dominant chain lives on a 2D face; the 3rd dimension is "which face is far from the opponent?" — answerable in 1 bit.

### BEST QUALITY
Game 2's "wall + perpendicular-detour + double-capture" pattern is the only moment of genuine play. The placing player can use a *single* placement to (a) extend their chain by one cell, (b) capture two opponent stones, (c) win the game, all from a perpendicular-axis detour. That tactical resonance is the one piece of this game worth keeping.

### 3D STRUCTURAL CONTRIBUTION
**Minimal.** The 3D substrate is used for opponent-avoidance ("build on a face far from theirs"), not for strategy. The Game-2 detour shows one 3D-flavoured tactic; everything else flattens to 2D. The hypothesis "R17 found genuine 3D depth" is *not* supported by this game; this is one of the data points that argues against the R17 narrative for the *outnumber* family of top-10 games. (The custodian + connection champion may be different — that needs its own evaluation.)

### IMPROVEMENT IDEAS

**Increase axis_size to 6 or larger.** 4-cell axis with 26-connectivity is the root cause: the connection threshold is too low. At axis_size=6, a Chebyshev-1 chain needs 6 stones, the column-race tempo gap doubles, and there's room for genuine territorial interplay. (Counter-argument: at 6, the action space and training time blow up. But this is a fitness issue, not a strategy issue — for *human* play depth, 6+ is necessary.)

Alternative: **change topology from moore to 3D-grid (6-connectivity)**. That alone would force longer chains (~7-step diagonals impossible without zigzag) and reduce the outnumber-2 capture barrier. Most R17 top games on grid-3D would inherit this.

A complementary structural fix: **raise outnumber threshold to 3 or 4** so that capture requires meaningful coordination, restoring captures as a constructive tool rather than an attacker-suicide trap.
