- 6-connected neighbours (face-adjacency): −x, +x, −y, +y, −z, +z; boundary neighbours omitted

### Turn Structure
- **Alternating** (turn_type = "alternating", pieces_per_turn = 1)
- max_turns = 86 (long games revert to **piece-majority** at timeout per engine_v2.py `_end_by_max_turns`)
- Two consecutive passes ⇒ **draw** (R15 fix)

### Action Space (449 actions = 64 place + 1 pass + 64×6 move)
- **Place + Move hybrid**, move_constraint = "adjacent_empty"
- Action layout: `[0..63]` = place; `64` = pass; `[65..448]` = move actions encoded as `65 + from_cell·6 + nbr_idx`
- Action space ~26× larger than a place-only 4³ game; **most of it is dead** (see Phase 3 — movement is vestigial)
- Move-action legality requires `nbr_idx < len(neighbors[from_cell])` and target empty (adjacent_empty)

### Capture / Win
- **Custodian capture, threshold 1**: when you play a stone, walk along each of the 6 axis directions; if you collect ≥1 enemy stone(s) and end on a friend, all enemies in that walk flip to your colour. Walk stops at empty or board edge (no wrap; topology = grid).
- **No propagation** (`prop_type = none`)
- **Connection win** with hex-style asymmetric axes:
  - **P1 connects target_dimension = 2 (z-axis)** — a face-adjacent path of P1 stones from the z=0 face to the z=3 face
  - **P2 connects target_dimension_p2 = 0 (x-axis)** — same idea on x
- Connection BFS is restricted to the player's own stones; **the path can wind through any (x,y,z) cells**, not just a flat row/column. This turns out to be central — see Game 1.

### Degeneracy check
- No "play action 0 forever wins" — connection requires ≥4 stones touching both faces
- Capture rule is **active** (custodian threshold=1, both my Phase-2 winning lines hinged on it)
- Win threshold reachable in ~7–13 moves with optimal play
- No CA, no propagation — "classic" game in the engine sense
- Movement constraint is real (engine respects it) but the action set is mostly inert in practice (Phase 3)

### Strategy guides (one-paragraph each)

**P1 strategy guide.** Play a corner column; the corner cell at the face is custodian-immune on its two boundary axes. When P2 blocks a mid-column cell at z=1 or z=2, the *next* P1 move along the column triggers a z-axis bracket that re-captures P2's blocker (e.g. T3 in Game 2). Keep at least one P1 friend on each z-step before P2 can sandwich it. If P2 walls off your z=3 corner, build an L-detour through (x,y±1,3) — but make sure your z=2 staging cells stand far enough apart that P2's horizontal blocks at z=2 cannot themselves form a connected x=0→x=3 chain through some unexpected z-step (Game 1's failure mode).

**P2 strategy guide.** The (y=0,z=k) row for k∈{0,1,2,3} where the row is on a y-boundary is custodian-immune in the y-axis direction; cells at z=0 or z=3 are also immune in z. Pick a row that has at least one corner endpoint at x=0 *and* x=3 face — those endpoints are uncapturable. Build the row, but **stagger the z-coordinate** of interior cells so P1 cannot bracket-capture an aligned colinear segment. Game 3's loss was caused by playing 17, 18 on a single (y=0, z=1) line with P1's 16 already in place, which gave P1 a free triple-bracket on T11. Vary y (or insert a z-step that doesn't align with another P2 stone in the same direction).

## PHASE 3 — STRATEGIC ANALYSIS (joint)

**Distinct viable strategies?** Three are clearly viable:
1. **Corner-column race** with capture-the-block (my Game 2). Wins in ~13 moves.
2. **3D winding path** (P2's Game 1 win). Uses z-steps to bypass blocks.
3. **Bracket trap** (Game 3 P1). Lure opponent into a colinear segment, then close the bracket.

There's no single dominant strategy — the chosen line is shaped by what the opponent commits to in the first 4 moves.

**Counter-play.** Yes, every plan has a counter:
- Corner column → blocked by middle interior cell.
- Capture-the-block → countered by playing block at face-cell (uncapturable corners).
- 3D winding path → countered by bracket-capturing the bridge stones (e.g. P1 captured 53 in Game 2).

**Short-term vs long-term tension.** Strong. Long-term row/column construction is constantly threatened by short-term bracket captures. A single capture move can flip 1–3 stones and restructure the chain (T11 Game 3 captured 2 P2 stones in one move).

**Emergent concepts.**
- **Bracket sequencing**: needing an aligned A–enemy–B configuration *before* playing the trigger.
- **Corner immunity**: 8 corners are fully uncapturable; 24 face-edge cells are immune on one axis; only the 24 interior + boundary-mid cells are fully bracket-vulnerable.
- **Block reflection**: the player blocking the opponent's connection can accidentally build their own — Game 1's z=3 wall (60, 56, 52, 48) gave P2 a connected layer that contributed to P2's win path through 32-33-17-18 once 34 was bracketed.
- **3D path-routing**: connection BFS does not require a flat row, so any face-adjacent zig-zag through (x,y,z) counts. This matters most around z=2 where many bridges exist.

**Does 3D add depth?** Yes, decisively. The 3D connection BFS allows winding routes that are impossible in the corresponding 2D Hex/Reversi mash-up. P2's Game 1 win path (16→17→33→34→35) requires the z-step from (1,0,1) to (1,0,2) — that step is *only available because the board is 3D*. A flattened 4×4 version of the same rules would lose this dimension entirely and become much shallower (likely a forced first-mover win in ≤6 moves).

**Does topology matter?** Of all 3D substrates, grid is the most blocked-by-axis-walls choice. Custodian on torus would wrap, on moore would have many more directions (custodian on moore is engine-disabled), and on hex/sierpinski would be skipped entirely. The grid+custodian combination is the only one that produces clean axis-walks, which is why this combination became the R17 champion family.

**Movement utilisation.** **0 moves used in 3 games.** Movement is vestigial. The ~26× action-space inflation is wasted in practice. Reasoning: placing always adds material, while moving leaves the source cell free for the opponent. The only theoretical use case (escaping a custodian threat) is dominated by placing a defender on the bracket cell instead. **This is a genuine design flaw** — the action_rule says "['move', 'place']" but the move action set is dead weight. Note that this means PPO's action distribution learns to ignore ~93% of action IDs; that is consistent with the modest "trained_vs_random" scores (0.66, 0.74, 0.96 across 3 seeds — the high variance suggests the learner sometimes locked onto useful place actions and sometimes didn't).

**First-mover advantage.** P1 won 2/3 in my evaluation; the loss (Game 1) was due to P1 ignoring P2's 3D winding path, not a structural P2 advantage. With seat-swap (Game 3, where I deliberately played P2 thoughtfully), P1 still won via bracket capture. The 544/544 tournament score in the DB is consistent with a moderate first-mover edge — P1 has the tempo to start the corner column first, and the capture-the-block trick wins in 13 moves with care. **Estimate P1 winrate against equally-strong play: ~65%.**

## PHASE 4 — NOVELTY ADVERSARY (mandatory)

**Adversary (a) — broad catalog comparison.**
- Reversi/Othello: shares custodian capture exactly. Bracket-on-place is identical.
- Hex / Y / 3D Hex: shares the asymmetric-face connection objective.
- Lines of Action / Phutball: shares a place+move action pool, though those games use it for different purposes.
- Qubic / 3D Tic-Tac-Toe: 4×4×4 substrate exactly, but win condition is alignment not connection.
- 3D Hex variants (Trihex, etc) exist with face-connectivity wins.

The adversary's case: "This is just Reversi rules + Hex objective + 3D substrate + a vestigial move action. Each piece is well-known. No expert in any of those games would call this novel."

**Adversary (d) — the specific R17 hypothesis.**
The R17 hypothesis is that "3D + custodian + connection + mobile stones" is genuinely novel. The adversary's rebuttal: a *strong* Reversi player who learns Hex would transfer ~80% of the relevant intuition. Custodian's bracket logic is identical. 3D connection BFS is just the natural extension of 2D Hex BFS. The mobile-stone action is dead, so the "mobility" axis is fictitious. **Strip away the dead movement, and you have 3D Hex with Reversi capture — a 2-component composite, not a deep recombination.**

**Player rebuttals (with Phase-2 evidence).**
- Game 1's P2-winning move (T32: 33 captures 34, exposing the 16→17→33→34→35 path) does *not* exist in 2D Reversi or 2D Hex. The bracket on 34 by 33+35 is a Reversi move that *also* opens a Hex-style connection — the two layers interact. In 2D Hex, capture doesn't exist; in Reversi, connection isn't the win. Their *interaction* is what Game 1 hinged on.
- Game 2's T13 (54 captures 53) is similar: a Reversi capture that completes a Hex connection in the same move.
- These are not transfers from either parent game — a Reversi expert would not see the connection threat, a Hex expert would not see the capture threat. The genuine novelty is in *how often capture and connection coincide* on this specific 3D substrate.
- The 3D substrate matters: the 16→17→33→34→35 path uses a z-step that has no 2D analogue.
- However: **the price for these novel interactions is paid in the design's weakest axis — movement.** The action_rule wastes 384 of 449 actions. R17's claim that "Hybrid place+move action games are well-represented in R17's top 10" is true at the spec level but **functionally vacuous at the play level**.

**Net novelty score.** The interaction of Reversi-capture × Hex-connection × 3D-routing is genuinely emergent (the bracket-as-connection-completer trick has no direct analogue in any single ancestor game). The vestigial-movement and "this could be 2D" complaints are partly answered by Game 1's z-step path. **I score Novelty = 4** — above "skin of a known game" (would be 2–3) but below "genuinely new emergent dynamics" (would be 7+). The mechanic is composable from Reversi+Hex, but the composite produces non-trivial tactical patterns that neither parent game has.

Bracket captures can flip 1–3 stones in a single move (Game 3 T11: 2 stones, Game 2 T3: 1 stone, Game 1 T32: 1 stone with cascading effect on connection). However, 4×4×4 is small; total game length is 11–32 moves with optimal play, so the strategic horizon is shallow compared to e.g. 7×7 Hex. Tactical sharpness is the main contributor; positional depth is moderate.
- **Emergent Complexity: 5** — Three real emergent patterns: (i) capture-the-block (a P2 wall stone gets bracket-captured and stitches P1's chain), (ii) block-reflection (P2's z=3 walls become P2's own connection layer), (iii) 3D winding path through z-steps that couldn't exist in 2D. - **Balance: 5** — P1 wins 2/3 in my evaluation. The single P2 win (Game 1) was due to a P1 oversight, not P2 strategy strength; my seat-swap Game 3 still went to P1 with thoughtful P2 play. Estimated ~65% P1 winrate vs equally strong play. The asymmetric axis assignment (P1=z, P2=x) plus P1's tempo advantage tilts the game toward P1 but the corner-immunity geography is symmetric, so P2 can win — just needs ~5 more moves of correct defence than P1 needs of correct offence.
- **Novelty (post-adversary): 4** — Reversi capture + Hex connection + 3D substrate is composable, and a Reversi×Hex expert transfers most of the intuition. The genuinely new element is the *interaction* — capture-as-connection-bridge and the z-step winding path — but this is decoration on a well-trodden combination, not a fundamentally new mechanic. The vestigial move action does NOT add novelty (the "mobile stones" claim is functionally false in play).
- **Replayability: 4** — Multiple openings exist (which corner column, which corner row), but the optimal P1 line collapses to "corner column + capture-the-block" in ≤13 moves once you find it. The opening tree has maybe 10–20 meaningful first-3-move sequences before the game forks into known patterns. Adequate for casual play, thin for serious analysis.
- **Overall "Would I play this again?": 4** — Once or twice for the novelty of capture-completes-connection, but the ceiling is low: the game ends in 11–13 moves with optimal play, the move action is dead, and the 4³ board is too small for the depth Hex has at 7×7+. Compared to R8's 2D Connection Go at 8×8 (which I have not played but which is described as 8/10), this is a worse game on a different substrate.

### CLOSEST KNOWN-GAME ANALOG
**3D Hex with Reversi bracket-capture on a 4×4×4 face-adjacent grid.** Not identical because: (a) 3D Hex variants typically have no capture mechanic, (b) Reversi has no connection objective, and (c) the rules set the connection axes explicitly perpendicular, which couples the players' geometries in a way 3D Hex does not.

### KILLER FLAWS
- **Movement is fully vestigial.** 384 of 449 actions never used; PPO must learn to ignore most of its action space. This artificially inflates the rule-complexity rating without adding play depth, and is the single largest design issue.
- **4×4×4 is too small.** Optimal play converges in 11–13 moves. The game lacks a positional-strategic phase — it's pure tactics.
- **Block-reflection asymmetry.** A defender's blocks can accidentally complete the defender's own row (Game 1). This is interesting once, but means defensive play is structurally penalised.
- **Corner cells are hard-immune.** Eight corners cannot be captured at all; 24 face-edge cells are immune on at least one axis. This narrows the tactical space — a lot of cells are "safe" without thought.

### BEST QUALITY
**The capture-completes-connection moment.** When you place a single stone that triggers a custodian bracket *and* the bracket capture provides the missing link in your connection chain (Game 2 T13, Game 3 T11), the move feels mechanically integrated in a way that pure-Reversi or pure-Hex moves don't. This is the strongest argument for the game's existence.

### 3D STRUCTURAL CONTRIBUTION
**3D matters here.** Game 1's losing path used a z-step that has no 2D analogue, and the BFS-can-wind property is what makes "block here" calculations harder than in 2D. A flattened 4×4 version would (a) collapse to a few-move forced result and (b) lose the z-step routing that makes the connection BFS interesting. So the 3D substrate is doing real work — but the work it does could probably be done better on 5×5×5 (more room for positional play) without changing any rules.

### IMPROVEMENT IDEAS
**Single-rule change with the largest payoff: drop the move action.** It is unused, inflates the action space 26×, and slows PPO training. The "place" half of the action_rule is the entire game. Alternatively, change `move_constraint` to "adjacent_any" with capture-on-move (enemy stone gets removed when you move onto it). That would give movement a real role — risk a stone to gain capture mobility — and would likely double the strategic depth without bloating action space further.

**Secondary improvement:** axis_size = 5 instead of 4. Adds an interior z=2,3 layer of cells that aren't "one away from a face", giving room for genuine positional play and longer games. The corner-immunity arithmetic stays similar.
