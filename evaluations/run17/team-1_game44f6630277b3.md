## Phase 1 — Rule Comprehension

**Board.** 3-D grid, axis_size = 4 ⇒ 64 cells (cell index `z*16 + y*4 + x`). Topology = `grid` (no wrap, no hex). Each cell has up to 6 axis-aligned neighbours (max_degree = 6). 12 boundary cells per face × 6 faces have reduced neighbour counts.

**Turn structure.** Alternating, 1 piece per turn. P1 moves first. Max_turns = 86.

**Action space.** 449 actions = 64 placement + 1 pass + 64 × 6 move-encoded. Move encoding: `65 + from_cell*6 + nbr_idx`, neighbour slot order is `[-x, +x, -y, +y, -z, +z]` (the engine omits boundary slots, so a corner cell has fewer legal move targets).

**Placement & movement.**
- Placement: any empty cell, anywhere, no first-move restriction.
- Movement: `adjacent_empty` — pick up an own stone and drop it into an empty axis-neighbour cell. Custodian capture **fires on movement landing too** (engine line 463 `_apply_captures(to_cell)`), so movement *can* trigger captures.

**Capture.** Custodian, threshold = 1. On placement (or move-landing), each of 6 axes is scanned outward; a contiguous run of enemy stones bracketed by a friendly stone flips to the placer. Walks **stop at the hard board boundary** (no wrap on grid). Flipped stones change colour, they are not removed.

**Propagation.** `prop_type: none` — the `radius`, `strength`, `decay` fields in the rule blob are inert noise. `engine.board_values` stays at zero for the entire game.

**Win condition.** `connection`, asymmetric:
- **P1 must connect dim 2** ⇒ a contiguous P1-owned chain from any cell with z = 0 to any cell with z = 3 (vertical face-to-face).
- **P2 must connect dim 0** ⇒ a contiguous P2-owned chain from x = 0 to x = 3 (left-right face-to-face).
- Threshold 0.5 is unused — the engine's `topo.connects_faces()` is a pure BFS reachability test.
- Simultaneous completion = draw. Double-pass = draw. Max-turn timeout = piece-count majority.

**Degeneracy check.**
- The `propagation_rule` parameters (radius=3, strength=0.81, decay=0.30) are **dead** — `prop_type=="none"` short-circuits in `_apply_propagation`. Not a degeneracy, just noise in the rule blob.
- Movement actions (~26× the placement actions) are tactically active but, in my games, **never used** by either side in optimal play (see Phase 3).
- 4-stone column or row is enough to win, with no length penalty — opening play is highly forced.

### Strategy guides

**P1 (offence):** Open at an *interior* axis-2 column ((1,1,*), (1,2,*), (2,1,*), (2,2,*)). **Do not play z=1 then z=2 in the same column** — that line is the sandwich-trap setup; play z=0 first or skip a square (e.g. z=0 then z=2). Spread laterally at z=2 to multiply z=3 entries; accept that P2 must block each, then look for a custodian capture along x or y at z=2 to create a multi-cell bridge in one move. Never use the move action — placement is more efficient.

**P2 (defence + offence):** Pure blocking loses (Games 1–2). Best play is the sandwich trap: when P1 plays z=1 in an axis-2 column, place z=0 of that column; when P1 plays z=2, place z=3 to flip. Convert the captured (z=1, z=2) stones into a y=1, z=1 cross-volume that completes an x-row. The trap and a row-build are not competing strategies — the trap *gives you* the row.

## Phase 3 — Joint Strategic Analysis

**Distinct viable strategies?** Yes, two:
1. **Column-spread + custodian-bridge** (P1's playbook, Games 1 & 2). The 3-in-column threat forces P2 to block at z=3; lateral z=2 build then sets up a single-move custodian flip that completes the chain.
2. **Sandwich trap + opportunistic row** (P2's playbook, Game 3). Pre-place at the column's two endpoints, harvest the middle. The flipped stones are usable in P2's own x-row.

**Counter-play.** Real. Each strategy has a known counter (don't play z=1 before z=0; don't sandwich-trap when P2 has an open custodian counter at the other axis). Move-by-move tension is higher than I expected at the start.

**Short-term vs long-term.** The board is so small (64 cells, ~15-move games) that "long term" is 5–8 plies. Most decisions are tactical (what does the next custodian flip do?) rather than strategic.

**Emergent concepts observed.**
- **Sandwich trap** as an emergent capture pattern (a planned 2-stone harvest, not just incidental).
- **Custodian bridge** — the placer-flips-and-completes-chain combo. This is the same primitive R8's "Connection Go" used (custodian + connection), now in 3D.
- **Axis-conflict.** P1 wants z-chains, P2 wants x-chains. They share the y axis as "neutral" build space. Each axis-2 column blocks both sides differently, which is structurally interesting.
- **Double-threat at z=2.** P1 with multiple z=2 stones forces P2 into a single-block-can't-cover-both situation. (Games 1 and 2 both ended on a double-threat.)
- I did **not** observe ko-like fights, mutual annihilation, sub-volume territorial control, or non-trivial influence patterns — those are absent because propagation is off.

**Does 3D matter?** *Partially.* The 3D substrate matters in two ways: (a) the asymmetric axis assignment (P1 z, P2 x) lets each side occupy z=2 cells without immediately blocking the other's connection axis — z=2 is "shared bridging space" — which adds 1 layer of indirection over a 2-D Hex-style game; (b) max_degree=6 means each interior z=2 cell has 6 paths to z=3, multiplying the double-threat opportunities. **Could it be flattened?** Yes, a 4×4 Hex-with-custodian on a 2-D grid would capture most of the same dynamics — the "extra layer" is mostly bridging surface area, not a genuinely 3-dimensional plan. The 3D substrate adds depth modestly, not transformatively.

**Does topology matter?** Only between grid and torus / hex. Custodian on grid (this game) is meaningful; on hex/moore it's skipped. So this game's specific identity is grid-locked.

**Move-action usage.** **0 / 0 / 0 across all 3 games.** 384 of 449 actions (~85%) are movement encodings; not one was selected. Movement is **vestigial** — placement is strictly better because (a) move leaves origin empty (loses board control), (b) move can't help with the most pressing tempo issue (you still spend one ply for one stone-cell), (c) custodian fires on placement landing the same way as on a move landing, with no compensating advantage to the move route. This wastes ~85% of the action space and I'd expect PPO training was significantly slowed by the dead encoding.

**First-mover advantage / seat balance.** From my 3 games: P1 wins 2/3, P2 wins 1/3. The training data show 50/50 final-winrate after seat-swap, ELO 2627 with 544/544 tournament wins. My experience aligns with the engine's view that the game is roughly balanced *if* P2 plays the trap; pure-block P2 loses every time. The ELO-tournament headline ("won 544 of 544") almost certainly reflects this game beating the rest of R17's population, not within-pair seat balance — those balance numbers come from training and are mid-table, not exceptional.

## Phase 4 — Novelty Adversary (mandatory)

**Adversary case.** This game is a re-skin of well-known mechanics. Argument:

(a) **Connection wins** are Hex / Y / Havannah / TwixT / Slither. The asymmetric P1/P2 axis assignment is exactly Hex's "P1 connects black sides, P2 connects white sides".
(b) **Custodian capture** is Reversi/Othello (sandwich-and-flip on a single axis), or the historical Tafl family (sandwich-and-remove). Specifically the *flip* (not remove) makes this Othello-family.
(c) **The combination "custodian + connection"** already exists in R8's 2-D "Connection Go" (this codebase's prior 8/10 winner) — *which the prompt itself flags*. R17 explicitly rediscovered it. This is *literally a known game in this very project*, not novel within Genesis history.
(d) **3-D Hex** has been studied (Hales–Jewett, n-D tic-tac-toe, Qubic, 3-D Connect, 3-D Hex theoretical literature). Adding custodian to 3-D Hex is a small step.
(e) **Place + move hybrid** evokes Amazons (place a queen by moving and shooting) or Lines of Action (movement-only on a grid). Here the "move" is essentially trivialised to place-equivalent and goes unused.
(f) **Expert-transfer test.** A Hex player + Othello player working together would understand the game inside 5 minutes. The "novel" parts are (i) 3-D, (ii) custodian on a connection game on a grid where capture-walks don't wrap. Both are minor twists.

**Closest known-game analogue:** **3-D Connection Go with Othello-style custodian flips** — equivalently, "Reversi-Hex on a 4×4×4 cube". The R8 game `9d33eee27c66` is closer still — the difference is "2-D ⇒ 3-D" and "no-move ⇒ vestigial-move".

**Player rebuttal (P1 + P2).**
- The custodian-bridge tactic in Game 1 / Game 2 is **not** in pure Hex (no captures) and **not** in pure Othello (no connection). The combination produces a single-move chain-completing flip that neither parent enables.
- The **sandwich trap** in Game 3 (deliberately seeding column endpoints to harvest 2 enemy stones at once on the connection axis) is an attack pattern that doesn't transfer cleanly from any single ancestor.
- The **asymmetric axis assignment in 3-D** does add something: it makes z=2 a "bridging space" for both players that is neither side's connection axis — a structure 2-D Hex doesn't have.
- Movement being unused **subtracts** novelty rather than adding it. If we score this game, we should not give credit for the place+move headline since it never functioned.

**Novelty score (post-adversary):** **5/10.** Above pure re-skin (2–3) because the custodian-bridge and sandwich-trap interactions on a 3-D connection board do generate move patterns no single ancestor produces. Below 7 because (i) the same family was already published as R8's `9d33eee27c66` 8/10 game in this very project, (ii) movement is vestigial, (iii) the 3-D substrate's contribution is incremental over 2-D Hex+custodian. **The R17 hypothesis that "3-D + custodian + connection + mobile stones" is genuinely novel survives only after deleting "+ mobile stones"** — the 3-arg combination is mildly novel, the 4-arg headline isn't, because the 4th arg never fires.

## Phase 5 — Verdict

**Team ID:** team-1
**Game ID:** 44f6630277b3
**Rules Summary:** 3-D 4×4×4 grid; alternating placement (or unused move-to-adjacent-empty); Othello-style custodian flips along the 6 axis-aligned directions, no boundary wrap; first to a contiguous chain across the cube wins (P1 along z, P2 along x). Movement is dead code.
**Topology:** 3-D grid, axis_size 4, max_degree 6. No torus / no hex.
**Turn Structure:** alternating
**Hybrid actions:** yes, **vestigial** — 0 of 46 plies in 3 games used a move action; placement strictly dominates.

### Scores (1–10)

- **Strategic Depth: 5** — Custodian-bridge (Games 1–2 win-completes) and sandwich-trap (Game 3 win-completes) are real tactical patterns and force placement-order discipline; but the 4-stone winning chain on a 64-cell board is short, games end in ~15 plies, and there are no medium-term concepts (no territory, no influence — propagation is off; no ko fights — these are connection-wins; no long-range planning — board is too small).
- **Emergent Complexity: 5** — Sandwich trap and custodian bridge are emergent and non-obvious. Axis-asymmetry creates interesting move-equity patterns at z=2. But the rule-set is small (custodian + connection) and the emergent vocabulary is correspondingly small.
- **Balance: 6** — Better than naive tempo would predict. Across my 3 games P1 won 2, P2 won 1 (the trap-and-row game). The training-time seat-swapped winrate is reportedly 50% — consistent with my finding that the trap is a viable P2 win once seen. **Not** GE 0.3773 / ELO 2627 levels of dominance over its peers — the high ELO reflects relative strength against R17's other games, which collapse faster.
- **Novelty (post-adversary): 5** — see Phase 4. Above re-skin (the custodian-bridge/sandwich pair is a real combination), below "genuinely new" (R8 already produced this family in 2-D; the 3-D + place+move headline subtracts more than the 3-D alone adds).
- **Replayability: 4** — Once the sandwich trap and the custodian bridge are known, openings collapse to a small set. The 4³ board doesn't admit meaningful variation, and the move action that *could* have provided variation is unused.
- **Overall "Would I play this again?": 5** — Once: yes, to feel the trap. Repeatedly: no — the depth ceiling is hit fast.

### CLOSEST KNOWN-GAME ANALOG
3-D Connection Go with Othello-style custodian flipping. Equivalent to "Reversi-Hex on a 4×4×4 cube". Inside this project, the closer ancestor is R8's `9d33eee27c66` (Connection Go, 2-D, custodian) — this game is essentially that recipe rediscovered in 3-D with a place+move action layout that wasn't actually used.

### KILLER FLAWS
- **Move action is vestigial** (0 / 46 plies). 384 of 449 (~85%) of the action space is dead. This is both a design flaw (the game doesn't need it) and an evolution-engine flaw (the action space inflates training cost without contributing).
- **Inert propagation rule blob.** `prop_type=none` makes radius/strength/decay irrelevant noise in the rules — minor but means rule_complexity 17 overstates true rule count.
- **Small board ⇒ short games.** 4³ with a 4-stone connection win means tactical decisions dominate; there is no room for long-form strategic planning.
- **Sandwich trap dictates a forced opening order for P1.** Don't play z=1 before z=0 in any column. Once known, this constraint reduces opening variety.

### BEST QUALITY
The **custodian-bridge as a chain-completer** is the game's crown jewel. A single placement flipping 1–2 enemy stones can complete an otherwise-broken connection in one ply. This is the same R8 mechanic and it remains genuinely interesting; it's the reason the game scores above 4 on depth/emergence.

### 3D STRUCTURAL CONTRIBUTION
**Modest, not transformative.** The 3-D substrate gives (a) an asymmetric axis assignment that makes z=2 shared bridging space, (b) max_degree=6 so each interior cell has more bridging routes. But the same game on a 4×4 2-D grid with the same custodian + connection rules would preserve the bridge-and-trap dynamics — the 3-D layer is more of a multiplier on tactical surface area than a new strategic dimension. The "every prior champion was 2-D, now top-10 is 100% 3-D" headline is real but, on this game, the basin is being pulled by the +5% non-grid novelty multiplier and connectivity, not by genuine 3-D-only strategy. **Could plausibly be flattened to 2-D without losing more than 1 point of depth.**

### IMPROVEMENT IDEAS
**Single best change: remove the move action entirely** — it's unused and bloats the action space ~26×, slowing PPO training without contributing to play. (Equivalently: gate move actions behind a constraint that makes them strictly different from placement, e.g. moves can capture from cells where placement can't.)

Secondary improvements:
- Larger axis (5 or 6) to extend medium-term planning.
- Penalise too-short games in fitness so the engine doesn't reward quick-resolution columns.
- Re-test with `propagation_rule.prop_type` actually set — the noise fields look intentional but are dead.
