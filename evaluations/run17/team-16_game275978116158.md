## Phase 1 — Rule Comprehension

**Topology / board.** 3D `moore` topology, axis size 4 → 64 cells. Max degree = 26 for interior cells, 17 on faces, 11 on edges, 7 at corners. Cell index = `z·16 + y·4 + x`. (Note: `play_helper rules` may mislabel topology — verified against `topology.py` and `inspect_game.py`.)

**Turn structure.** Alternating, 1 piece per turn, max 100 turns.

**Action space.** 1729 actions = 64 placements + 1 pass + 64·26 = 1664 moves. Move = relocate one of your own stones to an adjacent **empty** cell (`move_constraint = adjacent_empty`). Place adds a stone; move does not. Pass is action 64; two consecutive passes draw.

**Capture.** `outnumber` with `threshold = 2`. After a placement (or move arrival) at C, every enemy stone N adjacent to C is removed iff the placer has ≥2 friendly stones inside N's 26-cell moore neighborhood (the newly arrived stone counts as one of the friends). Captures are **single-shot from C** — no chain reaction.

**Propagation.** `prop_type = "none"`. The `radius`/`strength`/`decay` fields in the rule blob are dead values: `_apply_propagation` short-circuits, `board_values` stays at 0. Pure owner game.

**Win condition.** `connection`, threshold 0.5 (vestigial), `target_dimension = 1` (P1 connects faces along **y**, i.e. y=0 ↔ y=3) and `target_dimension_p2 = 0` (P2 connects along **x**, i.e. x=0 ↔ x=3). Path uses moore (26-connected) BFS through own-color stones; needs ≥1 stone on each opposing face. Min stones for connection = 4 (axis_size). Both connection complete same tick → draw (R16 fix).

**Asymmetry note.** P1 and P2 connect on **perpendicular** axes (y vs x). The two paths are not directly contested — both can co-exist geometrically — so the contest is a race plus capture sniping, not a Hex-style block.

**Degenerate-rule flags.**
- Win threshold 0.5 is irrelevant (connection condition uses BFS, not threshold).
- Propagation parameters are noise — unused.
- Move actions occupy ~1664 of 1729 action IDs but are never preferable to placement under any normal sequence (see Phase 3).

## Phase 3 — Strategic Analysis (joint)

**Dominant strategy?** Yes. P1's edge-corner y-column is essentially a forced win in 4 of P1's own moves. P1 plays first, places at a corner, and the only way to stop the column is to capture a chain stone — which requires P2 to commit ≥2 stones into the chain's neighborhood, which P2 can't do without losing at least one to P1's natural extension first.

**Counter-play?** Almost none against best-play P1 edge-line. P2's single-stone threats die on P1's next extension (because the cell adjacent to two chain stones counts the chain stones as the 2 P1 friends triggering capture). The only "counter-play" is hoping P1 opens interior, which a competent P1 won't.

**Short-term vs long-term.** No long-term play exists — games end in ≤9 ply.

**Emergent concepts.**
- **2-friends gravity well.** Once a player has any 2 stones inside the moore neighborhood of any cell C, placing at C captures whatever enemy is at C. This means own-clusters cast a "kill cone" on adjacent space.
- **Tempo arithmetic.** P1's 4-move plan vs P2's 4-move plan — P1 wins by one tempo, period. Captures cost a tempo each (the captured stone must be re-placed), so the game is decided by who can force a capture first. Edge-line P1 forces 0 captures and wins; any other P1 opening leaks captures and can lose to P2's interception.
- **No territory, no influence, no ko.** Without propagation and with one-shot captures, the rich Go-like positional concepts don't appear. The game is essentially a tactical race.

**Does 3D add depth?**

The 3D substrate lets P1 and P2 have **perpendicular** target dimensions, which means the two races geometrically share only the (0,y,0)..(0,y,3) and (x,0,0)..(x,0,3) faces. The races don't physically intersect, so there's no "block your opponent on the same column" tension that 2D Hex has. **The 3D substrate hurts depth** — flattening this game to 2D moore (where x and y are the only axes and the players still have perpendicular targets) would arguably make it more interesting because the boards would intersect more often.

Could this be a 2D moore 4×4 with perpendicular Hex-style targets and outnumber-2 capture? Yes, and it would have the same structural flaws but in a smaller, simpler frame. The 3D-ness adds the third routing axis (z) but it's used purely as "extra room to build away from the opponent" — which is what makes P2's escape-line strategy possible at all but still loses by tempo.

**Topology effect.** Moore-26 in 3D is what makes outnumber-2 capture so cheap. With grid (von Neumann, max_deg=6 in 3D), the same threshold-2 outnumber would require 2 of the 6 face-adjacent neighbors — far harder to set up and far harder to capture. The Moore choice probably explains why fitness selected this configuration: it keeps games short and decisive.

**Move-enabled but vestigial.** 0 moves used in 4 games. The 1664 move action IDs are dead weight. Phase-2 quality flag: yes.

**First-mover advantage.** Severe under P1 edge-line. 3/3 P1 wins in main games, 7/9/7 ply average. The training data's seat_balance variance ({0.5, 0.0, 1.0, 0.5, 0.0, 0.5} across 6 seeds) supports this — multiple seeds resolved to a 1.0/0.0 split, consistent with a learned pure-strategy regime rather than a balanced game. The R17 seat-bal floor 0.1 didn't fire here (avg 0.5), but 6 seeds with this much variance suggests the population was selecting a strong basin per seed rather than a fundamentally balanced game.

## Phase 4 — Novelty Adversary

The Adversary's job is to argue this is **not** novel.

**Catalog comparison.**

- **Go.** Different capture (surround vs outnumber), different win (territory vs connection), no movement. Not Go.
- **Hex / Y / Havannah.** Connection wins. But Hex has no captures and is 2D triangular; Y/Havannah are 2D. This game is 3D moore with capture — a clear structural difference.
- **3D Tic-Tac-Toe / Qubic.** 3D, line-completion win. Qubic wins by a 4-in-a-line; this game wins by face-to-face connection (much weaker condition — staircase suffices). No capture in Qubic. Different.
- **3D Hex variants.** Several have been studied (e.g., Schensted's 3D Hex, the cubic-lattice "3-Hex" variants). These typically use 6- or 12-connectivity and no capture. Adding outnumber-2 capture to a 3D moore connection game is a less-explored combination.
- **Reversi/Othello.** Outnumber capture is conceptually similar (flank to flip). But Othello flips between custodian endpoints on lines; this game removes outnumbered enemies in a single neighborhood check. Not Othello — closer in spirit to a "majority-wins-the-cell" rule like in Lifelike CAs.
- **Lines of Action.** 2D, movement-based, win=connect-your-pieces. This game's connect-your-pieces win is similar but the topology and capture are different.
- **Connect6 / Pente.** 2D, line-formation, no capture / Pente has 5-row capture. Not similar enough.
- **Tumbleweed / Slither.** 2D, different mechanics.
- **Mancala / Nim / Diplomacy / Blotto / Gungo.** Not relevant (no simultaneous play, no count-and-distribute).

**Specific challenge: "this is just 3D Hex with outnumber capture".** That is essentially the most accurate analogue. The Adversary argues:
1. Use 3D moore for connectivity instead of cubic-faces — that's a parameter choice, not a new mechanic. Moore-3D was tried in the literature for Connect-K variants.
2. Outnumber-2 capture is Reversi-flavored. Borrowing it doesn't make a new game.
3. Connection win on perpendicular axes is the standard Hex-on-asymmetric-grid trick (e.g., Hex's two players have different sides). Just lifted to 3D.
4. Place-and-move actions are vestigial (verified 0 moves used in 4 games), so the "hybrid action space" is decorative.

**Rebuttal.** Three Phase-2 moments push back:

(R1) **The "kill cone" emergent mechanic.** In Game 2 T9, P1's stone at (0,3,0) captures P2's (1,3,0) because P1 already had two friends in (1,3,0)'s neighborhood from the chain. This is NOT a Hex-with-capture pattern — Hex doesn't have capture. It's also not Reversi — Reversi flips on a custodian line, not a neighborhood threshold. The combined "outnumber on moore-26 neighborhood while building a connection chain" generates a specific tactic — every chain stone retroactively threatens enemies in a 26-cell halo — that doesn't appear in any single-mechanism predecessor.

(R2) **Perpendicular target axes + 3D routing space.** Game 3-alt's P2-win sequence used the z-axis as routing space to build separately from P1, then captured back along the y=3 row. Pure 2D analogues don't have a free routing axis. The 3D substrate uniquely enables this.

(R3) **First-stone immunity.** Captures need ≥2 friends; the first stone you place near an enemy is always safe (only 1 friend at the moment of placement). This creates a deterministic "first attacker is exposed, second attacker captures" rhythm — distinct from Reversi (where placement always flips) and from Go (where capture is by liberty). This is an emergent feature of `outnumber + threshold=2 + small board`.

**Adversary's verdict.** The combination is a coherent point in design space, but the components are individually well-explored. The dominant-edge-line bug puts a ceiling on novelty — the game's "novel emergent behavior" is mostly *that one specific exploit*, not a rich emergent strategy lattice.

**Novelty score.** 4/10. "3D moore Hex with outnumber-2 capture" is a recognizable combination; the kill-cone tactic is a small but real new flavor.

- **Balance: 2** — 3/3 P1 wins in main games, all by edge-line. - **Novelty (post-adversary): 4** — "3D moore Hex with outnumber-2 capture" is a recognizable combination of well-studied components. The kill-cone tactic is a distinct emergent feature that doesn't appear in any direct predecessor. Move action vestigialness reduces effective novelty.
- **Replayability: 3** — Once the edge-line exploit is known, P1 always plays it and games are 7-ply rituals. P2 has nothing to explore. Re-playing only useful if you swap roles, but then P2 is just losing.
- **Overall ("Would I play this again?"): 3** — Has a couple of interesting tactical ideas (the kill cone, the asymmetric-axis race) but the dominant exploit makes serious play uninteresting after one exposure.

### Closest known-game analog

**3D Hex with outnumber capture** (no widely published name). Specifically: Hex's face-to-face connection win, Reversi's outnumber-flavored capture, Qubic's 3D substrate. Differences: Hex has no capture, Reversi captures along lines not by neighborhood-majority, and Qubic wins on 4-in-line. The hybrid is recognizable but not identical to any specific published game.

### Killer flaws

1. **Severe first-mover edge-line exploit.** P1 wins in 7 ply with a column at any y-edge corner; no realistic P2 counter exists.
2. **Move actions are vestigial.** 1664 of 1729 action IDs are unused under reasonable play. The hybrid action space is decoration.
3. **Outnumber-2 + moore-26 = capture too cheap.** Any 2 own stones in a 27-cell neighborhood is a kill threat. This collapses positional strategy.
4. **Game length too short.** 7–9 ply against max_turns=100. The board is barely populated when the game ends (4 P1 stones suffice). No mid-game phase exists.
5. **Win-threshold and propagation parameters are dead.** Threshold=0.5, prop_type='none' but radius/strength/decay encoded — confused/redundant rule blob.

### Best quality

The **kill-cone / 2-friends-gravity-well** mechanic — every stone you place subtly threatens every enemy stone in its 26-cell halo as soon as you have a second supporter nearby. This creates a specific, repeatable tactical pattern (build cluster → walk through enemy stones, capturing each on contact) visible in Games 2 and 3-alt. It's a real piece of emergent design.

### 3D structural contribution

The 3D substrate **does not** add meaningful depth here. It enables P2's escape-line strategy (build x-line on a far layer to avoid P1's chain) but that's a losing line anyway. The perpendicular target-axes setup means the two players' races geometrically don't conflict, so 3D primarily adds routing space rather than positional tension. **This game would be approximately equivalent on a 2D moore 4×4 board** with the same perpendicular-axis connection rule (probably slightly more interesting because the two paths would intersect more often and force real blocking).

The R17 hypothesis being tested is "3D + custodian + connection + mobile stones is genuinely novel." This game has outnumber (not custodian), and uses the 3D substrate as separation room rather than for genuine spatial-combinatorial depth. So this particular game is **weak evidence** for the 3D-shift hypothesis. The R17 champion (`44f6630277b3`) with custodian capture might fare better.

### Improvement ideas

1. **Raise outnumber threshold to 3** (or use grid topology with threshold 2). Either change makes captures rarer, killing the kill-cone exploit and making chains harder to plough through.
2. **Make P1 and P2 share the connection axis** (Hex-style — both connect along the same dimension, just opposing faces). Forces real blocking and converts the race into a real positional contest.
3. **Forbid corner placement on T1** (or first 2 plies). Removes the edge-line exploit and forces P1 into the central, capture-prone region where the game's emergent tactics have to be solved properly.
4. **Drop move actions entirely** (or require move to capture, not just relocate). The vestigial action space is wasted policy capacity.
