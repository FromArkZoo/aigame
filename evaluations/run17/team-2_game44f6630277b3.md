## Phase 1 — Rule Comprehension

**Board.** 4×4×4 = 64 cells, 3D `grid` topology, 6-neighbour von Neumann adjacency (face-only, no diagonals). No wraparound — boundaries are hard walls for both movement and custodian capture walks. Cell index = `z*16 + y*4 + x`. `max_degree = 6`. Action space = `64 (place) + 1 (pass) + 64 × 6 (move) = 449`.

**Turn structure.** Alternating, 1 piece per turn. P1 first. Two consecutive passes ⇒ draw. `max_turns = 86`.

**Action types — hybrid place + move.**
- **Place**: drop a stone on any empty cell (`target = "empty"`, `constraint = "anywhere"`, `first_move_anywhere = True`).
- **Move**: relocate one of your own stones to a face-adjacent **empty** cell (`move_constraint = "adjacent_empty"`). The move triggers the same custodian capture walk at the destination as a placement.

**Capture: custodian, threshold = 1 (Othello-style; threshold field is inert).** When you place or move-arrive at a stone, the engine walks each of the 6 axis directions; consecutive enemy stones are queued; if the walk terminates on a friendly stone (before hitting an empty cell or the board edge), the queued enemies all flip to your colour. **Custodian fires only from the placing/moving player's perspective** — a player who places between two enemy stones does NOT auto-flip; the bracket only fires when the bracketer places the closing stone. Walks do NOT wrap on grid (would only wrap on torus).

**Propagation: none.** `prop_type = "none"`. The `radius`, `strength`, `decay` fields are stored but never read by the engine. `engine.board_values` plays no role in this game.

**Win condition: connection (asymmetric).**
- **P1** must form a 6-connected path of own stones from `z=0` face to `z=3` face (`target_dimension = 2`).
- **P2** must form a 6-connected path of own stones from `x=0` face to `x=3` face (`target_dimension_p2 = 0`).
- Goal axes are **perpendicular** ⇒ P1 and P2 routes can intersect but cannot share cells.
- BFS connectivity, not threshold-based (the `threshold = 0.5` field is ignored by `_check_connection`).
- The path doesn't need to be a straight line — any 6-connected chain of own stones spanning the goal-axis faces wins (this turns out to be very important; see Phase 2 Game 1).

**Cellular automaton: none.**

**Degeneracy flags raised (Phase 1):**
1. **Inert capture threshold**: `threshold = 1` is meaningless for custodian (engine never reads it).
2. **Inert propagation block**: `prop_type = "none"`; `radius`, `strength`, `decay` are pure rule-complexity noise.
3. **Movement is structurally vestigial in early/midgame** because optimal play wins in 4–9 moves — confirmed in Phase 2 (0 of 22 turns used a move action across my 3 games).
4. **Boundary-anchor immunity is asymmetric**: the z=0 and z=3 faces are immune to z-axis flank-capture, the x=0 and x=3 faces are immune to x-axis flank-capture, etc. This creates strategic asymmetries that depend on which player owns the corner/edge cells.
5. **The 4³ board is small enough** that `max_turns = 86` is wildly oversized — most decisive games end in <12 turns.

## Phase 2 — Strategic Play

All moves engine-verified via `eval_pilot_helper.py`. I deliberately probed attack lines the pilot did not explore: in particular, **P2 playing a boundary x-flank capture against P1's z=0 anchor stones**, which proved decisive in 2 of 3 games and which the pilot's runs never tested.

**Reflections.**
- **P1**: My move 7 was the killer error — I misread (0,0,0)P2 as part of a straight x=0 column (irrelevant to P2's win condition along x-axis), when actually (0,0,0) was setting up the L-bend. **The connection-win does not require a straight row** — P1's defence has to think in terms of arbitrary 6-connected paths, not just rows. Also: my move-1 choice of (2,1,0) gave P2 the cleaner x-flank attack. Any z=0 stone in row y=1 is at risk if P2 anchors at (0,1,0) or (3,1,0).
- **P2** (me): The x-flank capture pattern (`P2 at boundary x-anchor + P2 closing bracket + P1 cells in between → all P1 cells flip`) is the core attacking idea this game enables. Plus the connection path can use captured cells as "free" intermediate stones, since custodian flips them to P2's colour and they immediately count toward P2's connection.

### Game 2 — P2 wins at move 4 via "wide x-flank" — a forced 4-move loss for P1

I explicitly tested whether the pilot's preferred opening (1,1,0)=5 has a forced loss. **It does**, against a P1 who naively defends adjacently.

| # | Player | Action | Coord | Reasoning |
|---|--------|--------|-------|-----------|
| 1 | P1 | 5 | (1,1,0) | Pilot's preferred opening — strongest cell on z=0 face by neighbour count (5 neighbours). |
| 2 | P2 | 4 | (0,1,0) | x=0 boundary anchor, sets up x-flank capture. |

The pilot didn't trigger this because in their games P2 always voluntarily played the row-bisector (1,1,2) or a vertical block, which leaves P1's z=0 anchor untouched.
- **P2** (me): The wide-bracket capture is high-leverage: 4 moves, 2 captures, immediate row connection. Any P1 that doesn't anticipate the boundary x-flank loses fast.

### Game 3 — Seat-swap. P1 (me, knowledgeable) plays the "interior column" defence and wins at move 9 via x-flank counter-capture

I deliberately took P1 with full awareness of the x-flank trap, and chose an interior anchor instead of z=0. P2 plays the same x-flank attack as Games 1–2.

**Reflections.**
- **P1** (me): The recipe is (i) interior z=1 anchor first (decouple from P2's x-flank threats on z=0), (ii) plant the z=0 column anchor on a row that doesn't have a P2 boundary anchor, (iii) use x-flank counter-capture when P2 closes in (move 5 swung 2 stones in my favour and bought decisive tempo), (iv) finish the column at z=2 then z=3. Won by 1 tempo.
- **P2**: I was effectively forced into the standard race once my x-flank trap missed. Without a P1 stone in the y=1,z=0 row, my (0,1,0) opening becomes a slow row-builder, and P1's column race wins by the alternation tempo (P1 places 4, P2 places 3 in 7 turns; +1 tempo for P1).

### Strategy guides

**P1 strategy guide** (after seeing all 3 games):

1. **Do not play a y=1,z=0 anchor on move 1** (or any z=0 cell where P2 can build a 3-stone wide bracket). The 4-move forced-loss in Game 2 is real.
2. **Open interior** — z=1 cells are flank-attackable but require *two* P2 stones to bracket on each axis, giving you tempo to respond.
3. **Plant the z=0 anchor on move 3 in a row that P2 has not committed to**. If P2 played (0,1,0), avoid row y=1 at z=0; use row y=2 or y=3.
4. **Watch for x-flank counter-captures of your own.** When P2 lays down a single stone adjacent to one of your z=0 stones, you may be able to flip it by closing the bracket from the boundary — this is a free +2 stone swing.
5. **Connection paths can be L-shaped** — both for you and for P2. Don't only think in straight lines.
6. **Movement is unused.** Do not waste a turn on it.

**P2 strategy guide** (after seeing all 3 games):

1. **Move 2 = boundary x-anchor (e.g. (0,1,0)=4)** sets up the x-flank capture. Against any P1 who places a y=1,z=0 stone, this leads to a forced 4-move win if P1 defends naively at move 3.
2. **L-shape connection paths** are routinely faster than straight rows. Use captured P1 cells as free path stones (Game 1 used (1,1,0) and (2,1,0) — both captured P1 stones — as connection-path cells).
3. **If P1 plays interior** (like (2,2,1)), pivot strategy: use boundary anchors on multiple rows to maintain x-flank threats on whichever row P1's z=0 anchor lands. With seamless threats P1 must spend moves defending, slowing the column race.
4. **Movement is unused.** Same as P1.

## Phase 3 — Strategic Analysis (joint)

### Major divergence from pilot

**The pilot evaluated this game as P1-dominant (P1 won 3/3) and gave Balance = 2.** My evaluation found the *opposite* dynamic when P2 plays the x-flank attack:

| Game | Opening | P2 strategy | Winner | Moves |
|------|---------|-------------|--------|-------|
| 1 | P1 (2,1,0) edge | P2 x-flank + L-shape | **P2** | 8 |
| 2 | P1 (1,1,0) pilot's choice | P2 wide-x-flank trap | **P2** | 4 |
| 3 | P1 (2,2,1) interior + counter-capture | P2 x-flank | **P1** | 9 |

The pilot's P2 played defensive blocks (e.g., (1,1,2), (1,1,3)), which are weak. Once P2 plays the boundary x-flank attack, P1 must avoid z=0 row anchors and use counter-captures.

So the actual game has a **knowledge asymmetry**: whichever side knows the boundary-flank tactics dominates regardless of seat. A skilled-vs-naive matchup is decided in 4–8 moves; a skilled-vs-skilled matchup goes ~9 moves and is decided by tempo.

### Distinct viable strategies?

- **For P1**: at least 2 viable winning recipes — (a) interior anchor + counter-capture (Game 3), (b) edge anchor + careful row-avoidance (not demonstrated cleanly in my games, but plausible from move analysis). The pilot's "edge anchor + extend column" works only if P2 doesn't attack the boundary.
- **For P2**: at least 2 viable winning recipes — (c) x-flank capture + L-shape connection (Game 1 demonstrates an 8-move version), (d) wide-bracket trap (4-move forced win against naive P1, Game 2). Plus the pilot's untested "boundary-row trap" (which I didn't replay but pilot reports loses).

So actually there are **3–4 distinct strategy archetypes** in this game — more than the pilot found.

### Meaningful counter-play?

**Yes, more than the pilot indicated.** The game has a tactical "rock-paper-scissors":
- P2 boundary x-flank beats P1 naive z=0 anchor.
- P1 interior anchor + counter-capture beats P2 x-flank.
- P2 row-race or capture combo beats P1 over-defensive (extending column without watching P2's row).

The interaction creates a real decision tree at moves 2–4.

### Short-term vs long-term tension?

Almost entirely short-term. Decisive games end in 4–9 moves; max_turns=86 is mostly unused. With both players playing strong tactics, a long game is possible (potentially heading toward draws or P2 board-control wins via majority connection at z=0), but my Phase-2 didn't reach those positions.

### Emergent concepts

- **Capture-as-connection-completion** (pilot identified this; confirmed in my Game 1 — captured cells go directly into the connection chain).
- **Wide-bracket trap** (NEW — multi-cell custodian capture that completes a connection in one stroke; Game 2 captured 2 cells and won simultaneously).
- **Counter-capture timing** (NEW — Game 3, move 5: an x-flank can be flipped by completing the bracket from the opposite direction, giving a +2 tempo swing).
- **Boundary-row anchoring** (the row-builder always wants to be on a boundary face for flank-immunity along that axis; the column-builder must avoid playing in those same boundary rows).
- **Asymmetric goal axes** create *structural* opening-theory: the choice of x-anchor vs z-anchor cells matters fundamentally.

### Does 3D actually contribute depth?

**More than the pilot estimated, but not centrally.** The interior-anchor strategy in Game 3 explicitly used the z=1 layer to evade the z=0 boundary trap — this is a 3D-specific manoeuvre (in 2D there is no equivalent "interior layer" parallel to the goal face). However, the *core tactical motifs* (custodian flank capture, connection path, boundary-anchor immunity) all exist in 2D. So 3D adds **defensive strategic options** (z=1 evasion) and **larger action space** but doesn't fundamentally change the game's character.

A 2D 4×4 version with the same rules would still be playable and still have the same wide-bracket trap, but P1 would have fewer evasion options.

### Topology change effect (counterfactual)

- **Replace 3D grid with 2D 4×4 grid**: game still works; P1 loses interior-anchor evasion option; tactical traps simpler but identical in principle.
- **Replace with hex topology**: custodian capture is **skipped** by the engine on hex. Game loses its defining capture mechanic. Becomes pure 3D Hex (non-trivial but stripped of the cross-mechanic).
- **Replace with torus**: custodian walks still bounded by axis (don't wrap), but propagation does — would change connection geometry significantly. Boundary-anchor immunity disappears.
- **Replace with moore (3D 26-neighbour)**: custodian skipped. Same as hex case.

Game's identity is "3D + grid + custodian + connection" specifically; topology change destroys the rule mix.

### Move actions: vestigial

**0 of 22 turns** across my 3 games used a move action (matches pilot's 0/23). 384 of 449 actions are move actions, almost all illegal or strictly dominated. **Confirmed vestigial at the strategic-play level** — but this also means the place-only sub-game IS the real game, and the rule-complexity score (17) is inflated.

### First-mover advantage

**My data: P1 won 1, P2 won 2** (over 3 games). Combined with pilot's 3/3 P1, the total picture is 4 P1 wins, 2 P2 wins out of 6 games. The win-distribution depends heavily on which player plays the better tactic, not on seat.

The trained PPO agents' reported 0.5 self-play winrate is now more credible — the agents likely discovered both the boundary x-flank attack AND the counter-strategies, leading to balanced training. The pilot missed the x-flank attack entirely with their human probes; mine surfaces it.

**Updated Balance assessment**: with knowledgeable play on both sides, the game appears reasonably balanced (~50/50 with tempo edge to P1). With knowledge asymmetry, whichever side knows more tactics wins decisively in 4–8 moves.

## Phase 4 — Novelty Adversary

**Adversary's case (game is NOT novel):**

(a) **Re-skin of 3D Hex with capture.** 3D Hex / Qubic / 3D Connect-4 are well-known abstract games where you race to connect opposite faces of a 3D cube. Adding Othello-style capture is mechanical, not creative.

(b) **"3D Othello with a connection objective."** Custodian capture is the defining mechanic of Reversi/Othello. Bolting on a connection win condition is a parameter swap.

(c) **The asymmetric goal axes** (P1 z, P2 x) appear in published 3D Hex variants — they're not novel.

(d) **The hybrid place+move** is decorative; vestigial in actual play (0/22 turns in my games, 0/23 in pilot's). An expert from 3D Hex × Othello would transfer all relevant intuition immediately and never miss the move action.

(e) **The R17 "3D shift" basin is substrate-effect, not concept-effect.** Higher dimensional spaces have more cells; fitness rewards strategic diversity; thus 3D wins on sheer combinatorial volume. Same dynamics on a 2D 4×4 grid would score lower simply because there's less to score over.

(f) **Verdict from adversary**: novelty score 3. Direct re-skin of 3D Hex × Othello.

**Rebuttal:**

1. **The wide-bracket trap (Game 2 move 4) is genuinely cross-mechanic** — neither 3D Hex (no capture) nor Othello (no connection) nor 3D Tic-Tac-Toe (no capture) nor Qubic (no capture) supports the dynamic where capturing 2 cells along an axis with one placement *is also the winning connection move*. An Othello expert reads "capture" as "stone count for territory"; a Hex expert reads "axis-connection" as "build chain" with no flanking risk. The motif of "single placement = double capture = connection win" is specific to this rule combination. It happened forced-fashion in my Game 2 in 4 moves — that's not a niche emergent pattern, it's a routine winning line.

2. **Counter-capture tempo swings (Game 3 move 5)** are also cross-mechanic. An Othello expert wouldn't think of a single capture as "tempo swap for connection-race"; an Othello game cares about end-of-game count, not mid-game tempo. A Hex expert wouldn't have a capture mechanic at all.

3. **The interior-anchor evasion strategy (Game 3 move 1) explicitly uses the 3D-specific structure**: there's no 2D "interior layer parallel to goal face" to retreat to. This is a structural use of the third dimension — not strong, but real.

4. **The connection path being arbitrary 6-connected (not row-only) plus the perpendicular goal axes plus custodian** creates a board-control problem that doesn't reduce to either ancestor. P2 in Game 1 won via L-shape — an Othello player wouldn't think to use captured stones as path components; a Hex player wouldn't have captured cells at all.

5. **Concession**: the rule combination is a recombination, not emergent dynamics. Novelty floor is 4–5, not 7+.

6. **Concession**: the game's dynamics largely reduce to a 2D analogue; the 3D-substrate contribution is small but non-zero.

**Adjusted novelty score: 5.** Genuine recombination novelty (the wide-bracket connection-completion is a real cross-mechanic motif) but not an emergent design (the rule-combination produces tactics that experts in either ancestor would recognise within ~5 games of play). Above pure re-skin (3); below emergent (8+).
