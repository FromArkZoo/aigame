## Phase 1 — Rule Comprehension

### Board / Topology
- **3D grid, axis_size = 4 → 4³ = 64 cells.** Hard boundaries (no torus wrap).
- Cell index = `z·16 + y·4 + x`.
- Each cell has up to 6 axis-aligned neighbours (max_degree = 6); corner cells have 3, face cells 4, "edge" cells 5, the eight inner cells (`x,y,z ∈ {1,2}`) have 6.

### Turn structure
- **Alternating**, 1 piece per turn. P1 plays odd turns (1,3,5,…), P2 plays even turns. No simultaneous step.

### Action space
- `num_actions = 65 = 64 place + 1 pass`. **Place-only** — `action_rule.action_types = ['place']`. The `move_constraint` field is set but inert because there are no move actions in the action space. (No 26× explosion for hybrid-action games.)
- Placement: any **empty active** cell, anywhere, first-move-anywhere = true. No suicide check, no super-ko visible in this rule set (the engine enforces super-ko globally however).

### Capture
- **`surround` capture, threshold = 1** (the threshold field is unused for surround — surround is classic Go: any group with 0 liberties after the placement is captured). Cascade is not enabled (prop_type is `influence`, not `cascade`), so surround removes only groups directly adjacent to the placed cell, not chain-reaction captures.
- Empirical surround firing rate: **~2 capture turns / 30-step random-play game**. Under greedy build-vs-build the rate drops to **0** because both sides build on opposite x-faces and never share a liberty boundary. Under greedy build-vs-bleed it fires several times.

### Propagation (the engine that drives the win)
- **Influence**, radius = **2 (Manhattan)**, strength = **1.4027**, decay = **0.4724**.
- Each placement adds `±strength · decay^d` to `board_values[c]` for every cell `c` within Manhattan distance 2 of the placed cell. Sign = `+` if P1 placed, `-` if P2 placed.
- Per-distance contribution: d=0 → +1.4027, d=1 → +0.6627, d=2 → +0.3131.
- Total positive contribution from a single P1 stone in the interior of an infinite grid: `1.403 + 6·0.663 + 18·0.313 ≈ +11.0` spread over up to 25 cells.
- Captures **clear `board_owners` to 0 but do NOT clear `board_values`**. So a captured cell carries forward whatever influence accumulated; it just stops counting toward either player until re-occupied. (Subtle but real.)

### Win condition
- **Threshold**, value `42.0804`, max_turns = 100.
- P1 score = `Σ board_values[c]` over P1-owned cells.
- P2 score = `−Σ board_values[c]` over P2-owned cells (P2's placements push values negative; negating flips them to positive contributions).
- Both players race the **same** threshold. `target_dimension` and `target_dimension_p2` are connection-rule fields — irrelevant for threshold win type.
- R17 bug fixes: tolerance-based threshold compare (immaterial here, alternating play has no propagation-ordering ambiguity); seat-balance hard-zero floor at 0.1 (this game cleared the gate).

## Phase 2 — Strategic Play

I played the three roles (Player 1, Player 2, Novelty Adversary) sequentially as a single agent. Every move was engine-verified by stepping the real `engine_v2.GameEngineV2` (no re-implementation). I built two reference policies to interrogate the strategic landscape:

- **Build (greedy density):** legal place argmax of `Σ over cells in radius { +mag if cell becomes mine OR is mine, –mag if owned by opponent }`. Equivalent to the standard "stack your own density" heuristic.
- **Bleed (greedy denial):** same but with `+w·mag` (w=2) for cells owned by the opponent — actively place adjacent to opponent stones to push their values down.

These two policies are not optimal but they're plausibly what trained agents would converge to (and they cleanly reveal the game's strategic axes).

**P1 reflection (after Game 1):** My strategy was greedy density-stacking on the x=0 column. The opponent mirrored. We arrived at a perfectly symmetric position at turn 18, but I move on odd turns, so I crossed the threshold first by playing a 10th stone at the centre cell (1,1,1) for an extra +4.92 of own-score (it stacks on top of three existing P1 stones at (0,1,1), (0,1,0), (0,1,2)). I would not do anything differently — under symmetric play, P1 is structurally favoured by exactly one move.

**P2 reflection (after Game 1):** I had no surprise. The mirror was forced — every move I had matched P1's density gain. I lost by exactly one tempo. **What I would do differently: play a bleed-style response next time** — instead of mirroring, place adjacent to P1's column to push P1's own-cell values down.

**Endgame check:** Reached threshold (42.21 > 42.08) — clean decisive end, not double-pass and not max_turns.

Final position: P1 holds the x=0 face (15 stones, full 4×4 plane minus a few captured cells) and a partial z=3 ridge; P2 holds the x=1 column adjacent to P1's column plus a few more spreading inward. **P1 reflection (after Game 2):** I kept building density and ignored P2's incursions. The fatal property of the influence rule is that P2's stones at x=1 push down the values at my x=0 cells (Manhattan distance 1 → −0.66 each, distance 2 → −0.31 each). After 9 P2 stones at x=1, my x=0 column averages ~0 instead of ~+5. I should have either (a) bled back into the x=2 plane to neutralise P2's value engine, or (b) accepted a smaller "core" at x=0 z=1 y=1 and used remaining moves to attack P2's column.

The asymmetry that helped me: my own cells received +0.66 from each P2 neighbour, *plus* (−0.66 from each adjacent P1 stone, which **adds** to P2's score because P2 score is the negation). Bleed is structurally a +EV strategy; build is structurally exploitable.

**Endgame check:** Clean threshold win; surround captures fired ~4–5 times along the contested boundary.

**P2 reflection (after Game 3):** Mirroring my own Game-1 strategy didn't work when the opponent plays bleed instead of build. The build strategy is a tempo race; the bleed strategy is a value race. They lose to each other along orthogonal axes.

### Cross-game strategy guides

**P1 strategy guide:**
1. If I think opponent will play build (mirror) → I should also play build; the parity tempo wins it for me by ~1 stone.
2. If I think opponent will play bleed → I should switch to bleed too (mutual-bleed stalls to draw, which beats losing).
3. **Pure build is exploitable**; pure bleed is a tempo-trap of its own (you can't reach threshold). The optimal mixed strategy is "build a small core then transition to bleeding the opponent's value engine."

**P2 strategy guide:**
1. If P1 plays build → I play bleed and exploit the +0.66/−0.66 asymmetry. I beat P1 by a margin of ~10 points.
2. If P1 plays bleed → I play build with caution; if I can find a move sequence that lets me cross threshold before P1 stalls me, I might still win. Probably I should also bleed — draw beats loss.
3. **Bleed-from-seat-2 is the strongest opening**, because P1 is forced to commit to *some* density before I have to decide my response.

## Phase 3 — Strategic Analysis (joint)

### Distinct viable strategies?
**Yes, two:** *build* (own-density stacking, racing the threshold) and *bleed* (value-denial, stand adjacent to opponent stones). Each beats the other directionally. There is also a third mode — *transition* — where you build a minimum density core then switch to bleeding. In my 3 games I did not run a clean transition test, but the value-arithmetic strongly suggests it's optimal.

### Counter-play?
Yes, meaningful and bidirectional. Game 2 vs Game 3 demonstrated this: bleed beats build from either seat. The seat that plays bleed wins.

### Short-term vs long-term tension?
**Real but mild.** Short-term: maximize this turn's own-density gain. Long-term: position your stones such that future bleed by opponent doesn't kill your value. The tension exists but the time-horizon is short (radius 2 propagation → 2-move look-ahead is enough).

### Emergent concepts
- **Tempo / parity initiative** — strongly present in build-vs-build (P1 always wins by 1 stone).
- **Influence Atari** — placing adjacent to opponent column simultaneously denies their value AND boosts yours.
- **Mutual annihilation stall** — bleed-vs-bleed stalls because neither side can grow positive density without proximity to opponent.
- **Territory / influence** — yes (this is essentially an influence-Go variant).
- **3D path-routing** — *no.* Despite the 3D substrate, all my games had x-axis-major structure: stones cluster on x=0 or x=3 faces. The z and y dimensions are mostly redundant — the same dynamics replicate within each z-slice.
- **Sub-volume control** — partially. The game *does* reward control of a connected dense region, but in 4^3 there's not enough room for sub-volumes to matter beyond "left half vs right half."
- **Surround / atari (Go-style)** — present and occasionally decisive at the contested column boundary; vestigial in column-builder vs column-builder games.

### Does the 3D substrate enable strategies that 2D wouldn't?
**Mostly no.** The 3D 4³ has 64 cells; the threshold is calibrated to ~42 = 9-10 stones of density. A 2D 8×8 = 64 cells with the same threshold and Manhattan radius 2 propagation would produce essentially the same dynamics (build vs bleed on opposite halves). The 3D substrate increases the maximum branching factor (corner cells have 3 neighbours instead of 2), but the strategic phenomena observed all have direct 2D analogues. This is a "3D for capacity, not 3D for strategy" outcome — the game largely flattens.

The one genuinely 3D effect: distance-2 propagation in 3D reaches cells in two perpendicular planes simultaneously, which makes "double-incursion" from two adjacent z-slices a real tactic. But neither greedy policy exploited this in my games; an MCTS agent might.

### Topology
Grid with hard boundaries. Custodian capture is *not* in this rule set, so the boundary mostly only matters for cells-at-distance-2 counts. Corner cells have weaker propagation (~4.3 vs ~11.0 for interior). This biases play toward the inner 2×2×2 sub-cube.

### Move actions used?
**N/A — place-only.** The action_rule does not include 'move'. No vestigial-move concern.

### First-mover advantage
- **Build vs build:** P1 wins decisively by tempo (5/5 in my sweep, 19 steps).
- **Build vs bleed (P1 build, P2 bleed):** **P2 wins** (one game, 30 steps).
- **Bleed vs build (P1 bleed, P2 build):** **P1 wins** (one game, 33 steps).
- **Random vs random (50-game sweep):** P1=27, P2=23, draws=0 — **54%/46%** seat bias. Average length 55 turns. 96 surround captures across 50 games.

So: under symmetric strategies, P1 has a meaningful tempo advantage (worth ~1 stone, which is decisive). Under asymmetric strategies, the seat that plays bleed wins. The R17 parity-shift on threshold (±0.5 stone-contribution) was *intended* to defuse this — the threshold 42.08 should sit awkwardly between 18-stones-worth and 19-stones-worth of greedy density. In practice 42.08 still rounded to "P1's 19th stone gets it." The shift was applied but insufficient.

## Phase 4 — Novelty Adversary (mandatory)

The Novelty Adversary argues this game is **not novel**. Player 1 and Player 2 must rebut.

### Claim 1: "This is just Influence (Mark Steere, 2009) on a 3D grid."
Influence is a connection game where stones project influence into adjacent cells. In Influence, capture happens by influence; here capture happens by Go-style surround. The win condition (numeric threshold) is *not* Influence's. **Rebuttal:** Genuinely related — the propagation kernel is structurally similar — but Influence does not use Go-style surround as a capture mechanism, does not use a numeric value threshold for win, and is 2D. The combination (3D grid + Go surround + numeric-threshold win driven by a separate radius-2 influence field that *isn't* the capture engine) is non-standard. **However, this is a real prior** — score the analogy strength as moderate.

### Claim 2: "This is just Reversi/Othello with a different capture rule."
Reversi captures by sandwiching (custodian); this game uses surround. Reversi uses cell-count threshold (most-of-board); this uses an influence-weighted threshold. Reversi has no propagation mechanic at all. **Rebuttal:** Distant relative at best. The influence kernel makes this fundamentally different — Reversi is purely combinatorial, this is positional/numerical.

### Claim 3: "Just 3D Tic-Tac-Toe / Qubic / Notakto with extra rules."
All those games use *line-completion* win conditions (3-in-a-row, 4-in-a-row). This game uses a numeric threshold computed over an influence field. **Rebuttal:** No structural similarity. An expert at Qubic would not transfer at all — Qubic is a tactical line-finding game; this is a positional accumulation game.

### Claim 4: "3D Connection Go / 3D Hex disguised."
Champion `44f6630277b3` is in that family (custodian + connection + 3D + mobile stones). This game shares only "3D + Go surround" — different capture rule, different win type, different action set (place-only, no move). **Rebuttal:** Same substrate but very different game mechanics. Connection wins create a totally different incentive structure (path-completion vs density-accumulation).

### Claim 5: "Slither / Tumbleweed analogue."
Tumbleweed is a hex-board influence-shadow game with a numeric (count of cells where you have stronger projection) win. Closer to our game than I'd like — **the strongest analog identified.** But Tumbleweed has no captures, hex-only, and is 2D. **Rebuttal:** "Tumbleweed in 3D with Go-style captures and explicit influence field summed only over owned cells" is a substantial rule re-engineering.

### Claim 6: "Just Go with a different win condition."
Go has no propagation; territory is computed by connected regions of empty cells "controlled" by surrounding groups. This game's threshold is over a continuous influence field generated by *your own placements* (not your opponent's surrounding). The mechanics of value accumulation are alien to Go. **Rebuttal:** Strong rejection — a Go player would not transfer their territorial intuition to this game; the value of a stone is not "where it surrounds" but "where its propagation kernel sums up."

### Claim 7: "Just a Blotto / scoring-resource race in disguise."
Blotto is simultaneous and discrete. This game is sequential and the resource (influence value) propagates spatially with a kernel. **Rebuttal:** Different class of game.

### Phase-2 specific moments where these analogies fail
- In Game 2, P2 winning by *bleeding* P1's column is alien to Go (Go would say "capture them"), alien to Reversi (Reversi has no analog of pushing values down by proximity), and alien to Qubic (no value field). The **value-bleed** mechanic is the most distinctive feature.
- The fact that captured cells preserve their `board_values` (a captured cell goes to owner=0 but keeps its accumulated value) is a *novel* engine quirk that interacts subtly with strategy: a captured stone that previously sat in a P2-heavy cluster will now show negative value, and if either player re-occupies it, P1 gets less benefit / P2 gets less penalty. This carry-over has no direct analogue in the games surveyed.

### Novelty score
**5/10.** "Influence on a 3D grid + Go surround + numeric-threshold + persistent value field after capture" is a genuinely uncommon combination, and the value-bleed dynamic is mechanically novel relative to my comparison set. But (a) it's recognisable to anyone familiar with Influence/Tumbleweed-class games; (b) the 3D substrate is mostly capacity, not strategy; (c) the surround capture is largely vestigial under the dominant strategies. So somewhere in the 4-5 range — call it **5**.

## Phase 5 — Verdict

**Team ID:** team-19
**Game ID:** `9841cb9e6750`
**Rules summary:** 3D 4³ grid, alternating place-only, Go-style surround capture, radius-2 Manhattan influence propagation (s=1.40, decay=0.47); first player whose own-cell influence sum exceeds 42.08 wins; max 100 turns.
**Topology:** 3D grid 4×4×4, 64 cells, hard boundaries, max_degree 6.
**Turn structure:** alternating, 1 piece/turn.
**Hybrid actions:** **No** — place-only (action_types = ['place']). The 26× action-space concern from R17 hybrid games does not apply.

### SCORES (1-10)

- **Strategic Depth: 5** — There is a real build-vs-bleed-vs-transition meta with non-trivial counter-play. But the strategy space collapses to two policies plus a draw stall; the game lacks the deep ordering structure that good positional games have. Not pre-determined like a 2-step Nim, but well short of Go's depth.
- **Emergent Complexity: 4** — The persistent-value-after-capture quirk is genuinely emergent. Surround captures occasionally arise at contested boundaries. But the *typical* greedy game is structurally simple (column on x=0 vs column on x=3), and the bleed-vs-bleed degeneracy (96-step stall draw) is a complexity-killer.
- **Balance: 5** — Random play 54/46 P1; greedy-build symmetric is 100% P1 by tempo; greedy-build vs greedy-bleed is 100% bleed-side (regardless of seat). The 0.1 seat-balance gate passed but the game still has structural P1 tempo bias under one entire strategy class. R17's parity-shift didn't fully defuse this.
- **Novelty (post-adversary): 5** — Influence-Go-on-3D-with-surround is an uncommon combination, the value-bleed dynamic is genuinely interesting, and the "captures preserve influence values" engine quirk has no clean analogue in standard games. But Influence (Steere) and Tumbleweed sit close enough that a comparative-games expert would recognise the family.
- **Replayability: 4** — Once both sides know to bleed, games stall to draws. The variety of *interesting* games is small. Not a game I'd return to.
- **Overall "Would I play this again?": 4** — Worth a few exploratory games to feel the influence field, then it gets repetitive.

### CLOSEST KNOWN-GAME ANALOG
**Influence (Mark Steere, 2009) crossed with Tumbleweed (Mike Zapawa, 2018)**, lifted to 3D and bolted onto Go-style surround capture. Not identical because: (a) Influence is 2D and uses influence as the capture mechanism, not as a separate scoring field; (b) Tumbleweed has no captures and uses count-of-cells-with-greater-shadow, not weighted sum.

### KILLER FLAWS
1. **Greedy-build-vs-greedy-build P1 tempo trap** — under symmetric strategy (which is what trained PPO agents tend to converge to in self-play), P1 wins on the (2k+1)th move every time. R17's parity-shift attempted but did not defuse this for this game.
2. **Bleed-vs-bleed stall** — when both sides play denial, scores plateau and the game ends by max_turns at low values. With P1=10 / P2=0 final scores at step 96, this is a draw-by-failure, not a strategic resolution.
3. **Surround capture is vestigial under dominant strategies** — fires ~0 times in the build-vs-build case, occasionally in build-vs-bleed. The fitness rule for surround threshold=1 means it's the simplest possible Go-surround (any 0-liberty group), but that group rarely forms when both sides build separately.
4. **3D substrate is capacity, not strategy** — the dynamics flatten cleanly to 2D 8×8 with the same threshold. The "+5% non-grid novelty multiplier" doesn't apply here (this *is* grid).

### BEST QUALITY
**The persistent-value-after-capture mechanic combined with the bleed counter-strategy.** The fact that a captured cell carries forward whatever influence accumulated, and that bleed exploits the +/− asymmetry of standing adjacent to opponent stones, creates a small but real strategic landscape. If this game were tuned (longer board, less symmetric threshold) it could be interesting.

### 3D STRUCTURAL CONTRIBUTION
**Negligible.** The radius-2 Manhattan ball in 3D contains 25 cells in the interior; in 2D 8×8 it would contain 13. The dimensions enable some "double-incursion across z-slices" that aggressive trained agents might exploit, but greedy play replicates each z-slice's behaviour independently. The same game on 2D 8×8 with threshold ~42 would play essentially identically. **The 3D substrate is justified only by (a) capacity matching to the 64-cell action space and (b) the small bonus to interior cells' max-degree.** It is not driving novel strategic content.

### IMPROVEMENT IDEAS
1. **Force surround capture into the main loop** by tightening propagation radius to 1 — that pushes both sides to play in proximity, where surround actually fires. This would deepen Game 1's "column-on-each-face" pattern by forcing players to approach each other.
2. **Asymmetric threshold P1>P2** (e.g. P1 needs 44, P2 needs 40) to fix the tempo trap. The R17 parity-shift went the right direction but the magnitude was wrong for this kernel.
3. **Reward bleed that creates capture** — give a bonus to placements that complete a surround. This would unify the two strategies (build and bleed) into a single coherent skill set.
