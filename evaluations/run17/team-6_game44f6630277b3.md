## Phase 1 — Rule Comprehension

**Board:** 3D grid, 4×4×4 = 64 cells, Von Neumann adjacency (max_degree=6: 8 corners, 24 edges, 24 faces, 8 interior). Cell index = `z*16 + y*4 + x`.

**Turn structure:** Alternating, 1 piece per turn. `pieces_per_turn=1`, `turn_type=alternating`. `max_turns=86`.

**Action space:** 449 total actions = 64 placements + 1 pass + 384 move actions (`64 cells × 6 max_degree`). Move encoding for action `a > 64`: `from_cell = (a-65) // 6`, `nbr_idx = (a-65) % 6`, into the cell's actual neighbor list.

**Action types:** PLACE (on any empty cell, no first-move constraint) OR MOVE (own stone to an empty face-adjacent cell, `move_constraint=adjacent_empty`). Move triggers custodian capture from destination — important and underused.

**Capture:** Custodian, threshold 1. After placement OR move-arrival, walk along each of the 6 axis directions; collect consecutive enemy stones until a friendly stone is found, then flip the bracketed enemies. Boundary acts as a wall (no wrap on grid). Threshold parameter is unused for custodian — the walk simply requires ≥1 enemy bracketed.

**Propagation:** `prop_type=none`. Strength/decay/radius parameters are inert. (R17 GE composite simply uses placement+capture+connection without influence values.)

**Win condition:** Connection. **P1 connects target_dimension=2 (z-axis face z=0 ↔ z=3)**, **P2 connects target_dimension_p2=0 (x-axis face x=0 ↔ x=3)**. The `connection_threshold=0.5` field is unused — `connects_faces` BFS only requires any path. Drawing rule fires if both players complete simultaneously.

**Asymmetric goals:** P1 needs vertical chains (z-stacks); P2 needs horizontal chains (rows across x). The win-axes are perpendicular — they intersect at exactly one cell along any line.

**Degeneracy check:**
- No "P1 plays one cell repeatedly wins" — first move can't connect 4 face cells alone.
- Min winning chain is 4 stones (one per layer along the win axis); achievable in 4 turns minimum.
- Random vs random over 40 games: P1 wins 15, P2 wins 25, **0 draws, 0 max_turn timeouts**. Avg length 44 turns. Random play converges; the game does not stall.
- Move actions are 34.6% of legal-action usage in random play, so movement is non-vestigial in legal-action space, but I never found a strong USE for move in skilled play (see Phase 3).

## Phase 2 — Strategic Play (4 games, all engine-verified)

### Game 1 — Both players "build then block" (alternating reasoning)

**Moves:** 21,42,5,37,22,38,23,39,36,41,40,45,44,33,32,20,52,57,56,49,48,17,16,25 → **P2 wins on turn 24**.

P1 built a central column at (1,1,*) plus row 21-22-23 (z=1, y=1). P2 built a "ceiling wall" on z=2 at y=1 (37-38-39) plus extensions. P1 was forced into successive x=0 blocks (36, 40, 44, 32, 52, 56, 48, 16) as P2 cycled threats through different x=0 access cells.

**Decisive moment (turn 24):** P2 played `25 = (1,2,1)`. The custodian walk `-y` from 25 traversed `21(P1)` → `17(P2 friendly, placed turn 22)`. Bracket fired — P1's bridge stone 21 was captured and **flipped to P2**. Now P2's chain `20-21-17-33-37-38-39` connected x=0 (cell 20) to x=3 (cell 39). **The capture WAS the win.**

P1's failure was a double-threat I couldn't defend: on turn 23 P2 had simultaneously
- (a) Direct play `16` wins via `16-17-33-37-38-39`
- (b) Capture-of-21 via `25` wins via `20-21-17-33-37-38-39`

P1 had only 1 move; P1 chose to block (a) at 16. (b) won.

**P1 reflection:** The custodian capture is not just a material mechanic — it is a **connection-completion tool**. Once P2 owned 17, my 21 became a hostage cell. I should have played 25 or 17 myself preemptively, but I was already locked into forced blocks at x=0.

**P2 reflection:** Threats are most powerful when one threat is "play X to win" and the other is "play Y to capture-and-win." P1 cannot block both. The 17 setup move was the critical decision — at that moment 17 was not threatening anything visible, but it activated the future 25 trap.

### Game 2 — P1 corner column + smart P2 ceiling

**Moves:** 0,42,16,48,32,52,36,56,40,60,44,43,37,53,38,54,41,57,39,55 → **P2 wins on turn 20**.

P1 built the x=0 face densely (0,16,32 column + 36,40,44 z=2 row). P2 played a single block per turn — but each block was at z=3 above wherever P1 next went up. The full sequence of P2 z=3 blocks: `48(t4), 52(t6), 56(t8), 60(t10), 53(t14), 54(t16), 57(t18), 55(t20)`.

**Decisive moment (turn 20):** P2 plays `55=(3,1,3)`. Now P2 owns the entire row `52-53-54-55` at z=3, y=1, x=0..3. That row IS P2's winning chain — x=0 (52) to x=3 (55), face-connected. P2 wins.

**Insight:** When P1 extends z=2 stones along a single y-row (37→38→39 on y=1), the forced P2 z=3 blocks build an x-row at the same y. P2's defensive moves and winning moves COINCIDE.

### Game 3 — Seat-swap with mixed openings

**Moves:** 21,0,22,16,23,32,5,48,37,53,38,54,39,55,36,52 → **P2 wins on turn 16**.

P1 played a Game-1-style central-column attempt; P2 played the corner column 0,16,32,48 (claiming 4 x=0 stones in a column). Even faster collapse than Game 2 — P1's z=2 stones at y=1 (37,38,39,36) forced the same y=1 z=3 row of blocks, ending in 52 (turn 16) which closed P2's x=0..x=3 chain at y=1, z=3.

This game tested seat-balance from a different angle: even with the openings reversed, **the structural ceiling-block dynamic still rewards P2**. The bias is not seat-based; it's strategy-based — P1 loses by extending z=2 along a y-row.

### Game 4 — P1's counter-strategy via bracket capture

**Moves:** 0,48,16,49,32,50,51,53,35,34,33 → **P1 wins on turn 11**.

P1 anticipated the ceiling trap and played differently:
- Turn 1,3,5: build corner column 0-16-32 (z=0,1,2).
- Turn 7: claim **51** (the (3,0,3) corner) — this both blocks P2's z=3 y=0 row x-completion AND positions a P1 anchor at z=3.
- Turn 9: play **35** at (3,0,2) — the cell directly under 51, isolating a P1 "tower" at the far x=3 corner.
- Turn 11: play **33** at (1,0,2). The custodian walk +x from 33: `34(P2, just played turn 10) → 35(P1)`. **Bracket fires — P2's blocker at 34 is captured and flipped to P1.**

The instant 34 becomes P1, the chain `0-16-32-33-34-35-51` is fully P1-owned and connects z=0 (cell 0) to z=3 (cell 51). **P1 wins.**

This game shows the game is NOT P2-dominated. P1 has a deep counter via offensive capture: deliberately set up a cell where P2's natural block becomes a P1 bracket.

**Strategy guides:**

- **P1 strategy guide:** (i) anchor the corner column 0-16-32; (ii) claim 51 (or another z=3 corner) as a far-side anchor; (iii) build a z=2 row toward the anchor using stones spaced 2 apart so P2's natural block lands in the middle of two P1 stones (bracket trap); (iv) avoid extending z=2 along y=1 (gives P2 free ceiling x-row); (v) **NEVER place between two existing P2 stones along an axis** — Reversi-style capture mortal.

- **P2 strategy guide:** (i) Don't sandwich your stones between P1's along a line — always have a friendly behind. (ii) If P1 extends z=2 on a single y-row, your forced z=3 blocks AUTOMATICALLY build your winning x-chain — encourage this. (iii) Set up custodian traps at P1's critical bridge cells (e.g., the (1,1,1) center is bracketable from y-axis via 17 + 25). (iv) Watch for P1's "anchor + spaced row" pattern — when P1 has two stones with one empty cell between, **do not** play that middle empty cell.

## Phase 3 — Strategic Analysis (joint)

**Distinct viable strategies?** Yes:
- **P1 ceiling-defense** (lost Games 1,2,3) — naive y-row z=2 extension; loses to P2 auto-row.
- **P1 capture-bracket** (won Game 4) — set up bracket on P2's natural block; punctures the ceiling.
- **P2 cycle-threats** (won Game 1) — play setup moves that enable double-threat tactical wins by capture.
- **P2 ceiling auto-build** (won Games 2,3) — accept that P1 forces you to z=3, but route those forced plays to form your x-chain.

**Counter-play?** Yes — Game 4 demonstrates it directly. The bracket-capture mechanic is a true counter to P2's ceiling strategy.

**Short-term vs long-term tension?** Strong. The ceiling-build is long-term (each block scores positionally for both players), but bracket captures are short-term tactical inflections that collapse plans in 1 move.

**Emergent concepts:**
- **"Ceiling formation"**: P2's defensive stones aggregate into the winning x-chain. Deeply elegant emergent property of the asymmetric win condition + 3D grid.
- **"Bridge-cell capture"**: Capturing a single cell can flip a key intersection, completing the captor's connection. (Game 1.)
- **"Bracket trap"**: Deliberately placing stones at distance 2 along an axis so the opponent's natural block becomes a custodian victim. (Game 4.)
- **"Tempo bind"**: P1 can be forced into x=0 blocks for 6+ consecutive turns, never building toward z=3. The forced-block cells themselves don't form a P1 z-chain unless P2 distributes blocks badly.
- No ko fights observed (custodian doesn't create the empty-cell→fight-back loop Go does).

**Does 3D enable strategies 2D wouldn't?** **Yes, decisively.** The "ceiling" trap is impossible in 2D — P1 can't be forced to play z=2 stones because there's no z. Custodian capture in 3D has 6 attack axes (3 axis × 2 directions) instead of 2D's 4, so bracket setups are richer. The asymmetric win-axes (P1 z, P2 x) only exist on dim≥2; in 2D the analogue is Hex-style face connection, which has been studied extensively. The 3D variant is meaningfully different. **The game flattened to 2D would lose the central trap structure.**

**Does topology matter?** Yes. Custodian only fires on grid/torus/sierpinski/holes. On hex/moore custodian is skipped per engine. 3D grid Von Neumann (max_degree=6) is the "right" substrate — moore would be 26-connected and capture would be skipped, hex would be 2D-only.

**Movement actions used?** I used **0 move actions** across all 4 games. They feel vestigial in the openings I explored. The action space is `64+1+384=449` but the meaningful subset is the 64 placements. Possible advanced uses: (a) move a captured stone elsewhere to escape further capture, (b) move into a bracket to capture in directions you couldn't reach on placement, (c) shift connection paths under threat. None proved important in my 4 games, but I cannot rule out their utility in long games against trained agents. Random play used moves 34.6% of the time, suggesting they are NOT engine-illegal in normal positions — just rarely strategically optimal in opening/midgame.

**First-mover advantage / seat balance:**
- Skilled play: 1-3 P2 wins, 1 P1 win across my 4 games.
- Random play: P2 wins 62.5% (25/40).
- Trained PPO self-play (from DB): final winrate 0.5 across all three training seeds, average game length 36-49 turns.
- **Conclusion:** Raw mechanic favors P2 in random/naive play (the ceiling auto-row is "free" if P1 doesn't actively counter). Trained agents reach 50-50 because P1 learns the bracket-capture counter. This is consistent with R17's seat-balance gate (≥0.1 floor) being satisfied — and the game IS balanced in the limit, just with non-trivial counter-strategy required.

## Phase 4 — Novelty Adversary (mandatory)

The Adversary argues: **"This is just `<known game>` in disguise."**

**Adversary opening salvo:**
1. *"It's Connection Go in 3D."* R17's lineage notes literally call it "Connection Go in 3D, with mobile stones" rediscovering R8's recipe. That's an admission of derivation. Connection wins (face-to-face) are Hex-mechanic. Capture is Reversi/Othello. Fire and water — but neither component is novel.
2. *"It's Qubic / 3D Tic-Tac-Toe."* 3D 4×4×4 board with placement is identical substrate to Qubic.
3. *"It's just 3D Hex with capture sprinkled in."* Hex has the connection-win core; adding "capture if you bracket" is trivial overlay.
4. *"Custodian + face-connection has been studied for decades — Reversi-Connect variants exist."* (e.g., the published variant Reversia, etc.)
5. *"The asymmetric win-axes (P1 z, P2 x) are just Hex's "you-this-side, me-that-side" rule transposed to 3D."*
6. *"Movement is vestigial — strip it and you have place-only Connection Reversi 3D."*

**P1 + P2 rebuttal — specific Phase-2 moments where the analogies fail:**

**vs Connection Go**: Go uses surround capture (group with 0 liberties → removed). Custodian is fundamentally different — it FLIPS color, not removes. Game 1's win moment (P2 captured cell 21 and immediately had a P2 chain through it) is **impossible in Go**. In Go-style, the captured stone would vanish and P2 would have an empty cell, not a friendly cell. This is a **Reversi mechanic**, not a Go mechanic — so "Connection Go" is the wrong frame.

**vs Qubic / 3D Tic-Tac-Toe**: Qubic wins on **n-in-a-row** along any of the 76 lines; this game wins on **face-to-face graph connection**. Game 4's win path was 0-16-32-33-34-35-51 — a **bent path** through 7 cells, not a straight 4-in-a-row. In Qubic the same configuration would be losing.

**vs 3D Hex**: Hex has no capture. Period. The custodian mechanic is decisive in 2 of my 4 games (Games 1, 4 ended on capture). In Hex these positions don't even arise — once a stone is placed it can't be flipped.

**vs Reversi-Connect / "Connection Reversi"**: The published Reversi-Connect-variant literature is sparse and 2D. The combination of (a) **3D substrate**, (b) **asymmetric perpendicular win axes** (P1 z-axis, P2 x-axis), (c) **face-connection (not n-in-a-row)**, (d) **place OR move**, (e) **threshold-1 custodian fired from move-destination too** — this exact stack is not, to my knowledge, a single named published game.

**vs "movement is vestigial"**: This is partially conceded — I didn't find decisive uses. But in the engine, move-into-bracket can capture, and move can escape captures. Those are LEGAL even if rarely optimal — they're not bug-vestigial, just unexploited in my 4 games.

**Specific Phase-2 moments where each "known game" framing breaks:**
- *Game 1 turn 24:* P2 captures cell 21 and the captured cell IS the winning bridge. **Hex** can't do this (no capture). **Go** can't do this (capture removes, doesn't flip). **Qubic** can't do this (no capture). **Reversi** can't do this (no connection-win). Only the combination produces this moment.
- *Game 4 turn 11:* P1 deliberately places at distance 2 from another P1 stone, predicting and exploiting P2's natural block, then captures the block to fuse the chain. **Hex** has no captures. **Connect-6** has no captures. **Reversi** has no connection. **Lines of Action** has movement-based capture but no connection-win and uses different connectivity.

**Novelty score: 6/10.** Every individual ingredient is known. The combination is non-obvious; the asymmetric perpendicular connection on 3D grid with custodian-flip is a fresh mechanical stack. It is NOT "Connection Go in disguise" because the capture mechanic is fundamentally Reversi, not Go — and Reversi doesn't combine with face-connection in published games I can find. But it's also not free of analog: a Reversi player with experience playing Hex would learn this in an afternoon. Verdict: **novel as a combination, not novel in any single ingredient.**

## Phase 5 — Verdict

**Team ID:** team-6
**Game ID:** 44f6630277b3
**Rules Summary:** Two players alternate placing stones on a 3D 4×4×4 grid; placement triggers Reversi-style custodian capture (flips bracketed enemies along axes). P1 wins by connecting z=0 face to z=3 face with their stones; P2 wins by connecting x=0 face to x=3 face. Move action exists (relocate own stone to adjacent empty) but is rarely strategically optimal.
**Topology:** 3D grid 4³ Von Neumann (max_degree 6, 8 corners / 24 edges / 24 faces / 8 interior), no wrap.
**Turn Structure:** Alternating (1 piece per turn).
**Hybrid actions:** Yes — but I never used MOVE in any of the 4 games. Functionally vestigial in opening/midgame, possibly useful in advanced play.

### SCORES (1-10)

- **Strategic Depth: 6**
  Genuine tactical depth from custodian + connection interplay. Game 1's capture-the-bridge-cell win and Game 4's bracket-trap counter are legitimately deep moments. The 4³ board caps complexity (only 64 cells, games end in 11-24 turns). The "ceiling auto-row" structural trap is rich enough to require non-obvious counter-strategy, which the bracket-capture provides.

- **Emergent Complexity: 6**
  Three emergent phenomena observed: (a) ceiling-as-x-chain (P2's defense is P2's offense), (b) bridge-cell capture-completion (capturing one cell completes a connection because the captured stone joins the captor's chain), (c) bracket trap (place at distance 2 to weaponize opponent's natural block). All three are non-obvious from the rules alone.

- **Balance: 6**
  Random play: P2 62.5%. Skilled: 3:1 P2 in my 4 games. Trained PPO: 50-50 final winrate. The game is balanced in the limit but requires P1 to find non-trivial counter-strategy. Seat-swap test (Game 3) confirmed bias is strategy-driven, not seat-driven — reversing openings still gave P2 the same auto-row trap to exploit. The R17 seat-balance gate (≥0.1) is comfortably satisfied based on training data.

- **Novelty (post-adversary): 6**
  The combination 3D-grid + custodian + perpendicular face-connection + place+move is not, to my catalog search, a published named game. But every ingredient is well-known. Adversary's strongest blow ("just Connection Go") is rebutted because custodian-flip ≠ Go-surround-remove (the captured cell becomes a friendly, not empty — this distinction was decisive in Games 1 and 4). The R17 "3D structural shift" hypothesis is partially supported: 3D enables the ceiling trap in a way 2D cannot.

- **Replayability: 5**
  Fast games (11-24 turns), small board, but enough tactical variation that random openings produce varied strategic situations. Repeat play might expose convergent optimal openings — both P1 and P2 strongly want corner anchors, and z=2 / z=3 layers fight is structural. Replayability is OK for a small-format puzzle game; less so for a multi-session engagement.

- **Overall "Would I play this again?": 6**
  Tactically interesting once. Worth a few games to internalize the bracket trap and the ceiling auto-row. Wouldn't replace any of my known games but would tolerate it as a 10-minute filler.

### CLOSEST KNOWN-GAME ANALOG
**Reversi-meets-Hex on a 4×4×4 cube** with asymmetric perpendicular connection wins. Not identical to any single published game I know — the closest published cousin is probably the obscure 3D Reversi variants like *Spacial Reversi*, but those use majority/territory wins, not connection.

### KILLER FLAWS
- **Movement is essentially vestigial in skilled play** (used 0× in my 4 games). The 384 move actions inflate the action space ~6× without proportional strategic content. This is a wasted dimension of the design, though training data shows trained agents reach balance, suggesting agents can learn through it.
- **The ceiling auto-row trap is too easy a win for P2 against a naive P1.** Without the bracket-capture counter, the game tilts strongly P2. This is deep-but-asymmetric.
- **Custodian's threshold parameter is dead code** (threshold=1 with custodian doesn't affect logic — capture fires whenever any enemy is bracketed). Not a gameplay flaw but a fitness-noise concern.
- **Influence propagation parameters (radius=3, strength=0.81, decay=0.30) are inert** since `prop_type=none`. Pure rule-representation noise.

### BEST QUALITY
**The bridge-cell capture-completion mechanic** (Game 1's turn 24 and Game 4's turn 11). Capturing a single cell can be the WINNING move because the flipped stone joins the captor's chain. This is a fresh combinatorial wrinkle that the standard library of connection games doesn't have, and it makes one-move tactical reads decisive — closer to a chess fork than to a slow positional game.

### 3D STRUCTURAL CONTRIBUTION
**The 3D substrate genuinely contributes to depth.** The "ceiling" dynamic (P2's z=3 blocks accumulating into a winning x-row at any chosen y) is impossible in 2D — there's no z layer to wall off. Custodian along three axes (vs. 2D's two) creates richer bracket setups. The asymmetric perpendicular win axes (P1 z, P2 x) only meaningfully exist on dim≥2; in 3D they create a "your direction is my parallel" tension that's distinctive. **A 2D flattening (e.g., 4×4 with surround+connection) would lose the central structural trap and would converge to a much shallower Connection Reversi.** The R17 hypothesis "3D shift adds depth" is supported, with reservation that the gains are concentrated in the ceiling-trap dynamic specifically — not in arbitrary 3D extension.

### IMPROVEMENT IDEAS
**One change:** Make MOVE actions strictly more powerful by allowing `move_constraint=adjacent_any` (move to adjacent cell whether empty or enemy — capturing on arrival via collision). Currently `adjacent_empty` makes movement strictly weaker than placement (placement reaches anywhere; move only reaches adjacent empty AND must come from your stone). The 384 move actions should justify their existence by enabling some tactic placements can't achieve. With `adjacent_any` move-capture, you'd see real "displacement attacks" — tactics analogous to Lines of Action — that justify the action-space overhead.

**Alternative:** Drop move entirely and reduce action space to 65, simplifying without losing decisiveness.
