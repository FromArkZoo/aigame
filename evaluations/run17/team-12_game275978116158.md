# Team-12 evaluation — Game `275978116158` (Run 17, rank 3)

## Phase 1 — Rule comprehension

**Board / topology**
- 3D cube, axis_size = 4 → 64 cells.
- **Moore (26-neighbour)** topology: cells with `max(|Δx|,|Δy|,|Δz|) ≤ 1` are adjacent.
  - Corners (8 cells): 7 neighbours.
  - Edges (24 cells): 11 neighbours.
  - Faces (24 cells): 17 neighbours.
  - Interior (8 cells, those with all coords ∈ {1,2}): 26 neighbours.
- Cell index = `z·16 + y·4 + x`. Axis 0 = x, axis 1 = y, axis 2 = z.

**Turn structure** — **alternating**, 1 piece per turn, `max_turns = 100`.

**Action space (1729 total)**
- 0–63: place at cell `c`.
- 64: pass.
- 65–1728: move actions, encoded as `64 + 1 + from_cell·26 + neighbour_idx`.
- Move constraint = `adjacent_empty` (move target must be empty Moore-adjacent cell — moving a stone never directly captures the destination, but **the move *does* trigger outnumber capture on the destination's enemy neighbours** via `_handle_movement → _apply_captures`).

**Capture rule** — `outnumber`, threshold = 2.
- After any place-or-move into cell `C`, every enemy stone `E` adjacent to `C` is checked. If `E` has **≥ 2 friendly (current-player) neighbours** (counting the just-placed stone), `E` is removed.
- The newly placed/moved stone counts toward the friendly-neighbour count.
- **No captures of the placer's own stones** — capture only flips opponents.

**Propagation** — `prop_type = 'none'` (radius/strength/decay fields are inert dead weight).

**Win condition** — connection, Hex-style asymmetric axes:
- **P1 must form a Moore-connected chain of P1 stones from the y = 0 face to the y = 3 face** (target_dimension = 1).
- **P2 must form a Moore-connected chain of P2 stones from the x = 0 face to the x = 3 face** (target_dimension_p2 = 0).
- The `threshold = 0.5` field is ignored for connection (engine just calls `connects_faces`).
- If both players complete connection on the same tick, draw (irrelevant for an alternating game except via final-move captures revealing both chains simultaneously, which I never observed).
- The z-axis is **not a winning axis for either player**, so it functions as a "thickness" dimension that adds escape routes / connection redundancy without contributing to anyone's goal.

**Degenerate-rule flags (Phase 1):**
- **Minimum connection length is 4 stones**, but with Moore-26 those 4 stones can be a straight axial line, an axial-with-z-offset, or a diagonal — every chain that hits both faces of a 4-cube axis works.
- **Outnumber-2 in Moore-26 is brutal.** Two adjacent friendly stones already create a 2-friendly-neighbour zone — any opponent stone that sits in their shared neighbourhood is captured the moment a third friendly stone touches it. Specifically, two P1 stones at `(1,1,1)` and `(1,2,1)` already give the cell `(2,2,1)` two P1 neighbours; **anything placed there is a sacrificial offer.** This is even harsher in 3D than it would be in 2D Moore (8-neighbour) because the 26-neighbourhood overlap between two adjacent stones is much larger.
- **Move action is essentially vestigial.** All three games I played ended on turn 7 with zero move actions used — placement is faster and equally effective. The 1664-slot move action space is bloat that scales action-space novelty / non-triviality scoring without affecting play.
- **Propagation rule is dead** (`prop_type='none'`). Strength/decay/radius fields are inert.

### Reflections (per role)

**P1 strategy guide.** Open at any "interior" cell with `y ∈ {1,2}` and pick a fixed (x,z); place 3 stones in a straight Moore-line along y to span y=1..3 (3 turns, 3 stones, turns 1/3/5). On turn 7 place a y=0 stone Moore-adjacent to your y=1 anchor — there are 9 such cells, P2 can only block 1 per turn. If P2 is foolish enough to settle adjacent to two of your stones, your turn-5 placement also captures their stone for free. **There is no defence: this wins on turn 7 against any P2.**

**P2 strategy guide.** You cannot win. The minimum P2 chain is 4 stones (your goal axis is x, also length 4), achievable only on turn 8 — one tempo behind P1. You cannot capture a P1 stone in fewer than two P2 placements adjacent to it, and any P2 stone that strays into the shared neighbourhood of two adjacent P1 stones is captured by P1's next placement. The best P2 can do is (a) start an x-spine on the x=3 or x=0 face well away from P1's column, (b) avoid placing in the "shared-neighbour shell" of P1's chain, and (c) hope P1 misplays. PPO didn't reliably find P1's win, which is why training-time win-rates muddled to ~0.5 in some seeds (30060, 40042, 42042) — **but humans see this in 5 minutes of analysis.**

**Endgame resolution.** All 3 games ended by P1 connection, *not* by double-pass / max-turns. Non-triviality holds at the surface level (the 0.938 score is correct that the game *terminates*), but it terminates the same way every time.

## Phase 3 — Strategic analysis (joint)

- **Distinct viable strategies?** No. P1 has one dominant family (place a y-spine, capture any P2 invader). P2 has one dominant family (build an x-spine far from P1's column) and it always loses by exactly one tempo.
- **Counter-play?** None on the timescale of the game. P2's "near P1" positions are capture-vulnerable; P2's "far from P1" positions don't disrupt P1.
- **Short- vs long-term tension?** Absent — the game is over before any long-term consideration matters. Outnumber-2 captures are immediate and resolve in ≤ 1 turn.
- **Emergent concepts?**
  - *2-cluster shadow*: any pair of friendly Moore-adjacent stones creates a "shadow zone" of cells (their shared neighbours) where opponent stones are 1-placement-from-capture. This is a real emergent concept but it favours the leading player and accelerates the game.
  - *Tempo race*: the game compresses to a 4-stone-vs-4-stone race; first mover wins by definition.
  - No territory, influence, ko, mutual-annihilation, sub-volume control, or 3D path-routing emerged.
- **Does 3D enable strategies 2D wouldn't?** Effectively no. P1's winning column lives at a fixed (x, z) — the y-spine doesn't use the z-dimension at all. The 26-neighbourhood matters only insofar as it makes 2-cluster-shadow capture *more* punishing to P2; it doesn't open new strategic axes. **You could flatten this to a 2D (4×4 Moore-8) board with no loss of strategy** (and it would basically be a known degenerate Hex variant).
- **Does topology matter?** Yes, but pathologically: Moore-26 makes the capture rule more punishing and makes connection paths easier. Replacing with **von Neumann (6-nbr)** would slow connections and make outnumber-2 actually a contested condition (only ~6 candidates per cell instead of 26).
- **Move usage.** Across 3 games (21 moves total): **0 moves used**, 100% placements. The hybrid action space is **vestigial** — the 1664 move-action slots are pure bloat. This matters because R17's GE composite credits the game for action-space novelty even though move actions never fire in optimal play.
- **First-mover advantage.** P1 won 3/3 in 7 turns (the theoretical minimum). Quantified: **100% P1 win rate in human-play seat-swap probe**, with P1's winning sequence at most 4 placements long. The R17 0.1 seat-balance floor must have either failed to fire on this game, or the trained PPO agents were too weak to actualise the forced win consistently — the training-run table shows two seeds at exactly 0.5 final-winrate (30060, 40042, 42042), one at 0.0, one at 1.0, suggesting agents play near-randomly in the opening and the seat-swap signal got smeared.

## Phase 4 — Novelty Adversary (mandatory)

**Adversary's case (game is NOT novel):**

*(a) Closest-known-game catalog.* This game is a thinly disguised hybrid of:
- **3D Hex** (a published variant — connection wins on opposite faces of a 3D lattice with full connectivity). The asymmetric P1-on-y / P2-on-x rule is the pure Hex bridge condition.
- **Pente / outnumber-capture games** for the capture rule. Threshold-2 outnumber capture in dense neighbourhoods is the same mechanic.
- **3D Tic-Tac-Toe / Qubic** for the small-cube substrate.
The combination is well within the design space already explored.

*(b) CA literature.* Not applicable — no CA in this game.

*(c) Simultaneous-game catalog.* Not applicable — alternating.

*(d) "3D Connection Go" search.* Even granting that "3D + connection + outnumber + Moore + place+move" is an unusual *exact* tuple, every individual pair has been studied:
- 3D + connection: classical 3D Hex.
- Connection + outnumber capture: "Custodian Hex" variants.
- Hybrid place+move: Lines of Action, Amazons (the R17 champion borrows this directly).
The combination produces no emergent dynamic that any one of these games doesn't already produce.

*(e) Re-skin under coordinate transformation.* As shown in Phase 3, **the game is functionally 2D** because winning chains live on a fixed (x,z) and the z-axis is decorative. So under the transform "fold the 64-cell cube into a 16-cell square keeping Moore-8 adjacency," the game is **Hex-on-a-4×4-square with outnumber-2 capture** — a known degenerate small-board case.

*(f) Expert transfer.* A Hex player would transfer immediately and win as P1 in 7 turns on first encounter. They would correctly identify the move action as ignorable.

**Player rebuttal.**

The strongest novelty defence is the **2-cluster-shadow capture dynamic combined with Hex-style asymmetric axes**: in vanilla 3D Hex the capture mechanic is absent, and in vanilla outnumber-capture games the win condition is territorial or threshold-based, not connection. There *is* a real interaction in Phase 2 — Game 1 turn 5 where P1's chain extension simultaneously achieves both "y-spine progress" and "free capture of a P2 invader." That double-purpose move is mildly elegant. But the elegance is undone by the fact that **the same game is forced-won regardless of whether captures fire** — they accelerate but don't change the outcome. A novelty score that only counts "did anything weird happen" would inflate; one that asks "did the weird thing change anything" deflates back.

The R17 hypothesis (3D + custodian + connection + mobile stones is a genuinely novel combination) does not survive contact with this game, because:
- The "mobile stones" feature is **vestigial** (zero move-action usage in 3 games).
- The "custodian" / outnumber capture is **dominated by the connection threat** (capture happens incidentally, not as an end in itself).
- The "3D" feature is **nominal** — the game projects to a 2D plane without losing any strategic content.

**Novelty score: 3/10.** "Hex on a small 3D board with an outnumber side-rule." A Hex player learns it instantly.

## Phase 5 — VERDICT

**Team ID:** team-12
**Game ID:** `275978116158`
**Rules summary:** 3D 4-cube with Moore-26 adjacency, alternating place-or-move, outnumber-2 capture; P1 wins by Moore-connecting the y=0 face to y=3 face, P2 by connecting x=0 to x=3.
**Topology:** 3D moore, axis_size=4 (64 cells, max_degree=26).
**Turn structure:** alternating.
**Hybrid actions:** **yes, vestigial** (0 of 21 moves across 3 games used the move action; pure placement dominates).

**SCORES (1–10):**
- **Strategic Depth: 2** — P1 has a 4-placement forced win in 7 turns; no meaningful branching or long-term planning required.
- **Emergent Complexity: 3** — One real emergent concept ("2-cluster shadow" capture zones), but it accelerates the inevitable rather than creating decision-points.
- **Balance: 1** — 3/3 P1 wins by turn 7 across three different openings (centre / centre / off-centre) and three different P2 strategies (aggressive-adjacent / safe-far / mirror-x-face). P2 is one tempo short by construction. The R17 seat-balance gate evidently did not catch this — possibly because PPO's weak openings smeared the empirical win rate to ~0.5 in 3/6 training seeds.
- **Novelty (post-adversary): 3** — Combination tuple is unusual but every component is mainstream and the 3D substrate is decorative. Effectively "small-board Hex + outnumber".
- **Replayability: 2** — P1's winning recipe is repeatable; once you've seen it, no replay value as a competitive game.
- **Overall "would I play this again?": 2/10**.

**Closest known-game analog:** **3D Hex + outnumber-capture side-rule** (or, equivalently, Hex on a 4×4 Moore-8 board with the z-axis as decorative thickness). Not identical because vanilla Hex has no capture, but the captures are tactically irrelevant to the forced-win outcome.

**KILLER FLAWS:**
1. **Forced P1 win in 7 turns** under any opening I tested — first-mover advantage is structural, not stochastic.
2. **Minimum connection path = 4 stones** in a 4-cube with Moore-26; this is the smallest possible connection length, leaving no room for P2 to catch up on tempo.
3. **Move action is vestigial** — 1664/1729 actions were never used. Inflates the action-space dimension and possibly the non-triviality / depth metrics without affecting play.
4. **Propagation rule is dead** (`prop_type='none'`) but the radius/strength/decay fields are still serialised. Not a play-time flaw, but it inflates rule-complexity (18) without contributing to play.
5. **No komi, no swap, no pie-rule** — the asymmetric Hex-style win condition is identical for P1/P2 *modulo coordinate axis*, so first-mover symmetry-breaking is uncontested.

**BEST QUALITY:** The "2-cluster shadow" capture dynamic in Game 1 turn 5 — placing the y-extension stone *also* captured the trapped P2 invader. That kind of double-purpose move is exactly what gives Connection-Go its character in 2D, and seeing it survive into a 3D Moore-26 substrate is mildly interesting evidence that the design family is robust (even if this *specific* parameterisation is broken).

**3D STRUCTURAL CONTRIBUTION:** **None of strategic substance.** P1's winning column lives at a fixed (x, z); P2's defensive options are equivalent under any z-foliation. The game flattens to "Hex on a 4×4 Moore-8 board with outnumber-2 capture" without losing strategic content. This contradicts the R17 hypothesis ("3D + custodian + connection + mobile stones is genuinely novel") for *this* game — it might still hold for the actual champion `44f6630277b3`, but rank-3 here is a 3D-substrate artifact, not a 3D-strategy game. The +5% non-grid novelty multiplier from R17 likely lifted this game above its play-quality merit.

**IMPROVEMENT IDEAS (one rule change):**
**Increase axis_size to 6 or switch to von Neumann (6-nbr) topology.** Either change roughly triples the minimum connection length (4 → 6/12) and roughly halves the 2-cluster shadow size. Combined: change `axis_size=4` to `axis_size=5` AND topology to `grid` (von Neumann) — minimum connection becomes 5 stones and outnumber-2 becomes a real contest because any cell has only 6 neighbours instead of 26. This would push the game out of the "P1 has forced 4-stone win" regime into something where P2 has tempo for counter-strategy.

Secondary suggestion: **add a swap/pie rule** (P2 can claim P1's first stone). This is a cheap fix that costs nothing in rule-complexity terms and is universal in connection games.
