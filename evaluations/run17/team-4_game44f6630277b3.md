# Team-4 Evaluation — Game `44f6630277b3` (Run 17 Champion)

## Phase 1 — Rule Comprehension

**Board:** 3D grid, 4×4×4 cube (64 cells), grid topology (no wrap). Cell index = `z·16 + y·4 + x`. Each interior cell has 6 axial neighbors; faces have 5; edges 4; corners 3. Max degree = 6.

**Turn structure:** Alternating, 1 piece per turn. P1 first.

**Action space (449 actions):**
- `0..63` — place on cell (target=empty, anywhere; first move anywhere true).
- `64` — pass. Two consecutive passes → draw.
- `65..448` — move actions, encoded `64 + 1 + from_cell·6 + neighbor_idx` over `topo._neighbors[from_cell]`. Constraint: `adjacent_empty` (own stone moves to an adjacent empty cell). Action space ~26× a place-only game; legal-move count is bounded by (own stones × empty axial neighbors).

**Capture:** Custodian, threshold = 1. On placement, walk each axis ±1 from placed cell; consecutive enemy stones bracketed by friendly at far end are flipped. Walk stops at empty or boundary. **One sandwiched enemy stone is enough to capture** — a Reversi-style atari.

**Propagation:** `prop_type = none`. The non-zero radius/strength/decay fields in the rule blob are inert.

**Win condition:** Connection win, threshold 0.5.
- **P1 must connect dim 2 (z-axis):** any connected component of P1 cells touching both `z=0` and `z=3`.
- **P2 must connect dim 0 (x-axis):** any P2 component touching both `x=0` and `x=3`.
- Tied connection in same tick → draw (R16 fix).
- `max_turns = 86`. On time-out, majority piece count wins; tie → draw.

**Degeneracy flags:** Threshold=1 custodian on a 4-cell axis (only 4 stones to span) is *very* loose — a single stone placed mid-line is almost always capturable. Connection length is also 4. Both numbers are low; tactical sequences resolve in 4–17 moves in practice.

## Phase 2 — Strategic Play (3 games, every move engine-verified)

Action-decoder helper: `eval_pilot_helper.py`. The pilot's warning about 3D cell-ID typos is well-founded — I made one (placed at cell 33 = `(1,0,2)` thinking it was `(1,1,2) = 37`); the engine's `connects_faces` exposed the broken chain instantly. Correct mapping: `cell = z·16 + y·4 + x`.

### Game 1 — P1 wins in 5 moves (10 plies cumulative)
Moves: `26, 41, 10, 42, 58`.
1. P1 `(2,2,1)`. Central z-column anchor.
2. P2 `(1,2,2)`. x-chain anchor near central z=2.
3. P1 `(2,2,0)`. z=0 face anchor; chain (2,2,0)–(2,2,1).
4. P2 `(2,2,2)`. Block P1's column at z=2 + extend P2's x-chain. **Trap entered.**
5. P1 `(2,2,3)`. Walks z−1 from (2,2,3): captures (2,2,2) — bracketed by P1 stones at (2,2,1) and (2,2,3). Flip → (2,2,*) is now an unbroken X-column z=0..3. **Connection wins on the same ply.**

### Game 2 — P1 wins in 6 P1-moves (11 plies)
Moves: `21, 32, 5, 41, 33, 53, 49, 57, 61, 37, 45`.
- P1 builds z-column at `(1,1,*)`. P2 anchors x-chain at z=2 face but disconnected.
- After P1's column reaches z=2, P2 correctly **blocks at the z=3 face** (53), avoiding the central-column custodian trap.
- P1 plays `(1,0,3)=49` — sets up y-axis bracket of `(1,1,3)`.
- P2 forced to block `(1,2,3)=57`.
- P1 plays `(1,3,3)=61` — **double y-capture**: line at z=3, x=1, y descending: (1,3,3)=X — (1,2,3)=O — (1,1,3)=O — (1,0,3)=X → both Os captured. P1's z=3 face row is now solid X.
- P2 must respond to block the remaining gap at `(1,1,2)=37`.
- P1 plays `(1,3,2)=45` — **double y-capture again** at z=2: (1,3,2)=X — (1,2,2)=O — (1,1,2)=O — (1,0,2)=X. Both Os captured. Column (1,1,*) is closed and connection wins.

### Game 3 — Seat swap (I play P2). P1 wins in 9 P1-moves (17 plies)
Moves: `26, 58, 10, 57, 42, 59, 54, 50, 56, 49, 48, 53, 52, 61, 60, 41, 40`.

Strongest defense I could find for P2:
- **z=3 face counter-block** at `(2,2,3)=58` first move — neutralizes P1's central-column and starts P2's x-chain.
- Successfully counter-captured a P1 attack with `(2,0,3)=50` — y-bracketed P1's `(2,1,3)` between (2,0,3) and (2,2,3).
- Built x-chain across `x=1,2,3` on z=3 face plus a bridge stone at `(1,1,3)=53`.
- P1 was forced into pure defensive blocking of P2's `x=0` threats: P1 burned 4 consecutive moves blocking `(0,*,3)`.
- After P1 owned all of `x=0` on z=3 face, P2 detoured to z=2: `(1,2,2)=41`, threatening `(0,2,2)`.
- P1's reply `(0,2,2)=40` was the killing move: it (a) blocked P2's z=2 x=0 access AND (b) custodian-captured `(1,2,2)` along the x-axis at y=2,z=2 (bracketed by P1's existing `(2,2,2)`) AND (c) the captured cell completed P1's chain: `(2,2,0)–(2,2,1)–(2,2,2)–(1,2,2)–(0,2,2)–(0,2,3)`, hitting both z=0 and z=3 faces. **Connection wins.**

Endgame: reached connection win (no double-pass, no max_turns timeout) in all 3 games.

### Player reflections (recorded before role switch)

**P1 strategy guide.** (a) Anchor a central z-column quickly (3 stones at (a,b,0),(a,b,1),(a,b,2) for some (a,b)). (b) If P2 blocks mid-column at z=2, capture immediately by closing at z=3 — a free piece + a face-stone. (c) If P2 blocks at the z=3 face (correct play), pivot to y-axis or x-axis brackets at z=2 or z=3 to capture P2's blockers — threshold=1 makes any isolated O bracketable in two moves. (d) Late-game blocks against P2's x-attack often double as captures, because P2 must build long lateral chains and P1 has central stones that bracket those chains.

**P2 strategy guide.** (a) Never block P1's central z-column at z=1 or z=2 — that stone is custodian-trap food. Only block at z=3 face. (b) Build x-chain on z=3 face along y=2 or y=1 — connection length is 4, two anchor stones plus two interior. (c) Be ready for forced y-bracket counter-captures using your z=3-face stones as the far-end of brackets. (d) Move actions are not useful in opening or mid-game; treat the game as place-only.

**Surprises.** Two recurring P1 motifs were not obvious in advance: (1) **column-trap** at small axis_size=4 — any P2 stone in the middle of P1's nascent column dies; (2) **block-as-capture** — P1's defensive placements at face cells often capture P2's bridge stones because the P1 stones still occupy central cube positions that bracket P2's lateral chains.

## Phase 3 — Strategic Analysis (joint)

**Distinct viable strategies?** For P1 essentially one: build z-column from the center, exploit threshold=1 custodian to either capture P2 blockers or punish a single-stone block. For P2: only the z=3 face-block defense + lateral x-chain on z=3 has any chance; everything else loses faster. Strategies are not symmetric and not numerous.

**Counter-play.** Yes for the first ~3 P2 moves (the y=z=3 face-block and counter-captures via `(2,2,3)` as bracket far-end were genuinely useful). After move ~10, P2 is reactive; every P2 push at x=0 face is a single threat that P1 blocks for free.

**Short vs long-term tension.** Mild. Every move is roughly forcing. The "deep" element is P1 timing: when to capture vs when to block P2's parallel x-build. But these decisions usually have a clear best answer once P1 sees the column-and-flank structure.

**Emergent concepts.** (i) A clear *atari* analog from custodian threshold=1 — single stones in line with two enemy axis-neighbors are doomed. (ii) **Capture-as-completion**: a single placement can simultaneously block, capture, and complete connection. This is the most interesting feature and is what game 3 turned on. (iii) **Face boundaries as safe defensive cells** — a stone on the z=3 (or z=0, x=0, x=3, y=0, y=3) face cannot be custodian-captured along the axis perpendicular to the face. P2 learned to exploit this; P1 exploited it on different axes simultaneously.

**Does 3D enable strategies 2D wouldn't?** Partial yes. The y-axis double-capture in game 2 (capturing two consecutive O stones using a (y=0, y=3) bracket) only works because the orthogonal axes are independent of the connection axis. In 2D Hex-with-custodian, you'd have only one orthogonal axis to bracket on, halving the tactical surface. But the *core* connection mechanic would survive 2D fine.

**Topology matters?** Custodian only fires on grid/torus/sierpinski/holes per `_capture_custodian` (line 512), so this game's behavior depends on it being grid. Moore would silently disable capture; torus would allow line walks but custodian still does *not* wrap (per the prompt caveat). On 4³ grid, neighbor count 6 plus axis_size 4 means columns are very short — all captures are within at most 3 hops.

**Move actions used.** **Zero in 3 games.** Move requires `adjacent_empty`, so moving costs the equivalent of a placement next to an existing stone — strictly worse than placing there. Move would only matter in a contested endgame where many cells are filled, but games never reach that phase here. **Vestigial action space.**

**First-mover advantage.** Severe. 3/3 P1 wins, including the seat-swapped game where I played P2 with my best defense. Self-play training reports a 50% win rate, which we believe reflects policy convergence on shared sub-optimal lines — neither learned policy executes the move-5 column-trap that beats unprepared P2 play.

## Phase 4 — Novelty Adversary (mandatory)

**Adversary's case.**

(a) **3D Hex / 3D Y / Qubic.** 4³ cube with two-color placement and face-to-face connection between opposite faces is essentially **3D Hex on a cubic graph** — a configuration discussed in the connection-game literature (Browne, *Connection Games*, 2005; "Y on the cube"). Adding orthogonal-axis custodian capture is just bolting Reversi/Othello onto Hex. Hex players already understand "block to extend, extend to win"; an Othello player understands custodian flips. Both transfer.

(b) **Reversi/Othello on a cube.** Custodian threshold=1 with axial walks is precisely the Othello rule lifted to 3D. Our P1's "column trap" tactic is a 3D version of the Othello "wedge" pattern.

(c) **Qubic / 3D Tic-Tac-Toe.** The 4³ + alternating placement structure is identical to Qubic. The win condition differs (line vs face-connection) but spatial reasoning patterns transfer: most of P1's good moves in our games would also be strong Qubic moves (central columns).

(d) **Move action.** Adjacent_empty placement-by-shift is the Amazons move (without arrows) or Lines-of-Action lite. We saw zero usage; an Amazons player would not call this a movement game.

**Verdict:** "It's just 3D Hex + Othello captures, with an unused Amazons-style move action." An expert in any of the components would transfer 70%+ of strategic intuition immediately.

**Rebuttal (P1 + P2).**

(i) The **capture-as-completion** mechanic — game 3's winning move `(0,2,2)=40` did three things in one ply (block P2, capture P2, complete P1's connection chain through the captured cell). This is unusual in either parent game: Hex has no captures, so the third move-effect is impossible there; Othello has no connection win, so the third move-effect is also impossible there. The combination *does* generate a behavior neither parent has alone. This is the genuinely novel Phase-2 moment.

(ii) **3D-specific bracket geometry.** Game 2's double-capture at z=3 used the y-axis (perpendicular to connection axis z) as the bracket. In 2D Hex+Othello you have only one orthogonal axis to bracket on; in 3D you have two, and they can both reach connection-relevant cells. Not just window-dressing — the choice of bracket axis is a tactical degree of freedom not present in the 2D parent.

(iii) **Move action present-but-unused does still affect the game tree** (legal-action mask, MCTS branching), but admittedly it is vestigial in human-style play — the rebuttal is weaker here.

**Novelty score:** **5/10.** The combination of 3D + connection-win + custodian + place-or-move is novel as a *pipeline output*; we have not found this exact game in published rule sets. But three of the four mechanics are well-documented, the fourth is unused, and the tactical surface is dominated by patterns that transfer from Othello and Hex. Above the "X on a hex/torus" floor (2–3) but well below "emergent dynamics with no analog" (7+).

### SCORES (1–10)

- **Strategic Depth: 4/10.** Tactics exist (column traps, double y-brackets, capture-as-completion), but the search horizon is shallow: games end in 5–17 moves, axis_size=4 means a connection is 4 stones long, threshold=1 makes any mid-line stone capturable. P2's options are nearly forced after move 5.
- **Emergent Complexity: 4/10.** The capture-as-completion triple-effect (game 3 move 9) is genuinely emergent — it's not present in either parent rule alone. Double-axis brackets in 3D are a real tactic. But the same patterns recur game to game.
- **Balance: 2/10.** 3/3 P1 wins under careful play; in self-play (per training metadata) the policies report 50% which we read as convergence on sub-optimal lines rather than true balance. Severe first-mover advantage from threshold=1 custodian + axis_size=4 + first_move_anywhere. R17's seat-balance gate (`<0.1`) clearly did not catch this — the game's seat-balance scoring evidently ran on pre-tactical greedy probes.
- **Novelty (post-adversary): 5/10.** Novel *combination* (3D + connection + custodian + move); each individual mechanic is standard. Closest known analog (3D Hex + Othello custodian) is not a published game but is what an expert would call this.
- **Replayability: 3/10.** Once both players know the column trap and the face-block defense, the opening tree narrows to ~3 lines. No known counter to P1's repertoire.
- **Overall "Would I play this again?": 3/10.** The capture-then-connect motif is fun the first time. After 3 games it is a solved puzzle.

### CLOSEST KNOWN-GAME ANALOG
**3D Hex on a cubic graph + Othello custodian capture, with a vestigial Amazons-without-arrows move action.** Not identical because (a) Hex never has captures, so the "capture-as-completion" tactic is genuinely new; (b) Othello has no connection win, so face-to-face goal is not in that parent; (c) the unified rule set has not (to our search) appeared as a published game.

### KILLER FLAWS
1. **Severe P1 advantage** — 3/3 in greedy play, including with seat-swap.
2. **Move action vestigial** — 0/3 games used it. ~26× the action space contributes nothing to play.
3. **threshold=1 custodian + axis_size=4** — single stones are too capturable; connection length is too short. Games end before depth can develop.
4. **Inert propagation rule** — `prop_type=none` but radius/strength/decay are nonzero in the rule blob; harmless but a bit-rot smell suggesting evolutionary noise.
5. **Self-play vs greedy disagreement** — final winrate 0.5 across 3 PPO seeds, but greedy human-style play is 100% P1. The training balance signal that promoted this game to champion appears to be measuring policy convergence rather than true balance.

### BEST QUALITY
**Capture-as-completion** — game 3 move 9 (`(0,2,2)=40`) was a single placement that simultaneously blocked, captured, and won. This three-fold move-effect is the tactical motif most worth calling novel: it requires a connection-win goal *and* a custodian capture rule *and* a 3D structure where blocking cells can also be on the connection path.

### 3D STRUCTURAL CONTRIBUTION
**Modest.** The 3D substrate adds *one* genuine extra tactic — orthogonal-axis double-capture using the y-axis when connecting along z (game 2). That's not present in 2D Hex+Othello. But the core game would compile to a 2D 4×4 board with a "longer connection" rule and lose ~25–30% of its tactical surface, not 70%. The R17 hypothesis ("3D + custodian + connection + mobile stones is a genuinely deep new combination") is partially supported only — 3D adds tactics but axis_size=4 truncates them; mobile stones add nothing observable.

### IMPROVEMENT IDEAS
**Single most impactful change:** Raise `axis_size` to 5 or 6. The 4³ cube is too small to give the connection task room to breathe; threshold=1 custodian is brutal at this scale. With axis_size=5, columns are length 5, custodian sandwiches require 2 stones to be created, and P2 would have meaningful counter-play tempo. Secondary: raise custodian threshold to 2 (no single-stone captures), or add `pass_to_end_with_majority` + a higher max_turns — both encourage longer positional games.
