## Phase 1 — Rule Comprehension

### Board and topology
- 2D torus, axis size 8 (64 cells). Every cell has 4 neighbors (von Neumann, all wrap).
- Crucially, cells at coordinate 0 on a dimension are adjacent (via wrap) to cells at coordinate 7 on the same dimension. On the torus, the "opposing faces" are actually the SAME neighborhood layer.

### Turn structure — SIMULTANEOUS
- Both players submit one action per tick via `step_simultaneous(a_p1, a_p2)`.
- Collision resolution: if both players target the same cell, **mutual annihilation** — nothing is placed, neither stone lands. This is the game's signature mechanic and only defensive tool against opponent placements.
- Two consecutive double-passes end as draw (R15 rule).

### Action types
- Place only (no movement). Action N-1 (= 64) is pass. Placement target = empty cells.
- **Constraint:** `adjacent_to_own` — after the first move, every subsequent placement must be adjacent (via torus neighbors) to one of your own existing pieces. First move is anywhere.

### Capture / CA dynamics
- `capture_type = none` (no custodian/flood captures).
- Cellular automaton: `steps_per_turn = 2`, max_neighbors = 4. R15 runs BOTH P1 and P2 perspectives per CA step, so each tick applies 4 total CA sweeps over the board.
- CA table (75 entries, verified symmetric under player swap per R15 claim):
  - Empty: `0,1,2 → 1` and `0,2,1 → 1`. That is, an empty cell with exactly 3 neighbors (1 friendly + 2 enemy OR 2 friendly + 1 enemy) is **born as the acting player's color**. Because both perspectives fire, this births to whichever color currently has more neighbors-of-it in the 3-neighbor mix.
  - Friendly dying: `1,0,2 → 0` (isolated friendly with 2 enemy dies); `1,1,3 → 0` (1 friendly 3 enemy dies).
  - Enemy dying: `2,2,0 → 0` (enemy with 2 friendly 0 enemy dies to EMPTY, not captured to friendly); `2,3,1 → 0`.
  - All other 69 entries are identity (freeze).
- Net effect: CA is a mild conversion tool. Most positions are CA-stable. The only interesting CA pressure is the "3-neighbor birth" which slightly inflates high-contact fronts, and the "surround-and-evaporate" death rules that require being deep inside enemy territory.

### Propagation / influence
- `prop_type = none`. Board values stay zero everywhere. Threshold metric never triggers (connection-win game, not threshold).

### Win condition — CONNECTION
- P1 must connect opposite faces along **dim = 1 (y-axis)**, i.e. own a connected chain touching y=0 AND y=7.
- P2 connects along **dim = 0 (x-axis)**, x=0 AND x=7.
- This is Hex-like … except on a torus.

### Critical degeneracy flag: torus wrap makes connection trivial

On a torus, **the cells at coord 0 are directly adjacent to cells at coord 7 via wrap**. Any two same-color pieces placed at (x, 0) and (x, 7) form a 2-cell "connected set" that touches both faces — because they are neighbors.

I verified this mechanically. `engine.topo.connects_faces({0, 56}, dim=1)` returns `True` — two P1 pieces at (0,0) and (0,7) constitute a winning connection. So **the connection-win threshold is reachable in TWO placements** if a player can place at both endpoints.

Because `first_move_anywhere = True`, round 1 can land anywhere. In round 2, the `adjacent_to_own` constraint includes wrap-adjacency, so a piece at (0,0) can be followed by (0,7). Unless the opponent collides or pre-occupies, P1 wins on tick 2. I verified this in play (Phase 2 Game 1): P1 plays cell 0, cell 56 → wins in 2 ticks.

This is the dominant mechanic of the game; every play decomposes into a 2-move wrap-race with collision-defense. The CA barely touches it because the game ends before CA pressure matters.

### Secondary flag: tie-break iteration bias on connection
`_check_connection` iterates `for player in (1, 2)` and returns on first connected player. If both players complete a connection on the same simultaneous tick, **P1 wins the tie** by iteration order. I observed this in Phase 2 Game 1 (both players had legal wrap-wins on tick 2; P1 credited).

### Summary of degeneracy
- 2-move forced-win exists for both sides from an empty board, gated only by opponent's predictive collision defense.
- Neither capture nor CA nor propagation meaningfully shapes play in the expected game length (typically < 5 ticks).
- This is **exactly** the torus-connection pitfall warned about in the evaluation prompt ("custodian capture walks do NOT wrap…" — the mirror caveat is that **connection walks DO wrap**, which completely breaks Hex-style connection goals on torus).

## Phase 3 — Strategic Analysis (joint)

### Distinct strategies?
No. There is essentially one strategy: **race the torus wrap**, with collision-defense as the only counter-tool. There are two flavors (direct wrap vs deny-and-wrap) but they are tactical micro-choices, not distinct macro-strategies.

### Meaningful counter-play?
Minimal. If the opponent is 1 tempo ahead of you, you can only collide-block. You have limited legal moves (adjacent_to_own after round 1) so you can't even always afford to collide — sometimes the collision cell isn't adjacent to your group.

### Short-term vs long-term tension?
Essentially none — games end in 2-5 ticks before any long-term structure can form. The CA never cycles into interesting patterns because the game terminates first.

### Emergent concepts?
- **Tempo race:** yes — simultaneous placement turns this into a 1-tempo race where both players are always 2 placements from victory.
- **Mutual annihilation:** yes — collision is the ONLY defensive resource.
- **Territory / influence:** no — win is binary (connection reached or not), no scoring, no propagation.
- **Tempo via adjacency:** the `adjacent_to_own` constraint creates a mild ko-like planning concern: after round 1, you can only play cells touching your piece, so you can't always block an arbitrary opponent wrap-move.

### Topology matters?
The torus is the WHOLE game. Replace with grid and this becomes harder, longer, and less degenerate. The entire strategic content is "how many rounds to the wrap."

### First-mover advantage?
In theory simultaneous eliminates seat bias. In practice:
- The **connection-check iteration order is P1-favored** on ties. I triggered this in Game 1: both players completed connections on round 2, P1 got credit.
- Our 3 games: P1 won 2, P2 won 1 (seat-swapped game still went to P1 in game 3 after P2 played suboptimally).
- I suspect perfect play gives P1 a slim edge because of the tie-break asymmetry. A seat-balance heuristic would likely flag this.

## Phase 4 — Novelty Adversary

**Adversary opens.**

(a) Direct catalog comparison:
- **Hex.** This is literally Hex's win condition ("connect your two opposite sides") applied to a square grid — except on a torus the "opposite sides" are the SAME cells via wrap, which trivializes it. Strip the wrap and call this "Hex on a square grid with 4-neighbor connectivity" — a well-known degenerate Hex variant. The wrap *breaks* the Hex idea, it doesn't extend it.
- **Gomoku/Connect6:** same "line-build" impulse but Gomoku has a fixed length win, this has a face-touch win. Simpler than Gomoku.
- **Go:** no capture, no territory scoring, no ko. The `adjacent_to_own` constraint is a *reverse* of Go's "liberty" concept — in Go you cannot self-suicide; here you cannot play isolated. This is a novel Go-adjacent constraint but it's also known as the "contiguous placement" rule in many proof-games and puzzle families.
- **Tumbleweed / Y:** connection-based abstracts. Tumbleweed uses sight-line placement; Y is triangular. Again, the wrap degeneracy makes the torus version strictly inferior to planar connection games.
- **Life-like CA (Conway, Day&Night, HighLife):** this CA has 6 non-trivial rules, so it is definitely a "sparse perturbation of identity". It is NOT Conway's B3/S23 (would need `0,3,0→1`, `1,2,x→1`, `1,3,x→1`). It is NOT HighLife. The birth rules `0,1,2→1` and `0,2,1→1` are not standard Life-variants; they depend on the 2-color split. This is a bespoke table but it barely matters because CA doesn't fire meaningfully.

(b) CA-based game lineage: the place+CA loop resembles Immigration Game (2-color Life). But here the CA is nearly inert (6/75 non-identity) and games end before CA can express anything. So the comparison is "Immigration Game but CA doesn't fire" — not novel, just broken.

(c) Simultaneous comparison:
- **Diplomacy's order resolution:** no — no orders, no support, no standoffs except the collision-annihilation mechanic.
- **Gungo** (simultaneous Go): the collision-annihilation IS a Gungo-like mechanic. This game basically = Gungo-lite (no capture) + connection goal + torus.
- **Blotto:** each tick, both players pick one cell from ~4 legal cells. Very low-dimensional Blotto.

(d) Re-skin argument: **this game IS "Hex on a torus with simultaneous play, first piece anywhere, subsequent adjacent-only"**. The torus breaks Hex by making the goal trivial. The simultaneous adds a tempo-race flavor that has no clean analog but isn't enough to save it.

(e) Expert advantage? A Hex expert would NOT dominate because Hex strategies (bridges, ladders, edge templates) are all irrelevant — you never need 3+ pieces. A Gungo expert would understand the simultaneous mutual-annihilation intuitively. Overall: neither expert dominates, but only because the game is too shallow to reward expertise.

**Players' rebuttal.**

Specific moments where analogies fail:
1. Game 2 round 1: P2 plays (0,7) to simultaneously *deny P1's win setup* AND *set up their own*. This dual-purpose move has no clean analog in Hex (no "partners") or Gungo (no "pre-built win condition at distance"). It's specific to the wrap+connection+simultaneous combination.
2. Game 3 round 5: P1's y-wrap close was only available because P1 developed a contiguous chain (adjacent_to_own constraint). This restricted-development dynamic is Go-adjacent but has no Hex/Gomoku analog. A Go expert's intuition for "connected groups with liberties" does partially transfer — but the lack of captures means Go's tactical library is mostly irrelevant.
3. The collision mechanic — identical-cell targeting → annihilation — is genuinely close to Gungo. But Gungo has captures. Here, collision is the ONLY interaction between players.

**Novelty score (1-10): 3.**

Justification:
- The closest analog is "Hex on a torus with simultaneous placement and a weak background CA." The torus breaks Hex; the simultaneous mechanic is genuinely distinctive but barely gets used because games terminate in 2-5 ticks.
- It is NOT zero-novelty because the simultaneous + connection + torus combination has a tempo-race character that no single known game has. But the degeneracy makes the novel content un-exercisable.
- If the torus wrap didn't break connection (e.g. if connection required non-wrap path, or grid instead of torus), the game might land at 5-6. As implemented, the novelty is latent but unreachable.

## Phase 5 — Verdict

**Team ID:** team-19
**Game ID:** 2e5262b76311
**Rules Summary:** 8×8 torus simultaneous-placement game; first-piece-anywhere then adjacent-only; minimal CA; Hex-style connection win. Torus wrap makes connection reachable in 2 moves.
**Topology:** 2D torus, axis 8, 64 cells, 4 neighbors each (von Neumann, all wrap).
**Turn Structure:** simultaneous (with collision=mutual-annihilation).

### SCORES (1-10)

- **Strategic Depth: 2** — The wrap degeneracy forces every game into a 2-tempo race. There is no deep calculation, no multi-piece tactical library, no long-horizon planning. The only micro-skill is "predict opponent's 1-move wrap target and collide."

- **Emergent Complexity: 2** — CA fires almost never before games end. No propagation, no captures. Mutual-annihilation is the one interesting emergent-ish feature, but it has very low action space (typically 4-8 legal cells per player). No ko fights, no territory, no influence field.

- **Balance: 4** — Simultaneous mechanic in principle eliminates first-mover advantage, but the connection tie-break iterates P1→P2 so symmetric simultaneous wins go to P1. Across our 3 games P1 won 2/3 (including the seat-swap). R15's new seat-balance heuristic would likely flag this. Not catastrophic but not balanced either.

- **Novelty (post-adversary): 3** — Strongest adversary argument: "Hex on a torus" (the wrap destroys Hex's strategic content). Strongest rebuttal: simultaneous + adjacent-only + collision-annihilation + connection is a genuinely unseen combination, but the combination's creative surface is unreachable because of the 2-tick termination.

- **Replayability: 2** — Games converge in 2-5 ticks and both players' optimal play is roughly determined after the first move. Very low replay value.

- **Overall "Would I play this again?": 2**

### CLOSEST KNOWN-GAME ANALOG
**Hex on a torus with simultaneous placement.** Not identical because (i) simultaneous mutual-annihilation has no Hex equivalent, (ii) `adjacent_to_own` constraint doesn't exist in Hex (Hex allows any empty placement), (iii) the CA background is bespoke. But the win condition and topology intent are directly from Hex — and the torus adaptation is strictly degenerate for that intent.

### KILLER FLAWS
1. **Torus wrap trivializes connection.** Win reachable in 2 placements. Empirically verified: `engine.topo.connects_faces({0, 56}, dim=1) == True`. This is the dominant flaw and likely the reason this game (despite being in a reasonable generation) didn't reach the R15 top-20. Go Essence 0.145 reflects this.
2. **CA is effectively inert** in the playable horizon — 6/75 non-trivial rules and games end in < 5 ticks, so CA pressure never builds.
3. **Connection-check iteration bias (`engine_v2.py:744`):** on simultaneous ties P1 wins. Minor but non-zero balance cost.
4. No propagation despite rule shape suggesting one (prop_type=none but threshold field exists) — orthogonal to connection win but increases the "inert subsystems" count.

### BEST QUALITY
The **dual-purpose defensive move** (Game 2 round 1): P2 plays at (0,7) to simultaneously deny P1's wrap win AND set up P2's own x-wrap. That move has genuine elegance — it's not a mirror, not a mimic, not a collision. It uses knowledge of how the torus wraps differently for each player's assigned dimension. This is the one moment where the game's novel structure shines.

### IMPROVEMENT IDEAS
**Change topology from torus to cylinder** (wrap only along one dimension, not both). Or more aggressively: **require a non-wrap path for the connection win** (ban wrap edges in the connection check). Either change kills the 2-move degeneracy and forces players to actually build chains of length 8, at which point:
- CA would finally matter (chains can be attacked by `2,2,0→0` or `1,0,2→0`).
- `adjacent_to_own` becomes a real strategic constraint (you have to route your chain through contested cells).
- Simultaneous collision-annihilation becomes tactically rich (both players fight over the same growth cell).
- Games would extend to 10-20 ticks and produce actual emergent structure.
- Novelty score would likely jump to 5-6, replayability to 4-5.

The simplest fix — one-line change in `connects_faces` to exclude wrap-adjacency when checking start/end cells — would preserve torus influence propagation semantics while restoring connection-game depth.
