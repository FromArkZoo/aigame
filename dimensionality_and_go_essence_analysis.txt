On Higher Dimensions (7D+): Almost certainly the wrong direction
================================================================

The math kills it. Your topology uses axis_size^num_dimensions with a 64-cell cap:

Dims | axis_size | Cells | Neighbors/cell | Min crossing distance
-----|-----------|-------|----------------|----------------------
2D   | 8         | 64    | 4              | 8
3D   | 4         | 64    | 6              | 4
4D   | 2 (banned)| 16    | 8              | 2
5D   | 2 (banned)| 32    | 10             | 2
7D+  | impossible| -     | 14+            | -

Three compounding problems in higher dimensions:

1. Neighbor explosion: Von Neumann adjacency gives 2n neighbors per interior
   cell. At 7D that's 14 neighbors -- surround capture requires filling all
   14, which is basically impossible. This is why 3D capture already doesn't
   work well (6 neighbors) and was confirmed across 3 runs of evaluation.

2. Shrinking crossing distance: To fit in 64 cells, axis_size shrinks. At 4D+
   you get axis_size=2-3, meaning connection wins need only 2-3 stones. The
   evaluators flagged this repeatedly -- 4-step paths in 3D 4x4x4 were already
   too short.

3. RL agent can't learn it: Your MLP policy sees a flat observation vector. It
   has no inductive bias for spatial structure at all, let alone 7D spatial
   structure. The agent would need dramatically more training budget to learn
   meaningful play on high-dimensional spaces.

Even raising max_total_cells to 256 only gets you to 4D 4x4x4x4 (256 cells,
8 neighbors) or 3D 6x6x6 (216 cells). The neighbor problem doesn't go away.


The Real Problem: You're Searching a Narrow Mechanic Space
==========================================================

Re-reading the spec, the vision is clear:

  "Games should NOT be defined in human terms (boards, pieces, turns).
   Instead, define them as abstract state machines."

  "Games should be alien. Resist the temptation to make games
   human-interpretable."

But V2 narrowed the design space to exactly five structured components:
placement + capture + propagation + win condition + turn structure. These are
all board-game mechanics. The highest-scoring composition of board-game
mechanics is... a board game that looks like Go/Hex. That's not a bug in the
scoring -- it's a consequence of the search space. You can't discover something
alien when the grammar only produces board games.

The Go Essence formula itself is actually well-aligned with the spec's vision:

  GoEssence = depth * diversity / complexity

This correctly measures "maximum emergent depth from minimum rule complexity."
The issue is that the mechanic vocabulary feeding into it is too narrow, so
evolution converges on the known optimum in that space (Go x Hex).


What Would Actually Work
========================

Instead of pushing to higher dimensions, I'd suggest broadening the mechanic
space while keeping the Go Essence metric:

1. Richer topologies (not just hypercubes)
-------------------------------------------
- Toroidal wrapping (eliminating edges/corners -- changes liberty structure
  fundamentally)
- Hexagonal grids (6-connectivity in 2D, already shown to produce better
  connection games)
- Irregular/random graphs (truly alien spatial structure)
- Layered/hierarchical topologies (cells within cells)

These add spatial richness without the neighbor-explosion of higher dimensions.

2. New mechanic types beyond placement games
---------------------------------------------
The spec envisions "transition functions that modify state" -- much broader
than placing stones:
- Movement (pieces relocate, not just appear)
- Transformation (pieces change type/strength)
- Economy (resources accumulate, get spent)
- Asymmetric roles (players have different action sets)
- State evolution (board changes between turns independent of player actions)

These break out of the Go/Hex attractor basin entirely.

3. More abstract state representations
----------------------------------------
Return to the spec's original V1 vision of "state as a tensor" with
"compositions of primitive operations." Not every game needs to be a grid
where you place stones. The state could be a graph, a matrix, a set of
counters -- anything the RL agent can observe and act on.

4. Novelty pressure in the fitness function
--------------------------------------------
Add an explicit novelty metric that penalizes games structurally similar to
known optima. If a game has surround capture + connection win on 2D 8x8, it
should get a novelty penalty to force exploration of other regions.


Bottom Line
===========

The Go Essence metric is doing its job -- it's correctly identifying that
depth-from-simplicity is the goal. The problem is that the V2 mechanic
vocabulary is a small island in the space of possible games, and Go/Hex sit
at its peak. Higher dimensions explore less of that island, not more. To find
something genuinely new, you need to expand the island itself -- richer
topologies, new mechanic types, and more abstract state representations.

The spec's aspiration -- "something that emerges from AI's own experience of
computation, that other AI systems find endlessly rich" -- requires a search
space where such alien games can exist. Right now, the search space only
contains board games.
